6. Magic is Unifying

Magic’s just belief that bites back. And like belief, it’s never neutral - it’s loaded, cultural, political, and pissed off. Magic allowed Mages to turn gods into mana batteries, worshipping into spell prep.

Let me guess - you thought magic had no borders. That it was some sort of universal force, accessible to all, pure and egalitarian, like sunlight or gravity or Spotify Premium. Cute. The reality? The world’s been chopped into 77 magical nation-states, each one with its own spells, gods, taxes, and geopolitical tantrums. Magic isn’t free - it’s franchised. And trust me, the global magical order runs about as smoothly as a three-legged centaur on cobblestones. The most powerful states - Vaticanus, Avalon, Nihon - hoard relics like dragons and rewrite the rules of reality from gilded parliamentary sanctums while the rest of us sweat through spellwork for minimum enchantment. Magical privilege is real. Healers like me? We’re the magical working class. We pour our lifeblood into mending wounds while some state-sponsored Illuminatus in a runic robe gets tenure for levitating spoons.

I live in the North Atlantic Magic Federation, or NAMF - and no, the acronym doesn’t get any cooler with time. Imagine if Silicon Valley went to Burning Man, read three books on Indigenous spirituality, then tried to run a government. That’s NAMF. A hyper-technocratic patchwork of mana grids, bureaucratic mysticism, and chakra-branding startups with names like "SoulSync." They gentrified magic. Here, shamanic rituals get repackaged as "neural field recalibration experiences," and ancestral spells are copyrighted by hedge-funded AlchemTech firms. It’s appropriation with an expense report. And the worst part? They think they fixed magic - standardized it, regulated it, made it safe for public use. But they didn’t. They just made it palatable to the colonizers. Every nation practices its own flavor of the arcane - from Celtic Druidry to Zoroastrian Firecraft - but NAMF acts like it invented the wheel because it gave it Bluetooth. Meanwhile, they still ignore the fact that the borders of these magical states were drawn with blood, theft, and bureaucratic quills by the same empires that once stole gods and named them "myths." Magic isn’t a gift. It’s a history. And in this world, history’s got landlords.

The corridor leading to the tribunal looks like it was carved by someone who hated angles. Every surface sweeps in soft cathedral arches—no corners for paranoia to roost, no shadows deep enough to hide contrition. Runic sconces glow in sterile amber, their light filtered through suppression glyphs that taste of copper on the tongue. Two Regulators flank me, batons cradled in practiced indifference. My cuffs have been replaced by a braided chain-hex—pretty, platinum, horrendously expensive. It hums against my wrist with the smug tenor of old money.

Note to self: Invoice the Council for accessorizing. Labor isn’t cheap.

Ahead, the doors loom. Twin titans of rune-laced lunastone, inlaid with the Academy’s crest—Serpens Ouroboros devouring its tail around a stylized caduceus. The message is simple: everything feeds the institution eventually, even itself. As I cross the threshold a chill sluices down my spine, equal parts climate control and psychological conditioning. I step onto obsidian that reflects me in broken prisms—fractured Bodhi multiplied, each shard a different mistake. The room smells of ozone and the gut-hollow lightness of huge, empty spaces designed to make you feel small.

The chamber proper is a vertical coliseum flipped inside‑out. Instead of spectators, tiered balconies hold mosaic panels depicting legendary miracles: Saint Torin closing plague‑sores with a hymn; High‑Cherurg Tenebris suturing reality to stop a dimensional bleed. All executed in luminous tesserae that shift color when you blink. I count twelve elders stationed around a horseshoe dais, each perched behind a half‑moon pulpit of orichalcum lattice. Their robes are winter‑storm gray, a color patented by the Academy to denote unassailable authority™.

Suspension wards shimmer overhead—concentric discs of frozen starlight rotating at contemplative speed. They leach magic from the air in lazy siphons, the way bored aristocrats drain champagne flutes. My tattoos prickle under the drag, entropy wards sighing like exhausted lungs.

A clerk in waist-coat and levitating quill reads charges that dance holographically between us: UNLICENSED MIRACLE-WORKING. RECKLESS THAUMATURGY. DAMAGE TO MUNICIPAL WARDS. Then the sub-headers scroll, the fine print that actually ruins you: Immediate Licensure Revocation. Retroactive Debt Bondage for Academy Tuition. Permanent Blacklisting from NAMF Territories. The words crackle, then evaporate into faint motes that settle on my shoulders like ash.

"Defendant, state your full name for the record."
"State it? Note the record says 'Defendant,' so let's save time. Bodhisunya Rao. Friends call me Bodhi. You can call me 'The Reason Your Grid Didn't Melt'."

A ripple of disapproval races the balconies. Good—wouldn’t want anyone getting comfortable. Looking up at the "Council of Porcelain Masks", I scan the horseshoe dais for the Primary Speakers.

Center stage is Arch-Elder Virelia Mournwright, skin like bleached parchment stretched over old fury, nose pinched by pince-nez that double as a rune scanner. To her right sits Elder Magistrate Cleon Astell, financier turned ethicist, his robe pin adorned with the sigil of the Chamber of Commerce because even hypocrisy needs branding. And on the far left... Elder-Healer Saanvi Belgaran. The only genuine clinician present. Her eyes are rimmed crimson—sleepless nights or suppressed empathy, hard to tell.

The rest form an equal‑opportunity mural of disdain—administrators, philanthropist backers, two politicians who arrived late and smell faintly of after‑banquet mead.

Seeing Elder Saanvi sitting amongst the Judiciaries causes my throat to tighten. The guilt hits me then—not a feeling, but a physical weight, like swallowing a stone. Saanvi was not only a personal mentor of mine at the Academy, but my mother's best friend. Disappointing her wasn't on the agenda.

Virelia leans forward, hands steepled.

"Mr. Rao, the Council appreciates your… enthusiasm for patient safety. However, your methodology raises grave concerns. You performed a Class IV Sub-Resurrection Causal Inversion within an unsanctioned Null Field. Alone."
"Correct. I also didn't wait for a committee to vote on gravity while the kid fell. Minor miracle that I managed to avoid spilling coffee on my white coat. If we're listing irrelevant facts."
Cleon’s jaw flexes. "Flippancy will not serve you here."
"Yeah, well neither will appeasement."

Saanvi interjects, voice brittle but weighted with something heavier than annoyance.

"The Entropy indices recorded a seventeen‑point escalation. You claim to have controlled a Class 4 Paradox with willpower, Bodhi. That is not science; it is delusion. The Ward Lattice was already destabilized by the Lunar transit. Your 'miracle' nearly triggered a cascade failure across the entire eastern seaboard. You didn't just risk yourself. You risked everyone."
I nod, the humor draining out of my posture. "I know the math, Saanvi. But the math doesn't scream when it bleeds out."

A few students in the upper mezzanine—allowed to observe for pedagogical value—stifle laughs before tutors glare them silent.

Virelia raps a gavel of moon‑silver. The sound is soft yet final, like a guillotine cushioned with velvet.

"Explain your spellwork. In detail."

I unspool it. How Standard Regeneration forces cellular genesis forward—too slow for a pulmo‑hemorrhagic collapse. How Time Reversal bends causality but drags surrounding reality like a toddler’s blanket, risking wide‑area paradox. How both require umpteen practitioners chanting in key while the patient’s life bleeds out a metronome. My method? Entropy drag.

"You treat injuries as open thermodynamic systems," I say, pacing within my hex‑radius. "Force the wound state to trade entropy with the Null Field. Body moves toward lower‑entropy configuration—aka healthy—while the field uptakes the disorder. The trick is establishing a fuse."
Cleon sneers. "Spare us the poetry, Mr. Rao. The actuarial tables don't care about your 'tricks'. The Null Field collapsed twenty‑three seconds post‑procedure."
"Because I was the fuse, Cleon. If the field collapsed, it would have imploded me, not the ward. I did the math. I just didn't factor in your comfort levels. Zero civilian casualties, unless you count my career. But that was terminal anyway."
Astell turns plum. "Irrelevant. You circumvented every safety protocol to flaunt your—"
"—to save a kid abandoned by protocol," I cut in. My voice echoes, riding the chamber’s perfect acoustics. "Had I waited for your four‑thaumiphysician choir, she’d be a chalk outline."

Murmurs bubble. Virelia raises her hand and the room stills.

"Intent does not absolve recklessness. Doctrine exists to harness power for the common good."
I laugh, short and crooked. "Doctrine exists to gatekeep the divine. If good were common we wouldn’t price it by the hour."

A sub‑clerk hovers a scroll‑slab toward me. Case histories flicker: Alcester v. The Collective, Van‑Luik Reversal Incident, each a cautionary tale of lone mages who cooked themselves alive attempting entropy inversion. My favorite bedtime stories.

Virelia taps the scroll. "These tragedies prove why quorum is mandatory."

"They prove why the quorum of cowards is lethal," I reply. "Every one of those med‑teams refused to act until they formed a committee. People died waiting for a vote that never closed."

Saanvi’s eyes flutter shut. She’s seen the same corpses. Cleon arches a brow, scenting weakness in his colleague.

"Elder Belgaran, perhaps you’d like to remind the defendant of the stable miracles ratio?"
She exhales. "Three practitioners minimize entropy feedback by distributing metabolic cost across synced circulatory matrices—"
"—Unless one practitioner is me," I interrupt. "My vitals remain within tolerance post‑spell." I hold out my hands, palms up. "No necrotic bloom. No fissured aura. Check the telemetry."

A holographic panel materializes mid‑air, courtesy of the Regulator medic who processed me. My biosign curves glide across in neat sine waves. Heart rate elevated but steady; entropy saturation at 62%—ugly but survivable.

Saanvi frowns at the data, torn between disbelief and grudging respect. Cleon overrides.

"Even if you withstood it, you jeopardized the ward lattice. We had to divert power from neonatal stasis rooms to reinforce the grid. Three infants nearly de-synced because you wanted to play god with a gunshot wound."

I bite back a retort about billable priorities. Instead I pivot.

"The patient lives."

The statement hangs like incense. No one responds. Truth rarely needs allies; it just needs air.

A hush falls so complete I swear I hear dust motes pirouette. In that pause my gaze drifts upward, past suppression discs, to stained‑glass oculi depicting The First Miracle: a robed figure splitting darkness with light. I wonder what tribunal he faced afterward.

Heat stings behind my eyes—unexpected, unwelcome. I channel it into words.

"My mother died in a ward ten meters from here," I say softly. "Tumor the size of regret in her frontal lobe. Academy surgeons called it economically non‑viable to attempt Class III excision. You signed that letter, Arch‑Elder."

Virelia blinks once, the only crack in her alabaster mask.

"You conflate policy with—"
"I conflate policy with hypocrisy." The admission tastes like rust. "So when a girl rolled into that alley with her lungs sautéed, I saw my mother’s ghost telling me to break the rules that broke her."

No one meets my eyes. Good. Let guilt crowd their retinas.

Elder Technocrat Imir Solvan, silent until now, adjusts his neural halo—thin ring of light mapping mind to archive.

"Mr. Rao, your incantation contained an anomalous glyphic sub-routine: ThRun-kel Tuu Vaar'lenna. Translation lost to public lexicons. Where did you obtain it?"

Ah, there it is—the real hunt.

"My grandmother’s prayer book," I lie smoothly. "A bedtime benediction for unruly weather. Old family superstition."

The lie settles heavy in my gut. I’m not just hiding a technique; I’m hiding Her. If they knew Saint Nalini was dabbling in Atavistic Entropy—messing with forces that could unzip the city—her statue in the atrium would be rubble by morning.

Imir’s halo stutters. He knows I’m bluffing. The ring of light abruptly contracts, tightening around his temples like a vice, casting a targeting reticle over his left eye. He leans forward, the light burning hot enough to smell like ozone. He knows. But proving it would require a deep-dive scan that overrides tribunal protocol. He slowly relaxes the halo, accepting the convenient fiction, but the threat remains scorched into the air. Better a reckless student than a corrupted legend.

Virelia signals the dais. A translucent veil descends, muting their voices while they deliberate. From my position I watch their silhouettes mime outrage, concern, self‑preservation—an interpretive dance of bureaucracy.

It hits me then, watching them argue not about if I should be expelled, but how. They aren't expelling me because I failed. They're expelling me because I worked. Because I used "impossible" magic to fix a problem their perfect System declared unsolvable. If I can rewrite entropy without their permission, what else can I do? I am a variable they cannot isolate, an irregularity in their perfect equation.

Above, suppression discs slow, as if the room itself holds its breath.

Minutes stretch origami-long. Students whisper; quills scratch; somewhere a regulator pops gum. I roll my shoulders. The hex-chain loosens a millimeter—enough to remind me entropy favors change. I could snap it, maybe. Melt through the suppression grid, sprint for the corridor. But that would undermine the point: the guilty should not need to run.

The veil rises.

Virelia stands, gavel poised but not yet struck.

"The Council finds the defendant—"

A hush funnels every heartbeat into my ears. I see Saanvi’s lips tremble, Cleon’s nostrils flare, Imir’s halo spin.

"—in violation of Articles 7, 12, and 19. Sentencing to be pronounced following procedural ratification by the Internal Safety Review Board at oh‑six‑hundred."

So they kick the can to sunrise. Bureaucracy’s favorite alarm clock.
Cleon adds, "In the interim, you remain in custody under probationary suspension."

Regulators move to escort me. I smirk.

"Guess I’m trending."

Before they guide me out, Saanvi catches my eye. She looks at the empty seat beside me—where my mother would have sat—then back to me. It's not confusion in her face; it's the grief of watching potential incinerate itself. She mouths a single word: why?
I tap my chest twice—heartbeat, hope—and let the question hang unanswered. She thinks I threw it all away. She doesn't understand that I'm the only one keeping the inheritance safe.

"I'll also accept 'Your Majesty' or 'Papa' if the first one feels too formal," I mutter under my breath, but the joke is ash in my mouth. It lands with a dull thud, protecting nothing.

As the doors seal behind me, the last thing I see is Virelia polishing her gavel like a trophy hunter buffing ivory.

The corridor greets me again, colder now. I breathe in recycled air heavy with cleansing aether and taste tomorrow’s judgment. Somewhere, the healed girl inhales too—lungs unscarred because I dared entropy to a duel. No council verdict can rewrite that equation. I saved her.

The thought crystallizes, sharp as the sigil drying on my palm. My resolve anneals in the furnace of official disdain. If the system thinks suspension will temper me, it misunderstands metallurgy: some alloys harden under fire.

I let the Regulators lead me toward holding—but my shadow stretches ahead, carving a path toward whatever rebellion waits at dawn.

7. Mages are a Monolith

People love to act like all mages are created equal - just a bunch of robe-wearing wand-wavers doing cool hand signs and quoting Latin. But the truth? The magical world is as stratified, smug, and stupidly hierarchical as everything else. Doesn’t matter how many spells you know or how many times you’ve saved someone’s life - your title says more about your bank account than your talent. Everyone wants to be a Sorcerer. No one wants to be a Cleric. Then there are five types of magic so far above our paygrade, they get capital letters: Creation, Spacetime, Truth, all that divine jazz. No one has seen those for the better part of 2 millennia. We call them the Five Divinities, because calling them "completely inaccessible plot devices" didn’t test well with the clergy. Supposedly, they were once wielded by god-tier magi who could rewrite existence, undo causality, and speak lies so true they became fact. These days? They’re more myth than method - classified, forbidden, or just plain extinct. But hey, if you ever meet someone who can conjure uranium or rewind time, run. Or bow. Maybe both.

So let’s break it down:

The 5 Types of Magi (And Why They All Kinda Suck)
  • *Sorcerers: Born with it. Magical bloodlines. Intuitive and arrogant.*
  • *Wizards: Learned it. Walk encyclopedias. Paranoid control freaks.*
  • *Warlocks: Borrowed it. Made a deal. Definitely overcompensating.*
  • *Druids: Channel it. Tree huggers with superiority complexes.*
  • *Clerics: Heal it. Soul surgeons who think band-aids are for cowards.*

I’m a Cleric. Which is a fancy way of saying I went to med school for magic and came out with a PhD in patching up people dumb enough to set themselves on fire.

My mom’s a Sorceress. Healing runs in our family - not power, just responsibility. You think ER docs have it hard? Try doing trauma surgery with your soul. Magic doesn’t just fix the body. It forces it back into shape. And every time we do it, a little piece of us goes missing.

You'd think that would earn me some respect. Maybe money. Some pity at least. Nah.

I sit at the bottom of the magical caste system, somewhere between "unpaid intern" and "miracle janitor." Nobody wants to talk about the politics of magic, but it’s everywhere. Thaumaturgy is treated like a hard science because it sounds like math. Theurgy gets funding because it invokes gods. Spellcasting? That’s the "Just Add Fireball" crowd. Meanwhile, my field - Healing Magic - is seen as passive, feminine, imprecise. Never mind the fact that I’m rewiring souls and stitching minds together with my bare aura. That doesn’t make headlines. It just makes survivors. But sure, let's keep pretending that magical academia isn’t shaped by the same classist, racist, colonial rot as everything else.

The head elder’s gavel isn’t made of wood. It’s wrenched from something older—petrified dragon‑bone inlaid with antimony runes that glint like collapsed stars. When it descends, the crack isn’t sound; it’s pressure, a micro‑quake shivering air, marrow, memory. Wards stitched into the vaulted ceiling flare, swallowing the vibration before it can bruise the gold‑lined frescoes. Justice must never dent the décor.

"By unanimous decree," intones Elder Virelia, voice smoothed by centuries of acceptable outrage, "Bodhisunya Rao is hereby placed on indefinite suspension pending further review. Effective immediately. All associated privileges, peripherals, and accreditations are rescinded. Assets held by the Academy Bank are frozen to offset ward damages."

Indefinite. The syllables hit like wet cement—slow, heavy, irreversible once set. I catalogue the council faces out of spite: Virelia’s alabaster serenity; Cleon's twitching left eye (expensive nerve tonic, ironically); Saanvi's fingers fussing with an invisible ledger only she can see, trying her hardest not to meet my eyeline. Not one of them looks relieved. No, relief would imply risk, empathy, shared consequence. What I register gleaming behind their polite masks is simpler: administrative closure.

Somewhere beyond the dais, the spectral scribe‑orb hovers, whirring as it etches the ruling into transmutable crystal. A new entry for the Academy archives, filed between Dormitory Fire, 241 AE and Alchemical Plague Inquiry, 243 AE. My name will glow there in neat rune‑font, a cautionary subheading: Violation: Unauthorized Miracle‑work, Null‑Field Causality Reversal.

"Do you understand the council’s verdict?" Cleon asks, ritual words echoing off pillars the width of ancestral guilt.

My tongue could choose tact—or it could pick the lock on their composure. I flex broken knuckles and decide I’m done with tact.

"Crystal," I answer, smiling just wide enough to weaponize a molar‑chip. "Though given the Academy’s fondness for crystal, maybe you should etch it on my forehead. Easier for the next patrol to scan."

Gasps ripple from the mezzanine where junior archivists cluster like flightless ravens. One drops a rune‑slate; it clatters, the noise swallowed by an acoustic damping field quicker than shame. Cleon’s eyelids shutter half‑mast—a palace guard ignoring a jester’s stool joke—and he pivots to the procedural finale.

"Place your left hand upon the Suppression Sigil."

A podium rises from the marble dais, silent as guilt, revealing a recessed plate of cold electrum inscribed with a living ward. Filaments writhe across its surface, searching for a pulse to devour. I plant my hand without ceremony. The sigil flares, scans, condemns.

A terse chime. Then the systematic unthreading begins.

My coat—a battered grey long‑line, regulation cut but riddled with illicit stitch‑sigils—reacts first. The chest pocket, once proud with a triad of badges, spasms like flesh meeting acid.

click‑tchhh— The Clerical Adept Crest pops loose, detaches, and hovers a heartbeat before drifting toward the podium’s receiving halo. The Medical Discipline Glyph follows, its serpent‑and‑chalice icon shedding cobalt sparks as authorization sigils erase themselves mid‑air. Last is the Field Supplicant Seal, earned during a charity plague ward two winters ago; it trembles, reluctant, then rips itself free, tearing a neat square of fabric as it goes.

Three emblems spin in lazy orbit above the Suppression Sigil, cooling slag to my smolder. They dissolve into light—the Academy reclaiming invested thaumic capital as efficiently as a loan shark cracking kneecaps for interest.

The theft leaves phantom weight on my chest, like bandages torn from a half-healed burn. It hurts—a sharp, tearing deeper than skin. I’m lighter, yes—but raw, nerves open to the chill exhaled by marble saints perched around the rotunda. I inhale that cold, let it plate the ache in iron, and meet Cleon’s gaze.

"Satisfied? Or do you need my spine too?"

No reply. But I see a twitch—a fractional tightening near his brow as the chamber wards record my insolence. Let them. Every syllable is another splinter under their lacquer‑polished ethics.

The session adjourns. Elders rise in synchronized exhaustion, a murmuration of ivory robes rustling like dry parchment. They descend side aisles to private lifts, refusing to pass me directly—as if suspension is contagious demon‑pox.

Court recorders deactivate scry‑orbs and collapse quill stands. Spectators file out, heads bowed, boots silent on ward‑hushed stone. Some cast sideways pity‑glances; others look right through me, calibrating optics for an inconvenient ghost. I don’t flinch until I catch the hazel stare of Fenya Sokolov—second‑year Rune Pathologist, debate partner, friend in better hours. She holds my gaze a full two seconds, lips parted with words she’ll never risk. Then she pivots, cloak flaring like a coward’s white flag.

Fine.

For a moment the chamber empties, the hush so complete I can hear my blood choreographing revenge between heartbeats. Then he appears. A boy no older than twelve slides from behind a pillar, clutching something in both fists. Regulation uniform two sizes too big, knees scabbed, eyes ringed in feverish gratitude. Recognition kicks in. The girl’s brother.

His voice is a library whisper: "Mister Rao?"

I drop to a squat, level with his terror. Up close I see dried blood in his hair—someone tried to clean him up and ran out of time.

"That’s me," I say, trying not to sound like the cellblock reaper. "Your sister holding steady?"
He nods enough to rattle bones. "They moved her to Intensive Miracle Ward. The doctors said… they said she shouldn’t be breathing, but she is. Because of you."

I want to smile but the muscles misfire. He extends a crumpled scrap of paper—Academy prescription pad torn corner, ink smeared by sweat and hope. I unfold it:
THANK YOU — scrawled in looped child letters. Beneath, a shaky glyph: a phoenix feather entwined with the null sign. The improvised rune for second chances.

My throat closes. All the snark, all the polished cynicism, gone useless against this guerrilla gratitude. I tuck the note inside my sleeve where badges used to be.

"Tell her she owes me for the—" The joke dissolves. I try again. "Tell her… I’m still on call. If she needs me."

He swallows, salutes in sloppy imitation of arrival drills, and scurries toward public egress before regulators notice him. His footfalls fade, but the warmth he left stings hotter than ward‑burns.

A new figure approaches: Superintendent Helene Draxis, steward of disciplinary facilities, reputation forged from tungsten and final notices. She carries a crystal tablet showing logistics: escort rotation, asset reclamation, restricted corridor clearance. Her gaze sweeps the room, verifying the emptiness before she addresses me.

"Rao, you are to vacate Academy grounds within the hour. Dormitory access revoked. Library credentials purged. Per code 117-B you may retrieve personal effects under supervision. Formal sentencing will be ratified at 0600. Until then, you are an unperson."
I stand, coat flapping ragged where badges used to anchor it.
"Does per code 117‑B include a farewell parade? Balloons? Maybe a marching band in minor key?"
She stares as if parsing a dialect she once knew and forgot on purpose. "Your humor is a waste of time."
"This whole thing is a waste of time, look around you." I gesture at the deserted benches, the rune‑scarred podium still tasting my DNA.
"Do we need an escort to the gate, or will the walls move for me?"
"The walls," she says, monotone as a null field, "are not your concern. Two regulators will accompany you."

She flicks her wrist; behind her, a pair of crimson‑armored sentries peel from shadow, wands holstered but hands tense.
Regulators again. Full circle. Cosmic joke.

Draxis turns to leave, stops, and without facing me asks,

"Was it worth it?"

There it is—the question everyone pretends is rhetorical. I check the sleeve pocket where fragile paper presses my pulse.

"Every magicked‑up minute."

She exhales through nostrils, either disdain or reluctant respect—I can’t read the signature. Then she walks. The regulators flank me.

We exit through Processional Exit Hallway E, a rabbit warren designed more for ceremony than traffic efficiency: Corinthian columns, azurite mosaics depicting sanitized miracles, chandeliers forged from salvaged phoenix ash. Everything screams victory lap, but tonight the corridor is a morgue in holiday lights.

My boots echo. The regulators’ boots refuse to—sound dampened by hushwards, because silence enforces dignity.

Halfway down the passage a flock of first‑years waits, jittering. They part like water around an oil spill, avoiding eye contact. One whispers,

"That’s him," another mutters, "Null-field butcher."

The nickname ricochets inside my skull—brand new, already rusting.
I keep walking.

At the corridor’s midpoint stands a statue of Madame Rehka Lyra, the Academy’s patron saint of sanctioned resilience. Bronze robes flow in alchemical perpetuity. She cradles an hourglass; inside, infinitesimal stardust resets every sixty seconds—a reminder that time, like policy, can be bent but never broken. Someone has draped a white ribbon around her wrist, symbol of structural mercy. I snort. Even effigies get sympathy.

A gust slides through hidden vents, fluttering my coat hem, stirring the still‑bleeding tear where badges were evicted. The fabric whispers against itself, almost shaping words.

At the ornate arch marking the transition from tribunal wing to dormitory‑spine, my escorts halt. They confer silently—one projects a hologlyph verifying transfer; the other adjusts sonic cuffs clipped to his belt. A warded turnstile looms ahead, currently dormant but hungry.

"Retrieve your belongings," the taller regulator says. He holds up a scanner. "We monitor. No unexpected artifacts."

I nod, step through the turnstile. It lights up, scanning for contraband miracle residue. The brand on my wrist—a new sigil formed by the suppression spell—pulses, interacting with the gate. For a second I expect alarms, grapples, maybe a humiliating strip search. But the ward recognizes its own handiwork: asset neutralized. A green rune blinks. Passage granted.

Dormitory corridor: dim, late, smelling of sweat, ink, and the last days of childhood. My doorplate has already rewritten itself from RAO, B. — CLERICAL TRACK to VACANT – TO BE ASSIGNED. Efficient.

Inside, the room is smaller than I remember, but grief always expands interior dimensions. I shove textbooks, arcane notebooks, half‑finished null field equations into a travel satchel. The canvas strains; calculation shards poke through seams. On the nightstand: a holo‑photo of my mother mid‑laughter, pre‑illness, when magic was still bedtime myth. I hesitate, then slip the wafer behind the boy’s note—double‑layered talisman against institutional amnesia.

At the closet, my reflection glints off a cracked mirror. Coat torn, eyes sunken, brand glowing sullen violet. The man staring out looks feral but awake.

"Rules be damned," I whisper. The mirror doesn’t argue.

Satchel slung, I return to the corridor where regulators wait like bored gargoyles. We march toward the exit logistics have mapped. Each step scatters the ghosts of conversations, friendships, lectures, debts. Hungry silence devours them before they settle.

Finally, a T‑junction. Left leads to administration egress and the world beyond. Right leads deeper inside—to academic spires, relic halls, lecture theaters. My escorts steer left.

But halfway through the pivot, I stop.

Because from the depths of Corridor Delta a light flickers—old gaslamp alley glow, impossible inside this palace of sanctioned lumens. It beats like a pulse, a dare. And I remember: Ghost‑Lit Corridors wait. Vitrines. Statues. A private zero‑hour pilgrimage to mock saints and mourn badges.

"I need the restroom," I say.

The regulators exchange glances—regulation code allows biological breaks. One points left toward public washrooms. I point right.

"These pipes," I lie, "don’t play nice with null field burns. There’s a med‑niche past the reliquary. Two minutes."

They hesitate. Then the scanner beeps—flatline normal. They checked me for active magic and found nothing because I'm suppressing my own signature. Their overconfidence is their blind spot. I layer exhaustion across my shoulders, add a tremor to my hand. Permission granted with curt nods.

I walk right. I don’t hurry—that would trigger pursuit. But inside, my pulse drumming a war cadence, narrative gravity pivots.

Each step away from egress is a step toward something else—Ghost‑Lit Corridors, vitrines of legendary healers, the roaring silence of icons who saved like I did but with permission slips. My destination unfurls ahead: a shadowed artery no longer patrolled at classroom hours.

Behind me, regulators remain silhouettes at the edge of ward light, giving the illusion of trust. Good. They don’t know I’m already forging a new badge from resolve and spite.

At the mouth of the reliquary hall I pause. I take the crumpled note from my sleeve, feel its soft rebellion whisper live. Tuck it near my heart. Straighten coat torn open for new mythologies.

I step forward, into gloom lit by relic glow, leaving suspension verdicts to fossilize behind me. They took my badges. They couldn't take the hands that earned them. I kept those, if not the dignity of title or station. If I left, I left with clean hands in my pockets and my gaze level with theirs.
