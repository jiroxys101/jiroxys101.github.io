Chapter 09: The Fugitive

Scene 10

The gold ring in my pocket pulsed.

It wasn't a standard mana-resonance. It was a recognition. As if the cold, dark fragment had finally noticed the frequency of the Ouroboros and found a matching vibration in the void. My left eye—the bleeding one—twitched with a violent, electric spasm.

Reality inverted. The floor vanished, replaced by a vacuum that sucked the air from my lungs. The world bleached white, the room erased by a humming silence.

For one shattering nanosecond, the hospice room was gone. Papa was gone. The Ouroboros was gone. I wasn't standing on a floor. I wasn't breathing air. I was—

Nothing.

My skin registered temperature, but wrong—a burning cold that felt like being dipped in liquid nitrogen and expecting flames. Every hair follicle screamed. I tried to shiver but my body had forgotten the reflex; there was no warmth to conserve, no core to protect. Just an absence so complete it had texture—like running your fingers across the surface of a silence that had killed every sound that ever existed.

This was the absolute absence of everything that had ever been. An infinity that had already forgotten the stars; a patience so vast it made the geological history of Terra feel like a frantic, passing thought. All memory of light was erased. The absolute cold of a universe that had never known a sun. A silence that mocked the frantic passage of time.

Attention without organs pressed against me, an awareness without origin. It bore down on the edges of my consciousness like a glacier against a pebble. Patient. Heavy. Older than etymology.

I tried to scream. Frigid silence flooded my open mouth. My throat a hard knot of tissue that refused to vibrate. My diaphragm buckled against the pressure. My chest locked tight. I was only a mouth, agape to a universe that had never invented sound. I clawed at the absence with a reflex that no longer had meaning.

I tasted the fine, grey dust of a universe that had run out of fuel billions of years ago and was merely waiting for the final cooling to finish. Ashes of stars that had names no one would ever speak. There was no "Bodhi." There were no "Dinesh." There was only the End.

And in that End, there was a presence.

It didn't have a face.
It didn't have a name.
It didn't have a voice.
But it was there,
waiting at the terminus of every possible timeline.
It wasn't predatory.
It wasn't evil.
It was simply...
inevitable.
And it was connected to me.

The Ouroboros behind my head wasn't a weapon I'd forged. It was a tether. A thin, recursive line of ink and gold that stretched from my chest across the impossible distance to that final silence.

The contact bypassed my heart and went straight for the architecture of my self. My identity, already thinned by the memory-trade, buckled under the absolute pressure of the void. For a second, 'Bodhi' was just a label stuck to a crumbling wall.

The vision released me—

—and I came back wrong.

My legs surrendered, the neuro-mechanical link severing as I hit the floor. The signal from brain to limb scrambled in transmission. I crashed to the linoleum hard enough to jar my teeth, hard enough to feel the cold of the floor slam through my palms and into my shoulder blades.
For three terrible seconds, I couldn't feel where my limbs were. Proprioception had failed. I was a consciousness floating somewhere near a body that had forgotten its own geography.

Then the vomiting.

My stomach revolted, purging a thin, acidic stream of bile across the linoleum. It splattered my hands and pooled against my knees, tasting of copper and ash and something else—something that didn't belong in human chemistry. My eyes watered. Snot joined the bile. A sound escaped me—a thin, jagged whimper. The noise of a wounded creature laid out in left lateral recumbent across the shoulder of an exit ramp that cannot understand why everything hurts.

I wasn't just shaking. My cells were struggling to remember how to be matter. Somewhere in the span between the void and the hospice floor, I had forgotten the molecular instructions for existing.

Every clinical defense had been stripped away, leaving only a bile-cold truth: I knew, with a certainty that bypassed every clinical defense I had left, that I had looked into the mouth of the thing coming for all of us. I had recognized it in the mirror.

"Beta?"

Papa's voice cut through the horror, sounding like it was coming from the other side of a vast canyon. The vision retreated, but it didn't disappear. It stayed in the corner of my eye, a flickering patch of unrendered space that refused to resolve.

I looked at my hands. Still trembling. Still slightly translucent. Bile cooling between my fingers.

I was no longer just saving my father. I was becoming an anchor for the end of the world.

Scene 11

I reached out blindly, my fingers scrabbling against the cold hospital linoleum until they found the metal rail of the bed. I pulled myself up, my muscles feeling like they were composed of wet sand, until I could feel the heat of Papa's hand again.

The world was shedding its texture. The linoleum under my hands felt smooth in a way that defied molecular physics—slick as glass, then sudden as velvet. The shadows glitched. Only his hand remained an anchor.

Behind my head, the Ouroboros began to stabilize. The frantic, stuttering rotation smoothed into a heavy, rhythmic yaw. The gold syntax-traceries stopped flickering and settled into a steady, low-level hum. I could feel the "Render Load" easing, the localized gravity well dissipating as the spell found its equilibrium.

The cost had been paid. The initial transaction was complete. Now all that remained was the debt-service—the stolen minutes I’d bought with the ruins of my heart.

"I'm here," I rasped. My voice sounded like it had been dragged through a gravel pit. "I'm still here, Papa."

My dad watched me, a slow, shaking hand reaching out as if to steady the Ouroboros's glow. His eyes were wet, but his jaw was set with the hard satisfaction of a man seeing his best student finally master a forbidden blueprint. The way his fingers tightened on my wrist told me he recognized the bridge I’d built, and the terrible price I’d paid for the stone. He looked at me the way he used to look at the suspension cables of the Keshava Bridge—acknowledging the strength of the arc while mourning the stress he knew every bolt was under.

"You went somewhere, beta," he whispered. He weakly squeezed my wrist. "For a few seconds, you... you weren't here. You were looking at the math on the other side."

I forced a smile, my face stretching like a texture map pulled too thin. "I saw the terminus, Papa. I saw where the lines end."

"And?"

"And it's empty," I admitted, my voice dropping to a whisper. "It's just... quiet. Perfectly quiet."

Papa’s eyes softened. "Then we shouldn't waste our noise, beta. We have a borrowed hour. Don't spend it staring into the dark. Tell me... tell me what I missed."

The question hit me harder than the void. Tell me what I missed.

I settled into the vinyl chair, my knees still shaking, and leaned closer. He was right. The Abyss could wait. The "Conceptual Zero" could wait. I wanted the heat of his hand and the sound of his voice. I wanted the nineteen years of missed conversations to start now.

"You missed my nineteenth birthday," I started, the words tumbling out before I could check them. "The one where I finally beat your time on the Structural Integrity Sim. I sent you a letter, but the Clinic filters blocked it. I wanted to tell you that I didn't use the standard load-bearing algorithms. I used that 'Resonance' trick you showed me with the wine glass and the tuning fork. I brought the whole simulation down with a single harmonic frequency."

Papa’s eyes lit up, the dementia fading behind a spark of pure, technical delight. "The harmonic shear? You found the frequency?"

"I found it," I said, tears pricking my eyes. "But the proctors didn't like it. They called it 'unorthdox.' They said I broke the testing environment."

"Good," Papa wheezed, a small, triumphant smile breaking through the pain mask. "If the environment can't handle the truth, it deserves to break. What else, Bodhi? What else did they try to censor?"

"Everything," I laughed, a wet, jagged sound. "They tried to censor my thesis on the Vatican's 'Divine geometries.' I tried to prove that the Saints weren't divine, just... highly optimized. That their miracles were just efficient code execution."

"And the expulsion?" he asked gently.

"It wasn't for the thesis," I admitted, looking down at our joined hands. "It was for the fight. Soldatos called you a 'deprecated relic.' I broke his nose."

Papa laughed—a dry, clear sound that rang with a sudden, startling lucidity, untethered by the fog, sharp and familiar as a slide-rule snap. A high, resonant note cutting through the hospital’s ambient hum like puja ghanti.
"Oh, beta. That wasn't a fight. That was a correction."

And for the first time in three years, the words flowed without the metallic grit of the clinic or the scripted weight of the Academy between us. We talked of the small, stupid, human things that the universe was currently trying its best to delete. We talked about Maa, about the smell of rain on hot asphalt in the Southern Sector, about the way the light hit the dust in his old workshop.

"Tell me about her," I whispered, needing to hear it one last time. "Tell me about the time she tried to make that cardamom cake and set the kitchen on fire. I... I can't quite feel the warmth of that memory anymore."

"That wasn't a fire, Bodhi," he smiled, his eyes drifting to a point in space I couldn't see. "That was a meltdown. She insisted the oven was calibrated wrong. She made me take the whole thing apart. And while I was reassembling the gas line, she was laughing. She was always laughing when things went wrong. She used to say that entropy was just the universe trying to improvise jazz."

He squeezed my hand. "She would have been proud of your bridge, beta. Even if you had to burn the blueprint to build it."

Scene 12

Papa leaned back against the hospital pillows, his chest rising and falling with a shallow, rhythmic wheeze that my bones transcribed as "Interest Accruing."

"You remember the Keshava Bridge, Bodhi? The one we built in the Southern Sector when you were ten?"

I nodded, the memory flickering with a dull, grey jitter. I remembered the heat of the sun on the steel girders, the smell of hot tar, the way the wind would whistle through the suspension cables. But the awe I’d felt—the way I used to look up at those massive arcs and think Papa was a wizard of iron—was already gone. It was just a data-point now. A bridge. Made of metal. In the south.

"You remember how the wind sounded in the cables?" Papa asked, his voice barely a rasp. "It wasn't whistling, Bodhi. It was singing."

"The wind shears off the struts," I said, reciting the textbook definition I’d learned in his study. "Aeroelastic flutter. It creates a vortex."

"No," Papa said, closing his eyes. "That's the math. That's the excuse the engineers tell themselves so they can sleep at night. But the bridge wasn't strong because we poured more concrete, beta. It was strong because we let it move. We built the weakness into the design."

He opened his eyes, and for a second, the dementia fog cleared completely. I saw the man who had taught me to read blueprints before I could read books.

"Every structure has a frequency," he whispered, his eyes distant as if calculating the shear strength of the ceiling tiles. "But it's not just bridges, Bodhi. It's people. It's history."

"Resonance," I murmured.

"Resonance," he agreed. "The Akasha, the Vatican, this Ouroboros you've got spinning behind your head... it thinks it's stone. It thinks it's absolute. But it's just geometry, Bodhi. It's just load-bearing code."

He turned his focus back to me, his gaze sharpening into something diamond-hard.

"Do you remember the day the monsoon took the roof off the shelter in sector 7? Everyone was trying to hold the tin sheets down with sandbags. They were fighting the wind with weight. But the wind, it doesn't care about weight. It cares about flow."

I nodded, the memory surfacing—a grey, wet day, the smell of ozone and fear. "You opened the back doors," I said. "You created a wind tunnel."

"I let the wind pass through," he corrected. "I stopped fighting the force and started working with the flow. That is Resonance, beta. Finding the point where the enemy's strength becomes your momentum. The harder they try to make the system static—the harder they try to freeze the world into their image—the more kinetic energy acts as a poison to them."

I looked at the Ouroboros. Perfection is brittle. It was tactical advice disguised as engineering philosophy. He wasn't talking about bridges. He was talking about The System. He was talking about the God-Machine that was currently eating my memories to fuel his heartbeats.

"It's too heavy, Papa," I said, my voice cracking. "The debt... I can feel it eating the edges of everything. I don't know how to stay 'me' and keep this going."

Papa’s hand found mine again. His grip was shivering, not with the weakness of age, but with a high-frequency micro-tremor that felt like a bridge-cable singing under terminal tension. I could feel the heat of the soul-instance I’d patched onto him—it was too hot, the bandwidth of his younger self trying to squeeze through the narrow, calcified channels of his dying brain. It was a mismatch of data and hardware, a monsoon being shoved through a straw. Every shudder passed through his palm into mine, a rhythmic vibration that felt like it was trying to transcribe his engineering blueprints directly into my blood.

"Then don't try to be the whole bridge, beta. Be the vibration. Be the unhandled exception. The universe is a vast, rigid architecture, Bodhi. It’s built to withstand pressure, but it’s terrified of resonance. Don't fight their walls. Find the hairline fracture they painted over. Find the resonance point where the math doesn't add up."

He leaned forward, his voice dropping to a conspiratorial whisper, as if we were back in his study planning a heist on the laws of physics.

"And then... just push."

He coughed, and the sound wasn't organic. It was a jagged, electronic rasp that made the air around his lips pixelate. This time, the Ouroboros didn't just flare—it glitched. A violent spike of violet light tore through the privacy curtain, de-rendering a three-foot section of the wall beside the bed into absolute, humming black. I could see the framework of the hospice ward—the underlying geometry of the room—flickering beneath the surface like a skeleton through translucent skin.

Papa didn't even blink at the void. He was watching me with an intensity that made the golden-black voids of my eyes burn, the heat radiating from my sockets as if my own corneas were being overwritten by the sheer density of his gaze. It was a feedback loop; the de-rendered wall and my own entropic eyes were vibrating at the same frequency, a localized resonance that threatened to strip the textures from my very soul.

"Resonance, Bodhi. Resonance is how you break the debt. It's how you... you..."

His voice trailed off, his jaw working as if he’d forgotten the next word in the sequence, the hardware failing to keep up with the processing load of his own revelation. The "Conceptual Zero" in his neural pathways was accelerating, eating the connections faster than my spell could rebuild them. The stolen hour was reaching its final partition.

The somatic thrum in my marrow wasn't just a staccato; it was a search. It increased in frequency, a rapid-fire drill that made my teeth ache and my jaw lock tight, searching for the structural weakness in my own skeleton. One-two-three-four. I could feel the debt-timer ticking in the hollow of my bones, counting down the thirty-one minutes of stolen time I had left, a deadline etched in marrow.

"You're tired, Papa," I whispered, my voice coming out hoarse, barely audible over the high-frequency hum that was currently trying to dismantle my ribcage. "Rest for a second, Papa. We have time."

"No," he said, the word a final, heavy strike of a hammer on a resonant beam. "We don't. You have work to do, beta. You have a system to dismantle. And you have to do it before there's nothing left of you to remember why you started."

He closed his eyes, his breathing settling into a slow, labored rhythm that sounded like heavy machinery cooling down. I stayed there, holding his shivering hand, watching the Ouroboros spin and the shadows crawl. The word Resonance echoed in my head like a virus, vibrating against the walls of my mind until I could see the hairline fractures in the world around me.

I realized then that he wasn't just giving me advice. He was giving me my first mission. He was handing me the key to the very cage I’d just locked myself into.

Scene 13

The Ouroboros stuttered once—a leaden, tectonic thud that vibrated through my teeth—and then the rhythm simply vanished. No decay, no echo. Just the sudden, biting cold of an empty Ward.

The Ouroboros behind my head didn't exhaust itself; it simply dispersed. The ink-fluid mass thinned into a fine, black mist, and the violet light guttered, the room flickering back toward the harsh, indifferent grey of the hospice ward.

The world was re-rendering.

But I was different.

I summoned the memory of the cardamom cake—the one we’d just laughed about.

It came up clear at first. I could smell the sweet, warm spice. I could see the flour on her cheek, the way the sunlight hit the dust motes in the kitchen. I could feel the heat of the oven vent against my legs.

Payment Required, the Ouroboros whispered in the silence of my blood.

I didn't want to let go. I wanted to hoard it. But Papa’s hand was getting cold in mine, and the silence was creeping back into the room.

I closed my eyes and pushed.

It wasn't a passive loss. It was an amputation. I felt the memory slide out of my grasp, pulled by the gravity of the spell. It was a physical extraction, like a hook passing through the grey matter of my temporal lobe.

The smell of cardamom didn't just fade; it turned to dust. Scentless, textureless silicon dust. The warmth of the kitchen vent became a stinging cold.

Then the visual data began to rot.

Her face, which had been high-resolution a second ago, pixelated. The eyes desynced, sliding off the geometry of her face. The audio of her humming slowed down, warping into a deep, guttural drone that sounded like a machine failing.

Glitch. Static. Null.

I tried to hold onto the emotion—the love, the frustration of the burnt cake, the sound of her laughter. But the metadata was stripped away. The emotional context was deleted, leaving only the raw, objective facts.

Subject: Nalini Rao.
Event: Cooking.
Outcome: Fire.

I could see her.

The file for 'Mother' opened with high-resolution clarity. Truth be told, the detail was nothing if not heightened. I could picture every pore on her face, visualize the color of each and every strand of hair. I could tell you exactly how many of them had turned grey from age. How many of them were bleached from the stress no doubt brought on by her sickly husband and errant son. But the emotional metadata had been stripped. There was no warmth, no ache—just a static image of a stranger in my head. I waited for the grief, the usual jagged obstruction in my throat, but the internal space remained flat, unrendered. I was looking at a stranger's passport photo. Caught in 4K. Barren of the context of history. The grief, the love, the aching absence that had defined me for three years—it was gone. Harvested.
I had fed the only thing that made me her son to the Ouroboros in exchange for sixty minutes that had ended with my father calling me by her name.

The universe's arithmetic had balanced.

I'd bought Papa an hour of lucidity with the only thing that made an hour of lucidity matter.

Stolen heartbeats. That's what I'd bought. Stolen, every one of them.

One stolen heartbeat. Two. Three.

The process didn't stop with the cardamom cake. Once the channel was open, the Ouroboros took its tithe. I felt other memories fraying at the edges. The smell of her perfume. The sound of her singing 'Happy Birthday' when I turned ten. The specific, sharp annoyance I used to feel when she reorganized my desk. All of it turned to grey noise.

I was hollowing out. I was becoming efficient. I was becoming... optimized.

I stayed by the bed, my hand still gripping his, as the last of the violet light evaporated and the shadows reclaimed the room. I didn't leave. I couldn't.
I just watched the fading afterimage of the Ouroboros and waited for the bill to come due.

Scene 15 (The Catalyst)

The silence that followed the Ouroboros didn't last. It couldn't. The universe abhors a vacuum, but bureaucracy abhors a discrepancy even more.

Bang. Bang. Bang.

Iron-hard fists hammered a rhythmic, authoritative sequence against the door. The gavel of reality hitting the bench, ready to pass sentence on my attempted theft.

"Open up," a voice boomed from the corridor. Not Soldatos. Not the tactical bark of Marco Cavaretta’s enforcers. This was lower, flatter. The bored, terrifying indifference of the hospital administration. "Asset Retrieval. We have a pickup order for Instance 881-R."

Asset. The word tasted like peroxide—a sterile, clinical classification that stripped the heat from the room faster than the Void ever could. They were just coming to audit the remains of an account. The Bridges that he had built be damned. In their mouth, it was simply a line-item, a depreciation schedule reachng its final zero.

I looked down at Papa. He was still there—the shell of him, at least. The cooling hand I was still gripping, the chest that had stopped rising, the face that had, for one confusing second, mistaken me for the wife he’d lost three years ago.

"Open the door, Mr. Rao. We know you're in there. Protocol mandates immediate processing."

Processing.

I knew what that meant. I’d worked in the clinic. I knew the efficiency of the New Canaan cremation incinerators. I knew how quickly a life was reduced to ash and a database update.

"No," I whispered.

It wasn't a defiance. It was a simple statement of fact. A rejection of the script.

The handle rattled. Then a key slid into the lock with the screech of metal on metal.

I looked at the empty air where the Ouroboros had been. I looked at the shadows lingering in the corners of the room—the "Conceptual Zero" that hadn't quite faded. And I looked at the gold wedding band on my left hand—Maa's ring. The dead, cold circle of grief.

I couldn't let them take him. I couldn't let them turn the warmth of his hand into ash.

The lock tumbled. The door handle turned.

I didn't think. I didn't calculate. Logic abandoned me. My breath came in short, jagged hitches that my brain couldn't categorize. The clinical silence was gone, replaced by a frantic, animal percussion in my chest.

I grabbed the ring from my finger—fumbled it, almost dropped it, caught it against my chest with a frantic, clawing grip. And then I spoke the word again.

Not the full incantation. Not the poem. Just the command. The function call.

"DISMANTLE."

This time, I didn't target the room. I didn't target the dementia. I targeted the distance. I targeted the separation between the vessel on the bed and the object in my hand.

Collapse, I willed it. Collapse the probability.

The world didn't just invert this time; it bleached.

A flash of Absolute White—not light, but the overload of every sensor in my body hitting maximum simultaneously—erased the room. The sound of the door opening was swallowed. The smell of the jungle-rot was cauterized. For a single heartbeat, there was no hospital, no Papa, no me. There was only the white, humming silence of a file being overwritten.

And then, heat.

Scorching, blinding heat against my palm.

The whiteout snapped off.

I was on my knees, my hand clenched against my chest in a white-hot scream of pain. The nerves in my palm were registering only static, a thermal overload that smelled of burnt sugar and copper. An electric tang, sharp as ionized silk and heavy as burning gold, filled the ward.

The door swung open.

Two orderlies stepped in—hulking shapes in pastel blue scrubs, dragging a retrieval gurney between them.

I didn't wait to see what they saw. I didn't wait for the questions. I turned toward the window I’d cracked earlier. My left hand was a radiating kiln; as I neared the glass, the thermal spike hit the cold night air outside. The pane didn't wait for my weight—it crazed instantly, spider-webbing into a million fractures before I even made contact. I went through it in a cloud of glass-dust, hitting the iron of the fire escape with a bone-jarring rattle.

Rain and exhaust lashed my face, a freezing welcome that smelled of the city. The shock of the cold slammed into my marrow—heavy, tectonic—a sharp reminder of a world that still followed the rules of physics. But it felt real. It felt rendered.

And then the pain hit.

It wasn't just the burn on my palm. It was the backlash of the Distance Collapse. My joints felt like they were being pulled apart by opposing magnets. Every step down the fire escape sent a jolt of vertigo through my skull, as if my inner ear was still trying to calibrate to a room that no longer existed. My proprioception was glitching; I felt taller, then shorter, then weightless. The universe was trying to reject me, trying to flag my body as an error in the scene geometry.

I scrambled down the metal stairs, the iron ringing under my boots, my left hand still clenched tight against my chest, guarding the searing, pulsing heat that was burning a hole through my shirt.

I ran.

Scene 16 (The Aftermath)

I didn't stop running until I hit the canal district, three miles south. My breath didn’t come in gasps; it was a rhythmic scouring of my throat, each inhalation tasting of sulfur and cold, industrial sleet. My lungs weren't just burning—they felt raw, the alveoli struggling to process the oxygen-poor soup of the Medical District’s exhaust. Every stride was a deliberate refusal of my own anatomy, my legs no longer feeling like flesh, but like heavy, uncalibrated servos dragging through the mud.

But my hand—my left hand—was still clenched against my sternum, protecting the heat.

I collapsed against the wet brick wall of an alleyway, sliding down into the muck. The rain was torrential now, a curtain of static that drowned out the city.

A crackle of radio static cut through the rain.

It was coming from above—or maybe from a dropped radio on the fire escape back at the hospice, echoing in my memory? No. It was a projection. A "Signal Bleed" from the spell I'd just cast. The Ring was still active, still tuned to the frequency of Ward 404.

"Hey Haas, are you on the fourth floor?"

A sound of raucous, heaving coughing answered. Wet, uncontrolled.

"You realize taking a 'smoke break' doesn't mean you can just face an entire blunt in the front gardens, dude."

More coughing.

"It's medicinal, Jimenez," the second voice wheezed.

"Oh yeah, sure, I forgot you had a script for 'night blindness' or whatever the fuck," Jimenez replied, the sarcasm dripping even through the static.

"Bite meeeHEEee—" The voice trailed off into another fit.

"Cool, well when you catch your breath, can you confirm the status of Instance 881-R for Municipal Transfer?" Jimenez asked. "I'm getting weird reports from Jackson on that floor."

"Yeah. Give me five."

I needed to see. The compulsion hit me like an itch behind the eyes. I leaned against the wet brick, shifting the Burning Ring to my thumb, and traced a quick, jagged sigil in the condensation on the wall. A thief’s cantrip. A Scrying Frame.

The brick dissolved, replaced by a flickering, grayscale view of Ward 404 from the perspective of a security feed. But I didn't stop at the surface. The Ring was pulsing against my thumb, demanding a connection. It wanted more data. It wanted context.

I saw a figure in the corner of the room in my vision. Not Haas. Someone else. Wrapped in shadow and... vines.

Jackson.

I focused on him. I pushed my consciousness through the scrying frame, riding the lingering resonance of the spell. I didn't just want to see him. I wanted to see what he had seen. I wanted the log file.

Accessing Memory block...

The world shifted. The grey alleyway vanished.

I was Jackson.

I was standing in the doorway of Room 404, five minutes ago. I could smell the antiseptic floor cleaner and the faint, underlying scent of old age. I was tired. My feet hurt. I just wanted to tag the body, get the signature, and go take my break.

I walked to the bed. The curtain was drawn.

"Mr. Rao?" I said. "Just checking the vitals."

No answer. Typical.

I pulled the curtain back.

The old man was lying there, still as stone. Eyes closed. Breath gone. Standard expiration.

I reached out to check for a pulse—the muscle memory of a hundred hospice shifts taking over. He expected the cooling stillness of Instance 881-R, the rote paperwork of a Municipal Transfer. But his fingers didn’t find the steady rhythm of a heart; they found only the velvet resistance of petals.

"What the..."

I looked down.

The wrist wasn't skin. It was green. A thick, verdant green that pulsed with a slow, bioluminescent light. As I watched, a vine erupted from the cuff of the pajama shirt, spiraling up my arm faster than a snake strike.

I tried to pull back, but my hand was stuck. The vine wasn't just holding me; it was integrating me.

"Help!"

The word barely left my lips before the room exploded.

It wasn't an explosion of fire. It was an explosion of life.

The floor tiles shattered as thick, ancient roots tore through the foundation. The ceiling dissolved into a canopy of hanging moss. The air filled with pollen so thick it choked me, tasting of honey and rot.

And the man on the bed...

He wasn't a man anymore. He was a garden. A sudden, violent terraforming event confined to a single hospital bed. A dark purple flower, large as a human head, burst from his chest, its petals unfurling with a wet, tearing sound.

"No, no, no—"

I backed away, but the corner was already gone. The walls were soft. They were breathing. The ivy caught my legs, dragging me down into the soft, warm loam that used to be the floor. I wasn't being attacked. I was being planted.

The last thing I saw before the moss covered my eyes was the flower on the bed, pulsing, beating, thrumming with a sound that was half-heartbeat, half-hymn.

End Log.

I gasped, ripping my hand away from the alley wall. The connection severed.

Back in the scry, in the present, Haas was standing in the doorway.

"I can't find them," Haas said, his voice trembling on the feed. "There's only plants."

I watched, mesmerized, as Haas spun in a slow circle. The room wasn't a room anymore. It was a jungle.

"What do you mean 'There's only plants'?!" Jimenez shouted.

"It MEANS there's only PLANTS!"

"Are you still smoking in the gardens, you clown? Go to ROOM 404!"

"I AM on the fourth floor!" Haas yelled at his shoulder mic, throwing his hands up. "I am STANDING in his ROOM."

"AND?"

"AND you don't have to SHOUT at me!"

"WHAT'S IN HIS ROOM?"

"There's more plants!"

"WHAT?"

"There's just more plants! Oh, hey Jackson. Jackson's just sitting in the corner."

I shifted the scrying angle. Jackson was indeed in the corner. Or rather, he was part of the corner, wrapped in a cocoon of flowering ivy, his face frozen in a rictus of terror that was slowly being softened by a layer of velvet moss.

"CHECK the BED," Jimenez commanded.

I watched Haas approach the bed. The sheets rustled as he pulled them back.

"There's still plants!"

"WHERE ARE YOU RIGHT NOW?"

"APPARENTLY I'M AT 'PLANTS'!" Haas screamed, tearing his cap off. "Screw it, Jackson is babbling, I'm strapping them to the gurney."

"WHY ARE YOU STRAPPING JACKSON TO THE GURNEY?"

"FUCK. YOU."

The scry flickered, but not before I saw it one last time. The thing on the bed. Vines wove themselves into a static, green facsimile of the man, a chlorophyll-etched silhouette mimicking the curve of his ribs. It didn't look dead. It looked... blooming. A dark purple flower opened where his heart should have been, pulsing with a slow, biological rhythm.

I cut the connection. The brick wall rushed back.

Laughter bubbled up from my chest, a jagged, broken sound that hurt my throat. It wasn't funny. It was horrific.
It was the end of my life. But the image of Haas screaming "I'M AT PLANTS" while my dad turned into a goddamn botanical garden was too much for the structural integrity of my mind to handle.

I laughed until I couldn't breathe. I laughed until my knees gave out and I slid down the wall into the mud. And then, without any transition at all, the laughter shattered.

I curled into a ball in the filth of the alley, clutching the burning ring to my chest, and sobbed. I sobbed for the plants. I sobbed for the good and bad that I had lost for a mere hour. I sobbed for the thief, the monster, and the god who was currently crying in a pile of garbage because he’d finally performed a miracle, and it was a punchline.

Why did it always have to end like this?
