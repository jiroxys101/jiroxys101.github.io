Chapter 12: Torrent of Souls

Scene 1

The crash is immediate. Absolute.

Gravity dies. The concept of "down" is rewritten as "everywhere." I don't fall into the Shard—I'm detonated by it.

The impact is ontological. The boundaries of Bodhi Rao—the ego, the history, the specific biological arrogance of being one person—slough off like wet paper.

A million images per second claw through my optic nerve. Not a stream. A shotgun blast to the neo-cortex. Each one is a full-body experience I have no consent to endure.

1920. Birmingham.

The smell hits first. Boiled water, galvanized steel, and the copper tang of blood. The heat is suffocating, a wet wool blanket pressed against my face.

I am screaming. No, she is screaming, but my throat tears with the effort. I feel the contraction ripple through a pelvis that feels like it’s splitting at the symphysis. The pain is a rhythmic, blinding white noise that drowns out the rain hammering against the tin roof.

"Push," a voice says. Rough. Male. Impatient.

I bear down. I feel the anatomy rearrange itself, the wet, sliding terror of a crown breaching. The pressure is astronomical. My vision whites out. I taste the iron of a bitten tongue and the salt of sweat dripping into my eyes. This isn't a memory; memories don't feel like this.

The baby slides out—slippery, hot, alive—and the relief is hit immediately by a wave of cold terror.

Silence. The baby isn't crying.

I try to reach out with hands that are calloused and trembling, but the scene fractures.

1040. Namgyeong.

The air is different here. Acrid. Ionized. The taste of petrichor and synthetic ramen broth from a vent below.

I’m on a fire escape. The metal grating bites into my knees. It’s raining—that greasy, acidic drizzle that coats the sensory dome in a permanent gray slick.

I can’t breathe. There’s a serrated warmth between my fourth and fifth ribs. I look down. The knife handle is yellow plastic. Cheap. Mass-produced. The hand holding it is shaking.

"I didn't want to," the kid says. He can't be more than twelve. His eyes are wide, reflecting the neon pink of a 'GIRLS GIRLS GIRLS' sign flickering across the alley.

The pain arrives on a delay. A grinding vibration that travels up a spine I don't possess. My lungs try to inflate and find only fluid. I taste copper flooding a mouth that is suddenly filled with blood. My knees buckle, hitting wet pavement that smells of urine and old rain.

I feel the pavement vibrations—footsteps running away. Splash. Splash. Splash.

I try to curse him. I try to say, Run, you little shit. But only a wet bubble of blood pops on my lips. The neon pink light pulses. Pink. Pink. Black.

1808. Marseilles.

Soft lips press against mine.

They are warm, tasting of cheap red wine and clove cigarettes. The stubble on his chin scrapes my cheek—a friction that feels electric, illicit.

The sea breeze coming through the open window smells of salt and rotting fish, but in here, in this small, candlelit room, it smells of lavender soap and skin.

I feel the flutter of a pulse against my fingertips, the specific, fragile rhythm of a valve fluttering against a rib cage. I run my thumb over his eyebrow. He smiles. It’s a secret smile, one meant only for this room, for this candle.

"Stay," he whispers.

I open my mouth to promise forever, but the lips turn to dust. The cheek under my hand crumbles into dry gray ash. Calculations replace the romance. The candle flame freezes, stretches, and becomes a flat vector diagram of combustion.

The Kaleidoscope.

The strobe effect accelerates. The delay between lives drops to zero.

I am a soldier in a trench, inhaling mustard gas that blisters my lung tissue into bloody foam.
I am a bride adjusting her veil, feeling the cold silk against sweating skin.
I am an old man staring at a wall in a nursing home, the smell of urine and antiseptic burning my nose.

Faces contort in the final, ugly honesty of agony. Wedding veils dissolve into funeral shrouds in a strobe-light flicker. The rhythmic flicker of hospital lights over gurneys never stops moving, a strobe effect that burns afterimages into retinas that are rapidly failing to process the input.

867 AD. The North Sea.

The water is freezing. Sub-zero. It hits me like a hammer, punching the air out of my chest. Saltwater floods my sinuses, burning like acid. The chaotic roar of the storm is muffled instantly into a deep, crushing silence.

I kick. My boots are too heavy. The chainmail is an anchor dragging me down into the dark. I look up. The surface is a roiling ceiling of gray light, getting further and further away.

My lungs burn. The panic is a cold, desperate animal clawing at my ribcage. I open my mouth to scream, and the ocean rushes in.

The Mind-Break.

It racks my consciousness with relentless efficiency.

I try to scream. My throat convulses, searching for vocal cords that don't exist. There's only the sensation of pressure building behind a face I can't feel.

My sense of self erodes like sandstone in a hurricane. The version of me that once triaged alleyway casualties, the Bodhi Rao who hates magic and drinks too much chai, is now nothing more than a leaking container for a thousand endings.

The trauma is me. It webs across my ego, a sequence of recursive deaths.

I am the scream. I am the knife. I am the drowning. I am the silence of the stillborn.

A sequence of recursive deaths webs across my sense of self until the porcelain of my ego begins to crack. Snap. Snap. Snap. The sound of a mind breaking is wet and heavy, like a femur snapping under hydraulic pressure.

Scene 2

Subjective time dissolves.

There is no 'now'. There is only the data-stream. The 'I' is a rounding error.

The Rice Farmer.

I live a lifetime in a blink.

I am sixty years old. My lower back is a fused knot of chronic pain, a rhythmic, metronomic throbbing that defines my existence. It is the percussion section of sixty years bent over emerald-green paddies.

My hands—calloused, leathered, mapped with scars I don't remember getting—know the weight of rice stalks before my brain does. They move with the mindless efficiency of a machine. Grab. Cut. Bundle. Repeat.

The monsoon drums overhead, a relentless white noise that seeps into my bones. The rain is heavy, cold, and unending. Mud squelches between my toes, warm and alive and teeming.

I know the taste of harvest-season rice, just-cooked, still steaming. Sweet. Starchy. The only reward.
I know the brittle, dry-mouthed grief of a failed harvest. I remember the year the rains didn't come. The dust. The silence in the village. The way my daughter’s eyes grew too big for her face. The hunger is a hollow cramp that never leaves, a roommate living in my gut.

I feel the shame of it. Decades of labor condensed into a single, agonizing breath where the math of survival doesn't add up.

Flick.

The Corporate Drone.

Static bites at the edges of my perception. The emerald paddies dissolve into the gray fabric of a cubicle wall.

I’m on the 36th floor. The air is recycled, stale, keeping a steady temperature of 68 degrees that chills the sweat on my back.

My fingers tap on ghost-keyboards. Click-clack-click-clack. A staccato rhythm that matches the anxious flutter in a chest tight with caffeine and deadline stress. My heart is a bird battering itself against a ribcage made of cheap polyester ties.

My eyes scan spreadsheets bathed in the sickly, buzzing light of office fluorescents—a 60Hz frequency that drills a headache right behind my eyes. I can hear the hum. It’s maddening. A C-sharp drone that never stops.

I understand the syntax of foreign thoughts now. Mandarin. Swahili. Urdu. They overlay my own inner monologue until my internal voice is a crowded room of shouting strangers.

“Did you file the Q3 report?”
“The server is down.”
“My mother is sick.”
“I need a drink.”

The scents are a chaotic assault: the damp sweetness of wet earth from the rice paddy clashing with the industrial bite of toner ink. The cloying rot of fish in a sun-baked market in Jakarta mixes with the metallic tang of an overheated server farm in Silicon Valley.

I taste stale coffee on a tongue that isn't mine. It’s burnt. Acidic.
I taste the warmth of mother's milk in a body that is once again small and feverish.
I taste the iron of blood from a cheek bitten in a moment of panic I didn't author.

The Dying Child.

I live through the slow, agonizing decline of a dying child.

The fever is a fire under my skin. My joints ache with a majestic, deep-seated soreness. The sheets are soaked with sweat, turning cold and clammy against my legs.

I reach for a mother who never arrives. I want water. I want the coolness of a hand on my forehead. But there is only the white void of the manifest.

I feel my own pulse slowing down. Thump... thump... ... thump.

The small hand grows cold.

The Archipelago.

My sense of 'Self' thins until the boundaries of Bodhi Rao are nothing more than a suggestion.

I forget New Canaan.
I forget the hospice room.
I forget the smell of antiseptic.
I forget the ring.

I’m becoming an archipelago of borrowed selves. Islands of trauma separated by oceans of static. My singular point of awareness dissolves into a vast, collective ocean of grief.

I am not sinking. I am dispersing.

Scene 3

Amidst the screaming data-stream, a single engram remains unparsed.

It sits in the center of the hurricane, stubborn and corrupted. A black box that refuses to open. A diamond in a sewer.

Protected.

The protection isn't algorithmic. It’s primal.

'Papa.'

The name blinks in the conceptual white-out with the intensity of a lighthouse in a gale. It’s written in a font I recognize—my own handwriting, messy and hurried, from a birthday card I wrote when I was seven.

In that same heartbeat, the ring on my conceptual finger flares.

It doesn't glow. It ignites.

The sensation is immediate and blindingly physical. A searing, tearing heat that wraps around the base of my ring finger. It feels like someone has taken a soldering iron and clamped it down on the bone.

The Branding Iron.

The pain is absolute. It screams louder than the memories of the trench warfare or the dying child. It is a specific, local, personal agony.

It forces the question: Who is hurting?

The soldier in 1917 isn't wearing this ring.
The rice farmer in Jakarta isn't wearing this ring.
The dying child in the hospice isn't wearing this ring.

I am.

The pain acts as a metaphysical stapler. It grabs the scattered pages of my soul—the archipelago of drifting selves—and slams them back into a singular binding.

Snick. Snick. Snick.

I gasp metaphysically—a soundless heave of lungs I no longer possess—and claw myself out of the collective hive mind. I am being dragged back to the surface of my own ego by a hook made of molten obsidian.

The chaos recedes. The roar of a thousand strangers’ lives is muffled by the sudden, rhythmic thrum of a single, distant heartbeat. My heartbeat.

And then comes the smell.

It hits me like a slap. Not a drift of scent, but a chemical weapon deployed against the rot.

Old Spice.

Papa’s scent. Sharp. Peppery. Alcohol and nutmeg and clove.

It cuts through the cloying stench of the trench, the wet dog smell of the alley, the antiseptic of the hospital. It’s a sensory scalpel.

I can smell the specific molecular composition of it—the cheap alcohol base evaporating off warm skin, the synthetic musk that lingers on a collar. It smells like safety. It smells like the passenger seat of his old Honda Civic.

I remember the specific scuff on the shoes Papa wore to my wedding—the left toe, worn down from how he pivoted when he worked. I remember the hesitant, proud smile he gave me when I’d mastered my first complex healing sigil, the way the corners of his eyes crinkled.

This is the one grief that is his. That is mine.

The separation is violent. I rip away from the collective. I am no longer We. I am Me.

I cling to the pain like a life raft in a churning sea. The ring pulses against my spirit, a hot coal that anchors me to the name Bodhi Rao. I have one tether left in this void, and I will let it burn me to the bone before I let it go.

I scream my own name into the void.

"I am Bodhi Rao."

And the void shudders.

Scene 5

I retreat into the White Room.

It’s my mental construct for high-level spellcraft—a sterile, silent void where I can manipulate variables without the distraction of sensory input. Usually, it’s empty. Clean.

Now, it’s crowded.

The 1,000 nodes aren't just data points. They are boulders.

I try to calculate the vector for a mass release. I reach out with my mind to lift the Causal Load, to shift the souls from Stasis to Release.

The Weight.

It hits me like a collapsing building.

My knees buckle. Not my physical knees—I’m floating in a void—but my conceptual avatar is driven into the white floor with a bone-cracking impact.

The weight is astronomical. It’s not just mass; it’s time.

I am trying to lift 1,000 lifetimes of unspent potential.
The weight of the soldier’s unborn children.
The weight of the rice farmer’s unharvested crops.
The weight of the girl’s unkissed lips.

It’s a gravity well of grief so dense it bends the light in my mind.

I grunt, the sound tearing out of my throat. My arms shake, muscles screaming as I try to hold up the sky. I am Atlas, but I am a mortal Atlas, and my spine is turning to dust.

“Too heavy,” I wheeze.

I can’t save them. The realization is a cold knife in the gut. I don't have the bandwidth. I don't have the mana. If I try to process their release individually, I will burn out in nanoseconds. My brain will melt out of my ears before I save the first dozen.

I collapse. The weight pins me to the floor. The screaming of the wireframe souls gets louder, a deafening, accusatory roar.

Failure.

The word blinks in red letters in the HUD of my mind.

I lie there, crushed under the weight of a thousand tragedies, staring at the white ceiling of my own mind. And that’s when I see it.

The crack in the floor.

I’m looking at the problem wrong. I’m thinking like a doctor trying to save patients. I need to think like a demolitionist.

I don't need to carry them out the door.
I don't need to process their paperwork.
I don't need to heal them.

I just need to break the room they’re standing in.

The Fulcrum.

I close my eyes and shift my grip. I stop trying to lift the souls. Instead, I dig my fingers into the flaw in the Shard’s architecture—the congestion itself.

The system is over-pressurized. It’s a boiler with the safety valve welded shut.

If I can’t lift the weight… I’ll use it.

I will weaponize their grief. I will turn the collective scream of 1,000 souls into a battering ram.

I start to laugh. It’s a wet, jagged sound.

“All right,” I whisper to the glitching ghosts. “You want out? Let’s all go out together.”

Scene 4

The medical partition of my mind comes online with the cold, wet thud of a breaker switch thrown in a flooded basement.

It is a violent reboot. The part of me that is Son—the part that is weeping for a father lost to time—is quarantined. Shoved behind a firewall.

The part that remains is the Mechanic.

This is the part of me that dissected cadavers in freezing basements, seeing leverage and fulcrums instead of humanity. The part that diagnosed mana-burn in back alleys by the smell of ozone alone. The part that performed forbidden Causal Inversions when the hospitals turned the poor away.

I open my eyes.

The storm hasn't stopped, but my perception of it has shifted. I am no longer part of the weather. I am the weatherman.

I look at the swirling storm of souls. I don't see tragedy anymore. I see data.

The Polygon Horror.

It is a grotesque, high-fidelity nightmare.

The souls aren't ghostly vapors. They are fracturing spectral scaffolds. Low-resolution renderings of high-definition pain.

A woman to my left. Her face is frozen in a scream, but the geometry is wrong. Her jaw is unhinged, stretching down to her sternum, the polygons stretching like bad texture mapping in a corrupted game. Her eyes are just black triangles. She is buffering, stuttering between two frames of existence: Alive and Dead.

Scream. Silence. Scream. Silence.

To my right, a soldier. His chest is a mess of jagged, intersecting planes where shrapnel hit him. The blood isn't liquid; it's a solid, red mesh that clips through his uniform. He tries to raise a hand, but the limb spasms and de-renders into a cloud of binary dust before reforming.

They are polygonal tragedies. Artifacts of a system that has run out of memory.

A child runs past me—or rather, loops past me. He is trapped in a three-second cycle of tripping and falling. His knee hits the invisible floor, shatters into white shards, and then resets. Trip. Shatter. Reset. Trip. Shatter. Reset.

The sound is not a choir. It is the white noise of an archive on fire. It is the screech of a stylus scratching across a spinning record. A million voices compressed into a single, jagged waveform that spikes into the red.

In my mind, the air suddenly smells of sterile hospital corridors and industrial-grade disinfectant. It’s a grounding sensory memory, a tether to my training. I taste cold, clean water—a sharp contrast to the silt of foreign experience I’ve been swallowing.

The Diagnosis.

I view the horror with absolute clinical detachment. I am not horrified. I am diagnosing.

Symptom: Recursive looping.
Symptom: Texture failure.
Symptom: Geometry clipping.

Diagnostic: System Congestion.

The Shard is a prison.

These souls aren't fuel—they're inmates, freshly processed and permanently stalled. The system can't delete them, and it can't upload them. It’s just stacking them in the buffer.

I count them. Not one by one, but by the load they place on the manifold.

1000 Local Nodes.

Each one a human life trapped in its own final, broken moment. A thousand loops of pain spinning in the dark.

The clinical clarity is my greatest weapon. It gives me the distance I need to find the one thing every prisoner dreams of.

The one thing a system administrator looks for when the server hangs.

An exit.

Scene 5

I scan the architecture of the void with the precision of a surveyor mapping a crime scene.

I run my conceptual hands along the white walls, looking for a seam, a crack, a back door. Anything that suggests the manifold has an edge.

There is none.

The white walls are infinite. A perfect geometric prison designed by alchemists who understood that the most effective way to trap a soul was to remove all points of reference.

I try to calculate trajectories for escape. My mind runs probability curves and conceptual thresholds in a rapid-fire sequence. I visualize vectors of force, angles of incidence. Each simulation collapses. Every path I project ends in a recursive loop that leads me right back to the center of my own awareness. Arrows of intent shatter against the curvature of this space.

The hum of the machine is omnipresent now. A low-frequency thrum that vibrates through my conceptual bones and rattles teeth I no longer have.

The absence of scent is itself a sensory assault. It's a sterile void that mocks my attempt to ground myself. I taste the bitter, metallic aftertaste of failure as one calculation after another hits a dead end.

Then, the realization strikes me with the force of a physical blow.

I'm too Heavy.

My soul-mass, weighted down by the density of my attachments, my memories, and the sheer gravity of my own identity, is what pins me to this white silence. To ascend, to escape the Shard's gravitational pull, I have to become weightless.

The obsidian ring on my finger feels like a leaden anvil. A precious weight that is keeping me from the horizon.

The calculus is brutal: the more I am, the less I can leave.

Scene 6

I look at the memory.

It's not a static image. It's a living, breathing moment stored in the core of my sequence—the root directory, the file that defines home.

The kitchen in Queens. Our kitchen. Cramped, barely eight feet by ten, with linoleum tiles that had seen better decades. One of them, third from the stove, had a crack shaped like a lightning bolt. I used to trace it with my toe during breakfast.

The morning light comes through the window at a forty-five-degree angle, catching the steam rising from two chipped ceramic mugs on the counter. The mugs are mismatched—one pale blue with a faded rooster, one cream-colored with a chip on the rim where I'd dropped it as a kid. The porcelain is warm in my small hands, almost too hot to hold, but I don't let go.

Papa is standing by the stove. He stayed home from the shop that day. Just to be with me. The memory of why is corrupted, lost to time, but the fact of his presence is diamond-hard.

He's humming. An off-key raga I don't know the name of—something his mother used to sing, maybe, or something he made up on the spot. The melody wobbles, missing notes, but it sounds like safety.

The kettle whistles. Not a scream, but a rising, insistent tone—E-flat, if I had to guess. The sound of water reaching the edge of transformation.

Papa lifts the kettle with one hand, pours the boiling water into the mugs with the ease of a man who's done this ten thousand times. The steam rises in twin columns, dancing in the morning light like miniature stars.

Then the clink. The sharp, metallic percussion of a spoon mixing sugar. He stirs three times clockwise, then taps the rim twice. A ritual. His ritual.

The scent arrives last, the way scent always does in memory—complex, layered, overwhelming.

Cardamom, sharp and almost astringent, the green pods cracked open in the pot.

Ginger, earthy and warm, the kind that burns the back of your throat if you breathe it in too fast.

Boiled milk, sweet and comforting, coating the spices with softness.

And underneath it all, cutting through everything—Old Spice. Papa's aftershave. The smell of his collar when he hugged me. The smell of him.

This is the seed. The core file. The definition of what I used to call love.

And to escape the Shard, I don't just have to leave this memory.

I have to po it.

I have to sever the emotional pointer that makes this moment precious. I have to take Papa—this man standing at the stove, humming his off-key song, pouring chai with calloused hands—and turn him into just another stranger. Another ghost in the static.

The ring on my finger feels like a leaden anvil.

I feel the weight of Papa's hand on my shoulder—phantom pressure, the memory of safety. It's keeping me here. Pinning me to this void with the gravity of love.

The calculus is brutal: I can keep this warmth and drown, or I can cut it out and ascend.

I look at Papa's back. The steam rising from the chai. The morning light making the dust dance.

And I raise the scalpel.

Scene 7

I act.

There's no hesitation left. Only the cold, hard certainty of a man who's run the numbers and realized the only way to balance the equation is to cheat.

I visualize the Ego—not as the self, but as a bloated hard drive. Full of corrupted files. Full of hope. And hope, in this specific causal geometry, is just friction. It’s drag.

I raise the blade of my will. It's been honed by a hundred desperate procedures experienced in the kaleidoscope. By every time I chose to cut instead of comfort.

I need to lose weight.

I don't target the memory of my father. I target the warmth of it.

“Self Imposed Restrictive Covenant,” I whisper. The legal term used by the Clerics. It sounds so bureaucratic. So dry.

“I offer the Comfort. I keep the Scar.”

It's a Binding Vow, but twisted. I’m not trading the memory for power. I’m trading the joy of the memory for the physics to escape it. I’m keeping the grief. I’m keeping the guilt. I’m keeping the crushing weight of his absence.

I’m just evicting the love that makes it bearable.

The universe hears the offer. It weighs the sacrifice. To strip the light and leave only the shadow? To keep the wound open but cauterize the nerves that feel relief?

Accepted. The value is double.

I watch as the memory of my father begins to curdle.

It starts at the edges. The kitchen walls don't fade to gray; they rot. The cream color sours into the yellow of a bruise.

Then the steam rising from the chai. It stops looking like dancing stars and starts looking like smoke from a chemical fire.

Papa’s face is next. The warmth leeches out, but the details remain—high-fidelity and cruel. I see the lines of fatigue I never noticed before. I see the disappointment I always feared was there.

The smile—the one that used to mean safety—twists. Without the filter of my love, it looks naive. Pathetic. A weak man smiling at a son he couldn't save.

The scent of Old Spice doesn't vanish. It turns acid.

It hits the back of my throat like tear gas. It smells of cheap alcohol and desperation. It smells of a funeral parlor trying to mask the scent of decay.

The memory is still there. I remember him. I remember the kitchen.

But now, looking at him feels like swallowing a handful of broken glass. The comfort is gone. The safety is gone. All that’s left is the jagged, bleeding fact that he is dead and I am alone.

It burns. God, it burns.

It’s poison in my veins, a virulent miasma of depression that turns the golden light of the past into radioactive sludge. I want to vomit. I want to claw the memory out of my skull.

But I don't. I hold it close. I let the poison fill me up, displacing the heavy, buoyant hope that was weighing me down.

I am not hollow. I am septic.

And toxicity... toxicity is lighter than air.

Scene 8

The weight is gone.

In its place is something faster.

The instant I lock the Covenant, the drag coefficient of my soul drops to zero. The 1,000 souls that were snagging on my empathy, hooking into my guilt, simply slide off. There’s nothing soft left for them to grab. I am hard edges and toxic slippery surfaces.

I am 'Aerodynamic' because I’ve burned off everything that made me drag.

I float in the white silence for a nanosecond, and I feel the urge to crack a joke. Something about airline food. Something about how much lighter you feel after a good purge.

“Please keep your arms and legs inside the trauma response at all times,” I giggle.

The sound is wet and hysterical. It’s not a laugh; it’s a warning siren wearing a clown nose.

I’m not empty. I’m full. I’m full of a cold, high-octane self-loathing that burns cleaner than jet fuel. The memories of Papa—now just jagged shards of guilt—don't weigh me down. They propel me. They are the spikes on the armor.

I survived, the toxic voice whispers. And all it cost was everything good about you.

Worth it, I whisper back, and I actually mean it. That’s the horror. I mean it.

The survival mechanism has worked. I have paid for my life with my capacity for joy, and the receipt is printed on the inside of my eyelids.

Then the Shard vomits us out.

Scene 9

I hit the wet earth hard.

Gravity returns with a brutal, welcoming slap. It knocks the wind out of me, compressing lungs that suddenly need air again.

I bounce. Once. Twice. Then I skid through the mud like a skipping stone made of meat, tearing a furrow through the ruined lawn of the Cavaretta estate.

I stop.

Silence.

Well, not silence. The rain is hissing. The thunder is rumbling. But inside my head? The screaming choir is gone.

“System reboot complete,” I croak. “Please rate your near-death experience on a scale of one to existential terror.”

I spit out a mouthful of mud and ash. It tastes like copper and victory.

I try to stand up. My body protests with a chorus of alarms that I can visualize—literally. A red spectral outline of my skeleton flickers in the corner of my vision.

Left Tibia: hairline fracture.
Ribs 4-6: bruised.
Mana Capacity: 0% (Running on fumes/spite).

The Void-Sight.

I blink. The overlay doesn't disappear. It sharpens.

My eyes burn. The sensation is torrential, as if someone is pouring liquid nitrogen into my sockets. The sclera flood with ink-black mana, obscuring the white in a rising tide of void. My irises fracture, spinning, reconfiguring into complex golden mandalas that act as Heads-Up Displays for reality itself.

I look at the crater. I don't see mud. I see the 'Prime Logic' of the blast.

The residual causal vectors of the explosion hang in the air like glowing gold contrails. The tensile strength rating of the remaining foundation stones flickers. The precise chemical composition of the rain (H2O + Sulfur + trace amounts of Causal Fallout) scrolls past.

It’s brutalist. Sans-serif. The raw syntax of creation, stripped of the user interface.

“Nice graphics,” I mutter to nobody. “A bit high-contrast for my taste, but the frame rate is buttery smooth.”

I start to giggle again. It hurts my ribs, but I can’t stop. It’s just so funny. I’m a goddamn wizard now. I’m the Admin. And all I want to do is curl up in a ball and scream until my throat bleeds.

I look down at my hand.

The obsidian ring—the SIRC anchor—is cool against my skin. I try to summon the memory of Papa again, just to test the scar tissue.

The image comes up instantly. High-definition. 4K resolution.

The kitchen. The chai. The smile.

But the poison hits me just as fast. The smile looks mocking. The warmth feels like a fever. The love I used to feel is just a hollow ache in my chest, a reminder of what was stolen. A notification pops up in my mind: [ENGRAM CORRUPTED: EMOTIONAL_HEADER_MISSING]

“Thanks for the update, Clippy,” I whisper.

I flip the bird to the sky. To the Shard. To the memories I just mutilated to save my own skin.

“I’m back,” I announce to the storm.

I stagger to my feet, swaying like a drunk. The golden HUD spins, locking onto a new target signature approaching from the edge of the crater.

“I’m awake. I’m traumatized. And I am dangerously under-caffeinated.”

I grin. It feels like the skin of my face is splitting.

“Round two?”
