Chapter 06

Scene 24 (Triage in the Janitor's Closet)

The door clicked shut, a final, dull thud that severed me from the sterile hum of the hospital corridor. I stood in the dark for a second; the closet silence washed over me. It smelled of the chemical sting of high-pH surface cleaner and old dust—honest, unlike the lavender spray masking the rot outside.

I fumbled for the string and pulled.

A single, naked bulb buzzed to life. The bulb cast twitching, neurotic shadows across shelves stacked with industrial solvent and mop heads that hung like heavy, graying hair. This was my "Off-Grid Workspace," a tactical node where the hospital's pervasive surveillance wards couldn't penetrate the thick layer of architectural apathy. The Ministry didn't waste mana monitoring the broom closets. They saved the bandwidth for the paying customers.

I eased myself onto an overturned plastic bucket, the cold, rigid rim biting into my thighs through my thin slacks. The moment I sat, the adrenaline from the alley fight finally evaporated, leaving nothing but the wreckage essential for the systems check.

A wave of nauseating agony hit me with the force of a physical blow. My vision blurred at the edges, the yellowed wallpaper of the closet seeming to pulse in time with the throb behind my eyes. The air felt scrubbed of oxygen as a white-hot spike of pain through my cracked ribs forced a shallow expansion. I hitched, a wet rattle sounding deep in my chest.

Damage Control, my mind whispered, slipping instinctively into the clinical detachment of a triage doctor. Assess and stabilize.

The metallic ghost of blood filled my saliva—a mineral reminder of the internal damage I had been ignoring for the last hour. My hands trembled as I reached for the hem of my shirt. I pulled it up over my head, the fabric peeling away from the dried blood on my side with a sickening, sticky sound. Internal logistics failed; I retched.

I stared down at my torso.

I stared at the deep purples and mottled greens spreading across my ribs. Pain wasn't the metric; only the delay mattered. [Administrative Delay] resolved into a single, jagged directive.

Lagging Asset, I thought, tracing the edge of the worst bruise with a shaking finger. The heat radiating from the skin felt feverish, like my body was overclocking itself to death.

I forced my breathing to slow, counting the seconds between inhalations until the frantic rattling in my chest settled into a dull, rhythmic thrum. I stared at a bottle of floor wax on the shelf opposite me, analyzing the label with uniform focus. I needed to reset. I needed to stabilize before the morning shift began their sweep. Use of unauthorized healing magic in a non-clinical zone was a Class III infraction. Being caught bleeding to death in a janitor's closet was a one-way ticket to the asylum.

The hum of the bulb was an off-key B-flat; slightly sharp. A dirty note for a dirty room.

Focus, I told myself. You aren't dead yet. Don't let the paperwork file itself.

I'd been doing this since Mom died—translating horror into quest text. Clinical distance left my hands steady enough to patch myself.

Scene 25 (Smuggled Spells)

My fingers found the hidden seam in my sleeve—the one I'd sewn myself during a particularly paranoid semester—and retrieved the small glass vial I'd been saving for exactly this kind of catastrophic failure.

The salts inside shimmered with a sickly, artificial green, nothing like the warm amber glow of Ministry-approved regeneratives. Black-market goods. "Smuggled Content," as the enforcement briefs called it. A Tier II medical charm the Academy had deprecated three years ago when an intern used it wrong and liquefied her own kneecap. The glass clinked against my thumbnail—a small, sharp sound that felt obscenely loud in the silence of the closet.

I uncorked the vial. A faint wisp of scorched-sugar magic wafted out. It caught in my throat and made me cough. The cough sent another spike of agony through my ribs, and I had to grip the edge of the shelf to keep from toppling off the bucket.

This is going to hurt. This is going to hurt more than the beating did.

I raised my index finger, the skin still stained with grime and dried blood, and traced the Osteo-Knit sigil in the stale air. The magic responded with a reluctant, static-laden hiss—a faint trail of pixelated green light following my movement like a low-resolution graphic struggling to render. A tingle of thaumion depletion prickled my fingertips—a dry, electric sensation that told me the room's ambient mana was dangerously thin. The closet had been built to be invisible to magic. It was also starving for it.

The sigil hung there for a heartbeat, flickering and unstable, a broken piece of code waiting to compile. I poured my intent into the shape—heal, knit, bind—willing the messy, entropic energy to cohere into something servicable.

Then I slammed my palm into the center of it.

The green flare hit my side like a soldering iron kissing bare skin. The magic didn't enter smoothly—it punched through, a pixelated surge of energy that lacked the resolution and grace of high-tier spellwork. I heard the wet, sickening snap-click of bone being forced back into alignment by crude, magical stapling. My own bone. My own meat.

Fire raced across my nerves. I clamped my teeth shut to keep from screaming, but a strangled sound escaped anyway—something between a growl and a sob. The entropy-scars on my forearms flared violet. Lines of light traced the old wounds like fault lines. I could feel tiny capillaries rupturing under my skin from the pressure differential, bruises blooming even as the ribs fused.

The debt carved itself deeper, I thought, the words surfacing through the haze of pain. Seventy-two hours just became sixty-eight. Maybe sixty-five. The universe charges interest.

The smell of singed hair filled my nostrils—my own, burning where the sigil had flared too close to skin. Underneath it, the mineral bite of internal bleeding finally slowing, clotting, surrendering to the crude repair.

Then a cold gravity settled in my gut.

The sudden, violent restoration of the bone triggered a gag reflex I couldn't suppress. I lurched off the bucket and stumbled to the mop sink, my boots splashing through a puddle of dirty water as I retched into the stained porcelain basin. Nothing came up—I hadn't eaten in eighteen hours—but my body didn't care. It heaved anyway, rejecting the unnatural speed of the knitting flesh.

The taste of bitter gall and copper filled my mouth. A physical backwash. The magic's entropic cost, extracted in bile and trembling muscles.

I hung over the sink, chest heaving, arms shaking where they gripped the cold porcelain. The green light in the closet slowly faded back into the oppressive shadows. The pain was muted now—replaced by a dull, throbbing ache and the unsettling sensation of bone that had been fused too fast, tissue that had been forced to cooperate.

Vertical, operational. Brittle, but the hardware held.

Sufficient, my mind echoed. For now.

I straightened slowly, testing the repair. My ribs held. The motion didn't trigger the stabbing agony from before—just a stiff, tight pressure, like someone had wrapped my torso in invisible bandages two sizes too small. An ugly patch job. A biological hot-fix that would probably give me arthritis in ten years, if I lived that long.

Good enough.

Scene 26 (The Seventy-Two-Hour Sentence)

I stared at the empty vial in my hand, the glass now clear and lifeless. The salts were gone, consumed by the spell, leaving only a residue of fine, lifeless ash. I ground the vial under my boot, crushing it into the linoleum until it sparkled like the diamond dust in fairy tales—the kind of sparkle that belonged in children's stories, not janitor's closets hiding illegal spellwork.

But the repair was only the hardware. The software—my mind, my soul—they kept...they just kept...

God...My hands won't stop shaking.

I checked my wrist. No watch. I had pawned it three weeks ago for a half-gram of mana-stabilizer that turned out to be sawdust and food coloring. I checked the wall clock, but the hands were frozen at 3:00 AM, likely broken for years. The "Administrative Delay" extended even to the measurement of time in this wing.

I needed to move. But first, I needed to verify the integrity of the patch.

I twisted my torso, testing the range of motion. The ribs held. No pain, only a stiff, tight sensation where the magic had fused the bone. It felt functional. Brittle, but functional.

Acceptable, I thought. Optimization within expected parameters.

I reached into my pocket again. This time, my fingers brushed against something cold and metallic. The pager. It sat there like a dormant grenade against my hip. I hadn't looked at it since the ambulance ride last night. I knew it was buzzing—I could feel the phantom vibration against my thigh even when it was still—but I had kept it in "Silent Mode," postponing the reality of the incoming data stream.

I pulled it out. The screen was cracked, a spiderweb fracture running diagonally across the LCD display. I pressed the button.

The backlight flickered, then stabilized. A single message waited for me.

STATUS UPDATE: S. 881-R

I stared at the alphanumeric code. Subject 881-R. That was my father. Not Dinesh Rao. Not "Papa." Just a number in a database, a row in a spreadsheet that had been flagged for review. The sanitization of his identity made my stomach clench.

I pressed 'Read'.

The text scrolled across the small, low-resolution screen in blocky, red letters.

TRANSFER AUTHORIZED. 72-HOUR NOTICE. DESTINATION: MUNICIPAL STORAGE.

I didn't breathe. I didn't blink. I simply read the words again; I parsed the syntax and analyzed the variables. "Municipal Storage." A polite fiction for the mass graves. The system didn't kill the poor; it stored them. In ash jars.

The backwash rose in my throat again, but I swallowed it down. My heart was a high-RPM engine tearing itself loose from the mounts. Inefficient. A "Runtime Error" in a system that couldn't afford a crash.

Runtime Error. The label slid off like oil on water.

The pager's weight in my hand felt like a stone. Like a verdict. Like the exact moment I'd been running from since Mom's vitals went flat and I stood in a hospital corridor just like this one, twelve years old while the machines fell silent.

Papa.

The word surfaced before I could stop it. Not "Subject 881-R." Not "the patient." Papa. The man who read me the Mahabharata in bed until I fell asleep. The man who taught me to play chess with pieces he'd carved from soap. The man who was being erased from existence because I couldn't afford to keep him breathing.

My hands began to shake. The pager slipped from my fingers and it clattered against the bucket with a crack that sounded like a gunshot in the small room. I lunged for it—stupid, automatic—and the motion sent a fresh spike of pain through my barely-healed ribs.

I stopped.

I stood there in the dark, breathing like I'd sprinted a marathon, gripping the edge of the mop sink with fingers that wouldn't stay steady. A sound escaped my throat—raw, wet, and too close to a sob for comfort.

Stop, I commanded myself. Stop. You don't have time for this.

I forced the firewall back up. Made myself pick up the pager. Made myself read the message again, letter by letter, until the words became data instead of a death warrant.

72 Hours.

I wasn't a doctor looking at a patient's chart. I was a son looking at his father's death warrant. And I had less than three days to do something about it.

I gripped the pager until my knuckles turned white. The plastic casing creaked, a sharp snap echoing in the small room as a piece of the housing gave way under the pressure.

72 Hours.

No Timeline. Just a sentence.

I opened my eyes. My workspace was both a refuge and a cage. I stood up, my boots slipping slightly on the wet floor where I had retched. The newly-knit ribs groaned with a dull pressure.

I reached for the door handle. It was cold steel, sterile and unyielding.

Mandatory Side-Quest. The label floated up automatically, my brain trying to process horror through game mechanics like it always did. But the words felt hollow. This was a countdown to ash.

I accepted the terms and the ticking clock. The game was on.

I pushed the door open and stepped out into the hallway.

Scene 27 (The Maze)

I stepped out of the janitor's closet and the world tilted.

The transition was violent—from the confined, chemical darkness to the blinding, sterile exposure of the fourth-floor corridor. The fluorescent lights overhead flickered in a staccato rhythm that synced with the throbbing in my ribs. Zzt-click. Zzt-click.

I adjusted my jacket, hiding the freshly mended ribs, and tried to orient myself. It should have been simple. Turn left. Walk fifty meters. Elevators.

But the hallway didn't look right. It stretched out, elongated and warping like a lens flare, the beige tiles repeating in an infinite, nauseating loop. "Hostile Architecture" wasn't just a theory in a textbook anymore; it was a weapon. No windows. Lights spaced chaotically. Endless sameness—designed to make me feel small. Disoriented. A rat in a maze that had no exit.

I stumbled, my shoulder hitting the wall hard enough to send a jolt of pain through my chest.

Focus, I commanded. Map the grid.

I forced my eyes to track the floor numbers. Room 402. Room 404... No, that was backwards. I spun around. Room 410.

My breath hitched. I was turned around. I, who had walked these halls for three years, who knew the quiet spots and the blind corners, was lost in a straight line.

The rhythmic hiss-click of distant ventilators echoed down the hall, a mechanical heartbeat that seemed to mock the fragility of the human lives it was sustaining.

I forced myself to walk. I kept my head down, staring at the scuff marks on the linoleum. If I looked up, the "Systemic Indifference" baked into the drywall felt heavy enough to crush me. Every flickering light was an eye. Every hum of the air circulation was a whisper.

You are not meant to leave.

I passed the nurse's station. A pool of brighter light in the dim river. A nurse sat there, typing, the blue glow of the screen illuminating her face. I didn't know her. She didn't look up. To her, I was just a shadow. A "Phase-Shifted" ghost haunting the edges of her shift.

I kept moving, my boots silent on the wax. I needed a corner. A shadow. Somewhere to stop shaking.

Scene 28 (Connection Terminated)

I found an alcove near the freight elevators—a dead zone in the surveillance grid where the cameras had blind spots. I slid down the wall until I hit the cold metal of a bench, the shadows clinging to me like a second skin.

I pulled my phone from my pocket. 12% battery. The screen cast a sickly, pale light on my hands. They looked like strangers' hands—bruised, trembling, stained with the grime of the closet.

Execute Communication Protocol.

I dialed. Sarah Vance. We'd shared coffee, notes, and burnout for two years. She'd covered my shifts when Mom died. She was safe.

Ring... Ring...

"Hello?"

Her voice was tight. Guarded.

"Sarah," I whispered. "It's me. I need—"

"Bodhi?" The whisper was sharp, panicked. "Oh god. You shouldn't be calling."

"I know. I just need a signature. A referral. Anything to delay the transfer. They're moving him in seventy-two hours."

"Bodhi? Oh god, stop. You shouldn't be calling. The word in the cafeteria this morning—the Ethics Board is tearing through your files. They’ve already pulled Hartnell in for a 'review.' They aren't just firing you, Bodhi. They're erasing you."

The silence on the line was thick, heavy with fear.

"Sarah, please."

"I can't," she breathed. "I have loans, Bodhi. I have a residency. If they see this call..."

Silence. I could hear her breathing on the other end—shallow, ragged, someone drowning in real-time.

"Sarah—"

"I'm sorry." The words came out broken, barely audible—a confession, not an apology. I heard her swallow, a dry, clicky sound of someone whose mouth had gone completely dust-dry from fear. Then her voice hardened, the administrative firewall slamming down. "Don't call this number again."

The line went dead before I could respond.

I stared at the screen. "Call Ended." The click was an amputation.

I tried Dr. Hartnell next.

Beep. Beep. Beep. "The number you have reached is no longer in service."

I lowered the phone. My ears began to buzz, a high-pitched static that drowned out the hospital hum. I was "Searchable" to the system, but invisible to everyone who'd ever bought me coffee. The "F*** Magic" manifesto hadn't just made me a hero to the undercity; it had made me a viral contaminant to everyone I knew. The chemical rasp of a high-voltage discharge from the spell-work still lingered in the back of my throat.

I felt the weight of the silence in the alcove pressing down on me. I was utterly and completely alone in a city of twelve million souls.

I didn't try anyone else. I knew the answer.

The link was severed. Clean. Total.

I let the phone drop to my lap. Lungs burning, I forced a shallow expansion. The floor wax smelled like a funeral parlor here. A clean, sterile scent that marked the end of my attempt to find a legitimate way out.

Scene 29 (The Butcher Shop)

I sat there for a long time. The "Administrative Closure" was complete. The legitimate paths were closed.

I picked up the phone again. 4% battery. A dying ember.

I opened the browser. My fingers moved automatically, typing a string of characters that didn't belong in a civilized URL. A backend IP address. A "Black-Mesh" node I'd found years ago and prayed I'd never use.

Accessing Repository: Organ_Liquidation_Protocols.

The screen flickered. A raw, textual void replaced the clean white of the Ministry web. Green code on black.

I wasn't searching for a cure anymore. I was searching for a buyer.

The list populated. It was a digital butcher shop.

Kidney (Type O): 4,000 Credits.
Cornea (Pair, unscarred): 2,500 Credits.
Bone Marrow (per liter): 6,000 Credits.

I scrolled. A cold gravity settled in my gut; internal logistics failed as I swallowed back the acid. This wasn't data. This was meat.

Liver Section (regenerative grade): 12,000 Credits.
Heart (In Commission): 45,000 Credits. [Requires certified extraction; mortality rate 100%]

My vision swam. I forced myself to do the math.

My kidney: 4,000. My marrow: maybe 6,000. If I strip myself to the frame...

I looked at my hands. I imagined them on a tray. I imagined my father's hands on a tray.

New Canaan Hospice Debt: 78,000 Credits.

I let out a sound that was half-laugh, half-sob. Even if I butchered us both, even if I sold every scrap of biological material we possessed, we were insolvent. We were trash.

A notification popped up.

WARNING: Unsecured Connection Detected. Tracing... 24%...

The counterspell crawlers.

I slammed the phone shut, my hands shaking so hard I almost dropped it. The snap of plastic echoed in the silent alcove like a gunshot.

I shoved the device into my pocket, my heart hammering against my ribs. I felt cold. Freezing. Converting human life into credits wasn't just illegal; it was damning. I had looked at my father and seen inventory.

I stood up, swaying slightly. I had to get out of the alcove.

A janitor passed me in the corridor—older, gray-haired, pushing a mop bucket that squeaked. He glanced up. Didn't speak. But for just a second, he saw me. Not a viral contaminant. Not a rogue asset. Just a kid shaking apart in a hallway.

He nodded once. A small, tight gesture of solidarity. Then he was gone.

I watched him go. The nod anchored me. It reminded me that there was a world beneath the clean, sterile lies of the hospital.

And in that world, there was one name left.

Marco Cavaretta.

The Cavaretta Club. The speakeasy in the textile district.

A suicide note, not a lifeline. But at this point, the distinction was academic.

Scene 30 (The Firewall with a Perm)

I wasn't ready to sign the suicide note just yet. I had to verify the legitimate path was closed—completely, irrevocably closed.

Billing was three floors down and across the atrium—close enough to hear the intake lobby's chaos, far enough to pretend it wasn't happening. The department was a glass-walled enclosure in the center of the floor, designed to look transparent but built to be impenetrable.

Inside sat Mrs. Gable. I'd dealt with her before, during Mom's final weeks. She wasn't human; she was a "Firewall with a Perm"—that's what the residents called her, anyway. She had sat at that desk for twenty years, denying coverage with the same flat, bored affect she used to order lunch.

A small photograph sat on her desk, slightly faded: two children in soccer uniforms, grinning at the camera. Grandchildren, probably. The frame was cheap plastic, the kind you got at a dollar store, but she'd placed it where she could see it while she worked. Where she could glance at it between rejections.

I approached the sliding glass window. It was closed. I knocked on the glass, a sharp, insistent rap that hurt my bruised knuckles.

Mrs. Gable didn't look up. She was typing. Her fingers attacked the keyboard with percussive violence. Clack-clack-clack-ENTER. Another claim denied. Another life rounded down to zero.

"Mrs. Gable, about Subject 881-R—"

"Visiting hours are concluded," she said, her voice as flat as a PDF. Clack-clack-clack-ENTER. "Outstanding balance on your 02:00 AM admission remains unpaid. I cannot open the file for 881-R while your own account is in arrears."

"It's my father. He's being stored—"

"He is being transferred to Municipal Storage," she corrected, finally looking at me with the eyes of someone who had seen a thousand fathers become ash. "Protocol for non-payment is non-negotiable. We have... obligations."

She didn't say 'shareholders.' She didn't need to. The word hung in the air anyway.

"I can pay," I lied. The words tasted like bile.

Mrs. Gable put her glasses back on. She tapped a few keys, checking the ledger of my failures. "Your credit score is 412. Your assets are frozen pending the tribunal hearing. And..." she squinted at the screen, and I swore I saw her wince. "You are currently listed as 'Unemployed/High Risk.' You couldn't secure a loan for a sandwich, let alone a Level 4 Hospice Transfer."

"I have skills," I said, my voice rising. Desperation was cracking my calm. I leaned in close to the speaking slot; I breathed the stale, recycled air of the lobby into her sanctum. "I'm a Class IV Healer. I was a Doctor of the Guild. Twelve hours ago. Now I'm Persona Non Grata in a hospital I used to call mine—but I can still work. Put me on the graveyard shift. I'll scrub the bedpans. I'll triage the overdose cases in the waiting room. Just... delay the transfer."

Mrs. Gable looked at me then. Really looked at me. For a second, I saw something human behind the lenses—not pity, not disgust, but something worse. Something like understanding. Like she'd seen this exact scene play out a thousand times and knew exactly how it would end.

Her gaze flicked to the photograph of her grandchildren. Then back to me.

She didn't look at me. She acknowledged the photograph for a microsecond—her internal validation check—before she brought the stamp down.

"For what it's worth, Mr. Rao…I'm sorry it has to be this way." Her voice caught in her throat midway through the sentence.

THUD.

Or maybe I had just imagined it.

REJECTED. The red ink looked like blood.

"Next," she said, looking past me to the empty lobby. Her voice was flat again. Professional. The glimpse of humanity sealed back behind the glass.

I stood there for a long moment, my breath fogging the window. I had thrown my pride on the counter, and she had filed it under 'Insufficient Assets'.

The horror lay in her lack of malice; she was just doing her job, a small, functioning cog in a machine that never stopped eating—and somewhere in her, she knew it.

I turned and walked away. The sound of her typing followed me, relentless as a heartbeat.

Scene 31 (Mirror of Resolve)

I stopped in front of a flickering vending machine in the hallway. The dark, polished glass acted as a crude mirror, reflecting the wreckage of the night.

I saw myself for the first time since the fight: my face was a mask of dried blood and deep, purple contusions, my eyes hollowed out by exhaustion and fear. I looked like a ghost haunting my own life, a "Subject" that the world had already begun to discard.

I leaned in closer. The low-frequency hum of the machine vibrated through the glass and into my forehead, a steady, mechanical pulse that grounded me in the cold reality of failure.

I stared through my own reflection, and for a terrifying second, I didn't see myself. I saw my father.

The same sharp angles of the jaw, the same weary set of the shoulders. I looked like a man who had been "Depreciated" by the system. A "Legacy Asset" running on expired code.

I am becoming him. The loop is closing.

The coil had turned, but the bag hadn't dropped. It was the perfect microcosm of the City. You paid. You followed the prompt. The machine ate your credit anyway. The loop wasn't just closing; it was strangling me.

I felt a sudden, sharp surge of heat in my chest—a spark of entropic energy that burned through the cold fog of my despair. My hands curled into fists, the knuckles turning white against the dark glass.

The flickering red "Sold Out" sign reflected in my eyes looked like a spark of fire.

I pulled my forehead away from the glass. The cold spot on my skin vanished, replaced by a feverish heat. I stared directly into my own eyes, no longer finding a victim. There was no more room for the system's rules or the Guild's ethics. If the architecture was built to keep me out, I'd find the fault lines and tear it open.

If the legitimate paths were closed, I would build a new one out of the wreckage.

"Fine," I whispered to the reflection. The voice wasn't a rasp anymore. It was a command. "We do it the hard way."

Scene 31.5 (The Ransom of Reality)

My phone buzzed against my hip. A death rattle vibration. 2% battery. A priority override notification pulsed through my cracked display, bypassing every firewall I'd built.

The vending machine's fluorescent tube stuttered. My reflection fractured into strobe-light ghosts.

No sender ID. No number. Just a sigil rendered in bleeding pixels: the Cavaretta crest.

The crest brought it all back. Eighteen months collapsed into a single, sour taste at the back of my throat.

The Cavaretta Famiglia. Calabrian blood, old money, and a network that stretched from Naples to New Canaan. They'd never produced a single mage in their entire lineage—a fact that burned Marco Cavaretta like acid—but what they lacked in magical talent, they compensated for in leverage. Financial reach. Political muscle. The kind of pull that could bend the rules governing who deserved care and who deserved to rot.

I'd first walked into their offices sixteen months ago. Desperate. Stupid. My father's diagnosis had been accelerating for years, but the final prognosis hit like a hammer wrapped in legal documents: Terminal cognitive decline. Six months without intervention. Eighteen with standard Mundane palliative care.

Standard Mundane care. As if Dinesh Rao were just another failing body in a failing system.

He'd spent thirty years as a civil engineer. Built bridges. Designed infrastructure. And when the Federation decided New Canaan needed power that didn't rely on coal or nuclear, my father had been the one to oversee the installation of the Artificial Ley Lines that now hummed beneath every hospital, every Academy building, every magical institution in the city.

Dinesh Rao had literally wired the magical grid that kept New Canaan alive.

But when his mind started to go? When the plaques and tangles began chewing through the neurons that remembered my mother's face, my childhood, the lyrics to stupid Bollywood songs he used to sing while making chapati?

The Ministry told him to apply through standard channels. The Academy's Ethics Board reviewed his case and concluded that—despite his "proximity to magical infrastructure"—he remained a Mundane. Ineligible for in-patient magical care. The Ley Lines he'd installed could heal a hundred mages a day, but they couldn't spare a single joule for the man who'd put them there.

That was the system. Clean. Efficient. Brutally logical.

Marco Cavaretta had offered an alternative.

"We have pull, Dr. Rao...sorry, mister Rao," he'd said, sitting across from me in a leather chair that probably cost more than my annual salary. "The Federation owes us favors. The Hospice Director owes us more. Your father will receive Class III Magical Care—the finest available to any mundane in North America. And in exchange..."

In exchange, I became the Cavaretta Family physician.

The deal seemed almost fair, at first. My unique skillset—the Entropic Manipulation techniques I'd inherited from my mother's research, the oversized magical circuits I'd been born with, the scalpel-precision I'd honed through years of obsessive practice—all of it made me one of the few Clerics in the Western Hemisphere capable of performing Class IV healing without a support team.

I could do the work of five healers, working alone. Marco needed that.

Don Vincenzo Cavaretta, his father, had been dying for years. A magical wasting disease that no amount of mundane medicine could touch and no ordinary Cleric could reverse. I became his personal life-support system. Every week, I'd make the drive to the Cavaretta estate. Every week, I'd pour my mana into the old man's collapsing body, buying him another seven days.

Every week, the interest on my debt grew.

Marco had structured the loan like a predatory mortgage. Variable rates tied to my "performance metrics." Bonus penalties for any decline in Vincenzo's condition—as if I could outrun entropy itself. Compounding interest that made the original sum feel like a rounding error.

I'd started working for the Cavarettas to save my father.

Sixteen months later, I owed them more than I'd ever be able to repay.

Of course. Marco had been circling for weeks. Breadcrumbs in my voicemail, "opportunities" slipped through intermediaries. I'd ignored every one. The universe had a sick sense of timing.

I tapped the screen.

Raw video feed. Uncompressed. Marble floors, medical equipment worth more than my father's entire care plan, a bed like a sarcophagus.

Don Vincenzo Cavaretta lay in the center, comatose. His chest rose and fell with mechanical rhythm—a man whose body hadn't received the death memo. On his chest, resting like a tumor made of light, sat a sliver of something old.

The Shard.

I'd heard whispers about objects like this—fragments of power that didn't obey natural physics.

"Calcified mana," the black-market dealers called them. A lie for people who didn't want to know they were holding pieces of reality's source code.

The Shard pulsed with an entropic frequency that registered in the Class I range—Creation, Destruction, the kind of power that rewrote history. Marco was holding a fragment of the most powerful magical artifact in the world, and he had no idea he was playing with a nuclear warhead.

Digital ghosts crawled across the pixels. Tengura semagrams—a language older than the Academy—flickered in the distortion.

WE SEE YOU TOO, HEALER.

Marco's voice cut through, smooth as oil on water.

"We're both out of time, Bodhi," Marco said, his voice stripped of its usual mob-boss polish. "Fix my father or lose yours. Take the job, and the Cavaretta Famiglia no longer owns a single breath of your father's life."

The video feed cut. My phone's screen died with a pathetic whimper. 1% battery.

I stared at the dark glass of the vending machine. My reflection stared back—hollow, battered, calculating.

The Cavarettas had put my father in this bed. Their "pull" got him admitted when the Ministry would have left him to rot. Their interest rates bled me dry ever since. Working for Marco meant walking into the den of the monster that had been slowly digesting my family for years.

And Marco's jealousy made everything worse. He'd spent his entire life watching others wield the power he could only purchase. His own son, Bianco, represented everything Marco craved and would never possess—a natural sorcerer, born with an Innate Personal Magic the catalogues called "The Domain of Fear." Even worse, Don Vincenzo had been grooming Bianco as his successor. The old man saw his grandson's magical talent as the key to elevating the Cavaretta Famiglia from mere brokers to true power players.

Marco was being bypassed. His own father preferred the grandson over the son.

That kind of resentment didn't stay contained. It leaked into every deal, every negotiation, every predatory interest rate adjustment. Marco didn't just want Vincenzo saved—he wanted to prove that he was the one who made it happen. That his connections, his resources, his dealings brought the Famiglia back from the brink.

I was a tool in Marco's succession war. Nothing more.

But the Shard could remove my physical limiter. And a Class V ritual could do more than just restore a comatose mobster.

The variables aligned into a single, jagged intent. Sharp. Dangerous. Entirely my own.

The ritual architecture had gaps. Missing anchors that Marco couldn't provide and didn't understand. But I understood. I'd spent years working with my mother's research on soul-binding and entropic inversion. I knew where those gaps were—and I knew what could fill them.

Mom's ring sat heavy in my pocket. A piece of her, condensed into a loop of gold.

The ring could act as a soul-anchor. Imperfect, improvised, but adequate. A tether binding a fragment of Dinesh Rao's consciousness to a physical object—exactly the kind of component Marco's ritual was missing.

If I slipped it into the ritual circle, no one would notice. Marco didn't understand the technical architecture. Bianco was still learning the basics. The Shard's overwhelming power would mask the additional variable.

And when the Class V inversion peaked, I could redirect the flow. Use the Stone's raw processing power to run two resurrection threads instead of one. Vincenzo would get his revival—Marco would get his trophy. But the same reality-patch would also restore Dinesh Rao. Two resurrections for the price of one betrayal.

The math worked. Barely. The Shard had enough power left to fuel both operations if I timed the execution correctly. One distributed computing job split across two targets.

I'd be stealing processing power from a Divine Artifact that the entire magical world had been denied for nineteen centuries—an artifact hoarded by emperors and cardinals while people like my father died waiting for care they were told they didn't deserve.

The system had failed him. The system had failed us both.

If the people who controlled the Stone refused to use it for its intended purpose, then maybe it was time someone else did.

My mother would have called me reckless. The Academy would have called me a criminal. The Federation would have called me a traitor to everything medical ethics stood for.

But my mother was dead. The Academy had classified her research as "unstable" and buried it. And the Federation had already decided that Dinesh Rao didn't deserve to live.

I'd spent sixteen months following the rules. Paying the interest. Performing the weekly healings. Playing the good contract worker.

The rules had lived me dry.

I slipped the dead phone into my pocket. The "hard way" had just become specific.
