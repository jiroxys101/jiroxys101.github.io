Chapter 10: Ultimatum at Midnight

Scene 56

The taxi driver's knuckles were white against the artificial leather of the steering wheel, his breathing shallow and rhythmic in the stale, recycled air of the cabin. He stopped the car three hundred yards before the gates.

"This is it," he muttered, his eyes fixed on the rearview mirror rather than the road ahead. "I don't go up the drive. Rules."

"Rules, or a sense of self-preservation?" I asked, pulling a crumpled ten-lumen note from my pocket.

He didn't answer. He just waited, the engine idling with a hesitant, metallic rattle that betrayed his urge to shift into reverse and floor it back toward New Canaan. I stepped out, and the door's slam was swallowed instantly by the heavy, unnatural silence of the hill.

I stood within the "Fear Radius"—the perimeter of a subsonic repulsion field designed to tickle the primate brain's amygdala. It wasn't magic you could see, but you could feel it in the way the local squirrels didn't chatter and the wind seemed to detour around the estate's boundaries. It was a localized brown-out of life.

I paid the driver. He didn't wait for the change. The taxi’s tires spat Venetian gravel as he executed a violent U-turn, his exhaust smelling of cheap fuel and desperation.

Then there was only the mist.

The Cavaretta Estate loomed over the landscape, a Victorian tumor of blackened brick and choking ivy that metastasized from the damp, grey earth. It didn't belong to the topography; it was an infection the land was too weak to purge. Every window was a cataract, every chimney a soot-stained vent for a house that breathed in secrets and exhaled rot.

I started the climb. My boots crunched onto the imported gravel—a percussive, aggressive sound in the stillness. It was too loud, a broadcast of my identity. Bodhi Rao is here. The student who’d failed his finals. The back-alley medic with the illegal licenses. The man who’d seen the source code of death and decided he could patch it.

I wasn't just a "Specialist." I was a variable the Cavarettas thought they could solve.

November mist clung to the grounds like gauze on a festering wound. It didn't just dampen the sound; it prioritized the smell. It was a thick, cloying cocktail—the scent of money sweating out its toxins. It smelled of rotting orchids, expensive cigars, and the metallic tang of old blood. A boutique abattoir for the gentry, where the slaughter was done with silk ribbons instead of hooks.

I adjusted my collar, a useless gesture against the chill radiating from the obsidian ring pressed against my sternum.

The ring.

It felt heavy enough to bruise bone—neutron star density packed into a loop that should have weighed mere ounces. It pulsed against my skin, a hot, scorching rhythm—a vibration that settle into my intercostal muscles and made my lungs feel tight. Thum-thum... thum. It wasn't a heartbeat. It was a signal struggling to find a carrier wave in a world that was already de-rendering.

Diagnostic scan, I told myself, slipping into the clinical detachment that was my only armor. Assess the patient. Ignore the prognosis.

I looked at the house. My diagnostic sight—the Eye of Ajna—flickered open without a conscious command. The world desaturated. The brickwork faded to grey wireframes, and the mist dissolved into shifting streams of data.

The estate was screaming.

Thaumic wards wrapped the structure in layers of suffocating complexity. I saw them as "Spaghetti Code" of the worst kind—ancient Roman protection circles spliced with modern security glyphs, a sloppy, brute-force architecture that leaked mana like a septic wound. The ley-lines were jagged, gangrenous things, pulsing with a sickly green light where the ward-integrity was failing. It was generations of paranoid sorcerers patching over each other’s work until the original intent was buried under centuries of cruft.

"Amateurs," I whispered, the word smoking in the freezing air.

The Ouroboros—the parasite I hosted—stirred in my marrow, distinct and hostile against any spell I’d ever prepared. A subsonic vibration rattled my teeth, a hunger waiting for a menu. It sensed the leakage in the wards. It wanted to lick the mana-bleed.

As I crossed the threshold of the primary wards, the pressure against my chest spiked. The ring inhaled. The obsidian surface became a "Mana Sink" that swallowed the frequency of the barrier like a vacuum. The wards didn't flicker; they just dimmed, a localized brown-out in the magical architecture centered entirely on my sternum.

Easy, I thought at the snake, and the stone. Dinner is coming. Don't spoil your appetite.

I walked toward the mahogany double doors, my hands thrust deep into my pockets to hide the tremor. I wasn't shaking from fear. I was shaking because I was holding a grenade with the pin pulled, and the explosion was the only exit strategy I had left.

The doors swung inward before I could knock.

Bianco Cavaretta stood in the violently lit foyer, a silhouette of erratic motion. He’d shrunk since I last saw him, his expensive suit now swallowing his frame like a shroud. His eyes were wide, the pupils dilated until they were just black pits rimmed with the red exhaustion of a man who sleeps with one eye on the exit.

"You came," he whispered. His voice was a thread, caught in the high-frequency tremor of a prey animal nearing the killing floor.

I stepped across the threshold, the air in the foyer hitting me with the cloying heat of a hothouse. "I'm a man of my word, Bianco. Even when the word is a lie."

He flinched, his hands fumbling with the expensive cufflink on his left wrist—a nervous, repetitive tic. He glanced toward the east wing where voices—loud, confident, dangerous—echoed off the vaulted ceiling.

"My uncle... he thinks you're a specialist. A 'Containment Architect' from the Vatican underground."

"I know what he thinks," I said, my voice flat, my eyes already scanning the foyer for exits. "I wrote the script. I'm just here to deliver the performance."

Bianco swallowed, his Adam’s apple bobbing in his throat. He looked at my chest, his gaze drawn by the invisible gravity of the ring hidden beneath my robe. For a second, his terror broke, replaced by a jagged confusion. "You feel... wrong. Bodhi, the air around you is... heavy. Like you're dragging the whole world behind you."

"That's just the luggage," I said, brushing past him. The fear-sweat coming off him was sharp, cutting through his expensive cologne. "Take me to the prep room. Before I lose my nerve."

Scene 57

Bianco led me through a side corridor that smelled of formaldehyde and lavender—the specific, nauseating perfume of a funeral home trying to pass as a spa. The air was thick, a greasy condensation clinging to the walls and my skin, making the heavy silk of the robe I hadn't yet put on feel like it was already damp with sweat.

The "Preparation Anteroom" was a glorified pantry, its shelves lined not with food for the living but with snacks for the dead. Glass jars filled with amber fluid held things that might have been organs, or might have been alchemical mistakes. I saw a preserved hand with six fingers, its nails still growing against the glass; a heart that seemed to be made of quartz, pulsing with a slow, crystalline light; and a row of canaries that were motionless but definitely not dead.

The hum of the preservation units was a constant, low-frequency pressure on my skull, a 50Hz drone that vibrated the fluid in the jars.

"He tests on them," Bianco said, catching my stare. He was still fumbling with his cufflink, the gold glinting in the harsh, sterile light. "The Shard... it hums. It hurts my teeth. It ate the bird last week. It didn't kill it, Bodhi. The bird just... stopped updating. Like a frozen screen."

I froze, my hand halfway to the ceremonial robe draped over a workbench stained with old reagents and dried blood. Stopped updating.

"Show me," I said.

"Glitch," I murmured. The jagged edges of the wings dissolved into tiny, perfect squares of yellow light—render artifacts in the fabric of reality. "The local reality rendering engine can't process the object's state. It’s a buffer overflow in the physics engine."

Bianco looked at me like I was speaking Aramaic, his eyes searching for sense in my jargon. "What?"

"Nothing." I grabbed the robe—heavy black silk embroidered with silver thread that felt greasy, like it had been soaked in rendering-fluid. "Your uncle is playing with a debugger he doesn't understand. He's trying to overclock a system that’s already thermal-throttling."

I stripped off my jacket, folding it neatly on the workbench. I transferred the obsidian ring from my shirt pocket to a hidden fold in the robe's inner lining, placing it directly over my heart.

The ring. Dinesh.

The sensation of touching the obsidian was like touching absolute zero. A burning, friction-free cold that sank deeper than skin, settling into the intercostal muscles like a weighted compress. It felt like mass was being added to the room every time I touched it.

"Why are you helping him?" I asked, watching Bianco’s reflection in the glass of the specimen jar. "You know he’s going to kill Vincenzo. You know this isn't a cure. It's a harvest."

Bianco hunched his shoulders, his frame shrinking under the weight of his legacy. He looked at the floor, his hands twitching against his thighs. "Because he's family. And because... if I don't help, I'm next. The ledger has to be balanced, Bodhi. Usually with someone's life."

"Self-preservation," I nodded, my reflection catching his. A shared glance of recognition—two people trapped in the same gear-train, waiting for the teeth to catch. "The most honest diagnostic there is."

"And you?" Bianco turned, his eyes searching my face for a savior I couldn't afford to be. "Why are you here? You barely know us. You hate magic."

I paused, my thumb tracing the outline of the obsidian ring through the silk. The surface was a hole in the world, a void that drank the light of the room.

Because my father is currently a zip file of soul-data on a hard drive I stole from death, I thought. And your uncle built the only computer powerful enough to unzip him. And because I’m the only one who knows how to crash the system before it finishes the install.

"I'm here for a second opinion," I said aloud, my voice sounding hollow in the small room. "And to make sure no one else gets 'deleted' tonight."

The lie tasted like old copper. I wasn't here to save anyone. I was here to hijack a ritual. If saving Bianco was a byproduct, it was a secondary objective. If not... well, surgical success sometimes requires the sacrifice of the non-essential.

I pulled the hood up, the heavy silk smelling of dust and power. "Let's go. Don't keep the monster waiting."

Scene 58

The drawing room was an arena masked as a lounge.

Three mages stood in a loose semi-circle around the fireplace, their postures stiff, their eyes flicking toward the door as we entered. I clocked them instantly—"Licensed" practitioners. You could tell by the way they held themselves: arrogant but leashed, like predators that had been house-broken by the Empire. They wore the gold-and-crimson seal of the Licensing Council like a badge of honor, unaware that to men like Marco, it was just a barcode.

I named them mentally as I crossed the room. The Silver-haired woman was clearly the 'Corporate Lawyer' type, her mana signature sharp and litigation-focused. Beside her, a man with a grey beard and a constant, academic squint—The Tenured Academic, likely here to justify the ethics of the slaughter. And at the end, a thick-necked bruiser whose magic felt like a blunt instrument—The Hired Muscle.

Marco Cavaretta sat in a wingback chair that looked like a throne, swirling a glass of liquid that was too dark to be wine. He looked up, his face a landscape of sharp, expensive angles and skin treatments that couldn't quite mask the rot underneath.

"The Specialist," Marco said. His voice was a deep, resonant baritone—the kind of voice trained to give orders that ended lives without the bother of a trial. "You're late."

"Precision is the only variable that matters," I said, my voice steady despite the oppressive mana-bleed in the room. "The alignment is delicate. If you want a quick job, you’ll get a localized collapse of reality. If you want the Shard to open, you wait for the Specialist."

Marco smiled, but the expression was a purely muscular contraction, a horizontal slit in his mask that left his gaze untouched. His eyes remained fixated and flat, reflecting the floodlights like obsidian lenses, devoid of the micro-saccades that signal a living empathy.

"I prefer results," he said, gesturing loosely toward the three practitioners. "These are your battery packs. Feed them the primer, and they’ll carry the load."

Marco didn't even blink. He just stared through her with the flat, clinical detachment of a man looking at a used syringe—something necessary for the procedure, but ultimately disposable. "You are what I pay you to be. And tonight, you are input."

I scanned the "input" with a practiced, cynical eye. The Silver-Haired woman was a pure-blood traditionalist, her mana signature a high-frequency violet hum that smelled of overpriced incense and old money. The Academic beside her was a logic-miner, his robe stained with ink and the ozone of a thousand late-night simulations. He radiated a low-level anxiety, a jittery silver field that was dithering at the edges. The Hired Muscle was a brute-force channeler, a man whose frame was too large for his soul, his presence a dull bronze thud in the room's atmosphere. They were a curated collection of specialized power, three distinct frequencies that Marco had bought to weave into his own tapestry of ascent.

I walked past them, my boots clicking on the polished marble as I focused on the holographic schematic floating above the coffee table. It was a projection of the wine cellar below—a complex geometry of shifting light and data that looked more like a CAD drawing for a reactor than a ritual map.

I scanned it with my diagnostic eye.

Energy Input: 4 Sources.
Throughput Capacity: Unlimited.
Filter Protocols: Disabled.
Safety Margins: None.

My skin felt like wet slate. The math was explicitly, aggressively wrong.

"Four sources?" I asked, pointing at the glowing nodes of the schematic. "You have three mages. I’m the Specialist. I’m supposed to anchor the field, not power it."

Marco took a slow sip of his drink, his shark-eyes never leaving mine. "And you, Mr. Rao, are a unique resource. The Specialist must anchor the field, correct? But the anchor is part of the circuit. Connection requires... participation."

I looked at the mages again. They weren't just conduits. They were fuel. And so was I. Marco had no intention of "channeling" the Philosopher's Stone to heal his brother. He wasn't performing a surgery; he was initiating a cold, industrial harvest of human potential. He intended to crack the Shard open and feed it four souls to jumpstart the engine of his own immortality.

"Standard protocol," I lied, keeping my voice as flat as a dial-tone even as my adrenal gland dumped fire into my blood. "But I'll need proximity to the Shard to modulate the frequency. If I'm an input, I need to be at the South point."

"Granted," Marco said, standing up. The air in the room seemed to drop five degrees, the temperature-regulation units in the walls struggling to compensate for the sudden magical pressure. "We go down now. Vincenzo is fading. The cache is almost empty."

I glanced toward the corner, where a medical stretcher sat in the long, flickering shadows. Vincenzo Cavaretta—the excuse for all this madness—was a husk. His skin was the color of old newspaper, papery and thin, barely enough to cover the skeletal architecture of his frame. Each rattling breath was a struggle against the entropy already eating him from the inside out. He wasn't a patient anymore; he was a prop, a placeholder for Marco's ego.

Status: Terminal. The entropy was too advanced, the systemic failure absolute. He was a placeholder, a vessel maintained only long enough to serve a purpose that had nothing to do with healing.

"After you," I said to Marco, my hand pressing against the ring over my heart.

Null Variable, I thought, my mind running the calculation I’d been refining since I first saw the blueprint. That’s the play.

A spinning fractal of violet light began to rotate in the air between me and the Shard, a geometric impossibility that signaled the local reality engine was attempting to reconcile the two incompatible files. The Shard tried to process the new input. It expected pure, compliant mana—the high-octane souls of the licensed mages. It got a complex, encrypted, paradoxical Anomaly wrapped in sixteen hours of Entropic alteration. It got a file format that its divine architecture couldn't parse—a .SOUL file that had been renamed to .VOID and then compressed with a recursive algorithm.

Null_Variable.obj was incompatible with Cavaretta_Ritual.exe.

The pause lasted for a heartbeat that felt like an eternity, the reality of the room holding its breath as the kernel panic began. I saw the air ripple like disturbed water, the lead-plating behind Marco developing a texture-map error, the flat grey surface becoming a sea of flickering horizontal lines.

Then the crash report arrived.

Scene 63

The crash didn't arrive as a sound, but as an architectural failure of reality.

CRACK.

It was a digital, distorted sound—a jagged, square-wave frequency that felt like it was tearing the air molecules apart, a 1-bit audio sample of a universe breaking its own laws. A physical shockwave of rejected data rippled out from the Shard, a vacuum forming in its wake that sucked the remaining oxygen from my lungs. The pressure in the room plummeted to near-zero, the air turning into a thin, freezing mist of aerosolized lead and ozone-scented static.

Then gravity inverted.

The ceiling became the floor. The transition wasn't an instantaneous flip; it was a sickening, elastic stretch of the local coordinates. The marble tiles under my feet didn't just move; they were pulled away as the Shard’s gravity well spasmed, the textures peeling off the stone like wet wallpaper to reveal a flickering wireframe grid beneath. I was thrown upward, slamming into the lead-plated ceiling with a bone-jarring impact that sent white sparks across my vision. I didn't fall; I was repelled by the floor and attracted by the ceiling, a permanent inversion of the cardinal axes.

I looked at the mages.

The silver-haired woman de-rendered completely. She was an architectural error, her frame dissolving into a cloud of red pixels that hung in the air for a fraction of a second, perfectly stationary, before vanishing into the floor. The other two mages were worse—their bodies were being twisted into impossible, non-Euclidean geometries. Their limbs became Möbius strips, skin folding into bone and bone into air as the Shard tried to source more matter to process the Null Variable. It was a wet, crunching sound—the audio of physics being manually overridden by a kernel panic. I saw the Academic’s head rotate 720 degrees on an axis that didn't exist, his face becoming a smear of pink noise before his entire volume was compressed into a point of infinite density and sucked into the black hole.

The wine cellar was no longer a room. It was a "Null Namespace." The walls were rippling like disturbed water, the lead-plating developing low-resolution hills and valleys where the math of the containment field had failed.

Marco’s face was the worst of all. He let out a sound that might have been a question, but his throat was de-rendering, the vowels dissolving into a distorted static that sounded like a file-header being corrupted. He clawed at the air, his fingers flickering into grey wireframes, his mouth opening in a silent, jagged O. His face was distorting, his skin stretching and tearing in ways that were high-contrast and digital. A blue static flicked along his jawline, a "Clipping Error" that allowed me to see the stone floor through his cheek.

I didn't answer. I was too busy watching Bianco.

He was sliding across the ceiling—now the floor—falling toward the Dead Pixel. The Shard’s hunger had turned into a desperate, grasping gravity that was pulling everything in the room toward the black hole. Bianco’s fingers clawed at the lead plating—now a jagged, low-poly surface—his breath coming in high, panicked hitches as he struggled for purchase. He wasn't falling toward the center of the room; he was being pulled toward a "Data Sink" that wanted his mass to balance the error I’d introduced.

He was inches from the edge. I saw the tips of his boots start to pixelate, the expensive leather dithered into grey static as the Shard began to "read" him.

I didn't think. There was no time for calculation, no time to verify the ethics of the next move. In a world that was de-rendering, ethics was just another variable I didn't have the memory to load. I was a medic in a triage theater where the stage itself was collapsing.

The Blood-black Ouroboros lashed out from my shadow—a tendril of absolute negation that didn't grab Bianco, but struck the space between him and the cellar door. The serpent's head was a jagged, low-poly construct of black light, its fangs dripping with the "Void Drip" of an unhandled exception.

Delete coordinates, I commanded, the neural snap of the spell feeling like a whip-crack in my brain.

I didn't teleport him. I simply deleted the distance. I rewrote the local coordinate system to place the value of "Bianco's Position" equal to the value of "Exit Door's Position."

Pop.

Space folded. One second Bianco was falling toward the void; the next, he flickered and reappeared at the top of the cellar stairs, outside the ritual boundary. He tumbled onto the landing, gasping, his eyes wide as he looked back at the chaos. He was safe, but the sight of him—a high-resolution human in a world of wireframes—was a visual headache I couldn't ignore.

"Go!" I wheezed, the command tearing through a throat raw from the vacuum. "The threshold is breaking! Run, Bianco! Don't look back!"

Scene 64

The basement was no longer an architectural space; it was a file being deleted in real-time.

The stone walls behind Marco didn't just crumble; they de-rendered. The textures dropped out first, moving from blackened brick to a uniform, lifeless grey, and then into a glowing wireframe mesh of polygons. The medical stretcher holding Vincenzo flickered like a glitching cathode-ray tube before becoming a transparent, jagged polygon that the Shard inhaled with a high-pitched "hard drive read" squeal. Vincenzo himself—the husk that had been a man—was gone in a heartbeat, his soul-data too thin to resist the vacuum. He didn't die; he was simply "Cleared from RAM."

The air smelled of burning silicon and the absence of everything. It was the scent of a vacuum being filled by the debris of reality.

Marco finally stopped his silent screaming. The power he’d stolen was leaking out of him, useless in a world that had forgotten the protocols for his magic. He looked down at his own hands—now translucent, the resolution dropping until the fingerprints were just jagged aliasing. He was a sector being overwritten in real-time, a memory address being cleared by a system that no longer recognized his credentials.

His skin was a map of "Error: Code 404." The golden light of his stolen mana was sputtering, a blue static flickering along the seams of his silk suit. He was a high-level user who had forgotten the admin password, watching his own permissions being revoked by a higher authority.

"You..." he whispered, his voice a digital artifact, a crackling distortion in the static that sounded like it was being played through a broken speaker at half-speed. "You ruined... everything. The legacy... the ascent... all of it."

I looked at him, my own frame flickering at the edges as the room’s resolution dropped to zero. I didn't feel triumph. I didn't feel the need for a quip. I just felt the exhaustion of a man who’d seen too much source code and found it full of bugs.

"I didn't ruin it, Marco," I said, my voice barely a whisper in the static. I was struggling to maintain the "Bodhi" object in the local namespace. "I just finalized the calculation. The deficit of souls you’ve been accruing has been reconciled. The Shard is closing the account. You tried to build immortality on a hardware failure, and now the system is performing a mandatory reboot."

The Shard let out a final, piercing shriek—the sound of the read-head crashing into the platter at ten thousand RPM. The black void of the Dead Pixel expanded one last time, a terminal "Force Quit" that swallowed the last of the wine cellar.

The Ring—my father—was gone.

I felt the connection sever. It wasn't a magical snap; it was a physical amputation. The weight that had bruised my sternum for days vanished instantly, leaving behind a cold, slick patch of sweat where the obsidian had been—a localized vacuum on my skin that felt more like a wound than an absence. The rhythmic vibration that I’d started to mistake for my own heartbeat was gone, replaced by a silence so profound it was deafening. I felt a phantom weight where the ring had been, a "Ghost Limb" sensation of a soul-data file that had been permanently erased.

I lost him. The truth was cold and final. I’d stopped the monster, I’d saved the kid, but I’d lost the only piece of him I had left. I’d used my father as a malware payload, a sacrificial audit to crash Marco's dream. The clinical detachment I’d used as armor felt like it was melting, the liquid metal of my grief burning through the mesh.

The acute, physical grief of the second loss was a vacuum in my lungs, heavier than the Shard’s gravity. My heart hammered against a chest that felt disturbingly light, a "Null Address" where the Ouroboros usually resided.

But there was no time to mourn the data. The Dead Pixel was no longer a jagged hole. It was a doorway, expanding until it filled my peripheral vision, a white blindness that was devouring the last polygons of the Cavaretta Estate. Macro was gone—not just dead, but deleted. The lead-plating, the mages, the wine—all reclaimed by the void.

I was the only object left in the scene. And the scene was being cleared.

Scene 65

The noise rose past the range of human hearing, becoming a physical pressure that squeezed my eyeballs and made the marrow in my bones vibrate. It was a rhythmic, 120BPM throb—the heartbeat of a dying universe, a "Heartbeat Signal" that had moved from green to red and was now a flickering, terminal violet.

The white-out was total. The "Buffer Underrun" had reached the edges of the local universe, and the system had run out of memory to render the concepts of "Up," "Down," or "Bodhi Rao." I stood my ground on a floor that felt like slush, my nerves screaming for the impact that had to be coming. The amygdala-hijack was absolute, every synapse firing a warning of imminent de-rendering. I felt my skin begin to itch, a thousand tiny needles of static-on-tongue that signaled my own texture-maps were being cleared.

I tensed against the imminent destruction, my jaw locked until my teeth threatened to crack. I was a medical object waiting for the scalpel of the void, a patient on a table that was being moved into the trash bin.

The pressure built. Higher. Harder. It felt like my very atoms were being compressed into a single, high-density line of code, my volume being reduced to save on processing power.

I'm not ready, I thought, the memory of my first surgery—the one I’d "lost" during the extraction—flickering back with a desperate, low-resolution clarity. I haven't balanced the ledger yet.

The world held its breath. A single, frozen frame of absolute, blinding white.

Then it stopped.

The scream didn't end; it just vanished, as if the audio cable had been yanking. The pressure didn't release; it simply ceased to exist, leaving me floating in a space without resistance.

The blindness remained, but the context had changed. I wasn't in a basement anymore. I wasn't anywhere. There was no sound, no heat, no cold. Just an absolute, crushing silence that felt like weightlessness. I was a "Dangling Pointer," an object in memory that no longer pointed to anything real. I reached out a hand and found nothing—not even the air. I tried to speak, but the sound didn't propagate; it was just a silent vibration in my own chest.

I waited for the end. I waited for the shadow of death—the real one, not the digital mockery Marco had built—to finally catch up and claim the pieces of Bodhi Rao.

But the glass didn't break. The world didn't explode.

A tear appeared in the white, a jagged horizontal slit like a letter-opener cutting through a sheet of paper. It was a rift in the white-out, a narrow aperture into a world that had "Real" as its default setting. Cold, grey wind—real wind, smelling of salt and scorched silicone and the sharp, bracing ozone of a coming storm—rushed through the gap, hitting my face with the reality of a slap. It was the first "True" sensory data I’d received in minutes, and it tasted like life.

The world didn't end.

It simply... ceased to assume. The "Default State" of reality had been reset, and the Manifold was the only place left to go.

I took a step forward, and fell into the Manifold.
