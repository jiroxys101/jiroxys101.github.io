Chapter 11: The Shard & The Shatter

Scene 1

The crystalline shard doesn't shatter. That would imply physics, and we’re distinctly outside of that jurisdiction now. Instead, it undergoes a phase transition—from "object" to "event."

One moment, my palm is pressed against the smooth, vibration-dampened surface of the relic, the cold stone sucking the heat right out of my skin. The next, hairline fractures race down its vertical axis like lightning frozen in amber. The runic circle beneath my hand, seconds ago a dim auxiliary glow, blazes with a white-hot intensity that promises to cauterize my retinas if I don't blink.

I don't blink. I can't.

Then the sound hits.

It’s not a noise you hear with your ears. It’s a high-pitched whine that bypasses the auditory nerve entirely and drills directly into my molars, a frequency pitched so high it feels like my skull is being sanded from the inside out. A bass concussion follows—a rhythmic thrum that skips my heartbeat and rattles my occipital bone like a loose tooth in a jar.

Ozone. It’s the first thing to hit the senses, but calling it a "smell" is too polite. It’s an assault. The acrid, sterile, burnt-air scent of an electrical storm condensed into the single cubic meter of space I resolve in. It burns the lining of my sinuses, triggering a flash-memory of a blown transformer in the Ward 7-C basement—sparks showering onto wet concrete, the smell of fried insulation and impending darkness.

My mouth floods with a metallic copper tang. Capillaries in my nose have burst under the sudden pressure differential. I swallow blood and static.

Then the floor evaporates.

I fall. Not into the wine cellar floor, not onto damp earth, but into a cold, sucking void that offers no resistance, no friction, and no gravity. My knees buckle into nothingness.

The shard begins to scream.

It’s not a mechanical feedback loop or a magical hum. It’s a warbling, wet shriek that is unmistakably, horribly human. A chorus of throats tearing themselves apart in unison.

Unfiltered soul-data, my mind supplies. The thought comes with the terrifying, crystalline detachment of a man watching his own execution in high-definition. Compression algorithm failure. I just triggered an unhandled exception in sixteen centuries of Cavaretta math.

The cellar’s oak racks and damp stone walls begin to glitch. I watch them cascade into white pixels that stutter like corrupted video feed. The Soldatos flanking the circle—men with names, mortgages, favorite football teams—don't even get the dignity of dying. Their screams are truncated mid-syllable, clipped by a surgical edit as the physical world is garbage-collected around me.

I look up at the Victorian plaster scrollwork of the ceiling. It unspools like wet tissue paper in a monsoon, revealing a column of roaring white light where the parlor should be.

This isn’t destruction, I realize, watching the edges of my own vision begin to fray against the white expanse. This is a de-render. The Shard is treating reality as a memory leak.

Panic tries to claw its way up my throat, a biological imperative screaming that falling is bad and dissolving is worse. I shove it down. I’m a combat medic. Panic is a luxury for the triaged, not the triage officer.

Assess. Stabilize. Survive.

The mantra feels hollow here. There is nothing to stabilize. The environment itself is the wound.

My hand spasms, fingers curling into a fist around nothing. The ring on my finger—cold gold, heavy and scarred—burns against my knuckle. Not with heat, but with mass. In a world rapidly losing its definition, it feels impossibly solid. An anchor point in a dissolving sea.

Papa.

The presence in the metal is faint, static-filled, but it’s there. A stubborn refusal to be deleted.

I hold onto that sensation like a drowning man clutching a lifeline. Because right now, it’s the only thing convincing me I still exist.

Scene 2

At the epicenter of the white sprawl, Marco Cavaretta is still fighting.

Or trying to.

His arms are raised, his lips moving in a desperate, frantic counter-chant that should have been command-line authority. In the boardroom, or the ritual circle, that voice moved millions of dollars and bent reality. Here, it’s just noise.

Golden threads of mana spiral from his chest—filaments of pure capital, the accumulated "interest" of his lineage. But they aren't forming a shield. They’re being pulled inward. Sucked into the breach by a gravity that ignores the physics of wealth.

"NO!"

Marco’s voice cracks—a raw, wet scream that seems to lag behind the motion of his jaw, like a dubbed movie playing out of sync. "I am the investment!"

The words distort, pitch-shifting downward into an ugly, subsonic growl as time itself begins to stutter around him.

I watch, paralyzed by the sheer, chaotic wrongness of it. Marco’s fingers elongate, stretching like pulled taffy in a hot room. They unspool into golden filaments that whip toward the singularity’s core.

His face follows.

I see the skin pull away from the bone like wet clay in a high wind. His features—the arrogance, the fear, the Cavaretta jawline—lose their definition, smearing into streaks of light that are reabsorbed by the void.

The scream doesn’t fade. It’s simply clipped. Mid-vowel. As if a surgical hand somewhere in the backend code just deleted the audio track entirely.

He’s gone, I think. Just like that.

The stench hits me then—not ozone this time, but burnt sugar and rotting meat. The olfactory signature of soul-energy being metabolized. It flashes through the void, cloying and sickening, before vanishing as if the air recyclers just kicked in.

I bite my own tongue to keep from joining the chorus of screams that no longer exist. The pain is sharp, specific. The metallic taste of my own blood floods my mouth again. It’s the only thing anchoring me to my own body.

He’s being Garbage Collected.

The clinical phrase is a flimsy piece of sheet metal against the atrocity unfolding ten feet away, but I hide behind it anyway. The Shard doesn’t care about his lineage. It doesn’t care about his portfolio or his grand designs. It just sees a memory leak that needs to be plugged.

The other mages follow in a rhythmic, seismic cascade.

Thum-thum-thum.

A heartbeat amplified to a scale that rattles my teeth in their sockets.

One by one, the Soldatos and the minor mages twist, stretch, and are inhaled. I see a man I recognize—the one who held the door for us—disintegrate into a shower of golden motes. It’s almost beautiful, a kinetic sculpture of golden light and erasing history.

If you can ignore the fact that the medium is human lives.

Marco wanted godhood. He wanted to ascend. Instead, he’s become high-octane fuel for a machine he never understood.

Then the singularity turns.

There’s no face to look at, no eyes to meet, but I feel the shift in attention. It swivels toward me like a searchlight made of pure, needle-point need.

The heartbeat accelerates. Thum-thum-thum-thum.

Ozone floods back, sharp and electric. A tugging sensation begins at the center of my chest. It’s not physical—it’s not pulling on my skin or my clothes. It’s pulling on me.

Something is trying to extract my meaning from my meat.

Appetizers are done, I think, a hysterical bubble of laughter rising in my throat. Time for the main course.

Scene 3

Bianco Cavaretta isn’t screaming anymore. He’s past the point of vocalization, sliding across the frictionless white expanse of the manifold with his fingers clawing at a vacuum.

His shadow, the only dark thing in a world of blinding data, stretches toward the singularity’s core like pulled taffy. A physical representation of the existential weight trying to drag him into the recycle bin.

"BODHI!"

The name breaches the hush, a raw, hoarse shriek that cuts through the white noise of a collapsing reality.

I look at him. His eyes are fixed on mine, wide and glazed with the absolute terror of a man who just realized his invisibility has finally failed him. He’s the Sidekick. The comic relief. The guy who survives because he’s not important enough to kill.

But the Shard doesn’t know narrative tropes. It just eats.

I feel the tug at my own chest intensify. The Shard’s hunger focuses into a needle-point of demand, drilling into my sternum.

I should let him go.

The thought is cold, rational. Survival of the fittest. Triage protocol: save the salvageable. Bianco is dead weight. He’s a Cavaretta. He’s part of the rot.

But my body moves before my brain can sign off on the ethics. The combat-medic reflex—the hardwired biological imperative to fix, to save—fires hot and fast, overriding the logic.

Some Ol’ Bulls***, I think with a sudden, warped clarity. Protagonists make the sacrifice plays. It’s in the script.

I look at the singularity’s core. I don't see a god. I don't see a monster. I see a system. A spiral of golden metadata representing a compression algorithm.

And I see the lag.

There. A microsecond window between reclamation cycles. A stutter in the frame rate.

If I overclock my own conceptual mass, the calculation runs itself in the back of my mind, I can create a local exception. A priority object too heavy to process. For exactly one millisecond.

My hands blaze.

I don’t cast a spell. I open a wound in the fabric of the world.

The Blood-black Ouroboros manifests in a roar of anti-light, the serpent eating its own tail in a loop of creative destruction. It feels like shoving my arm into a blast furnace. The entropy screams through my channels, turning my veins into lines of molten lead.

I don’t aim at Bianco. I aim past him. Into the fold of space where the estate’s wards are flickering like a dying lightbulb.

The recoil is immense.

A concussive pop of displaced air rushes to fill the vacuum I just created. My shoulder dislocates with a wet, sickening crunch. The pain is dazzling, white-hot and immediate, grounding me in the moment.

The healing loop fires automatically, snapping the bone back into the socket before I can even scream. The grinding of cartilage sounds like rocks in a blender.

"EXIT!"

The word rips from my throat, raw and bloody.

The blast hits Bianco like a freight train of concentrated nothingness. It hurls him sideways, knocking him out of the singularity’s gravity well and through a chaotic fold in spacetime.

I watch him vanish into a burst of blue light—the ward boundary accepting him like a swimmer breaking the surface.

He’s out. Safe. The Sidekick has his reprieve.

The singularity roars.

It’s a frustrated, hungry sound that vibrates the atoms of my skeleton. The anchor I created by overclocking myself shatters instantly. The tugging at my chest becomes a violent pull.

I lose my footing—or what passes for footing in this void. I begin to slide toward the core, my feet scrabbling uselessly on a ground that is no longer there.

I taste blood. I taste triumph.

Narrative satisfied, I think, spitting a glob of red into the white. Sidekick saved. Now, the Protagonist faces the Boss.

My shoulder throbs in time with the singularity’s heartbeat.

Thum-thum-thum.

Bring it on, I think. And the void answers.

Scene 4

From my vantage point in the basement void—though "basement" is a memory and "void" is an understatement—I watch the world end.

The upper floors of the Cavaretta estate don't just fall; they fold.

It’s a slow-motion catastrophe. Victorian masonry, prize-winning privacy hedges, centuries of accrued architectural arrogance—all of it compresses into a spiraling column of debris racing toward the singularity’s maw. The estate isn't being destroyed. It’s being sucked into a vacuum that doesn’t recognize the concept of "up," "down," or "structural integrity."

A groaning, tearing cacophony fills the space between my ears. Wood splintering like dry bones. Stone cracking with the sound of gunshot. Glass shattering in a continuous, crystalline scream. The destruction is layered into a single, dissonant chord that vibrates in the marrow of my bones.

The air is thick with the scent of dying history. Ancient dust, older than the country itself. The sharp ozone of rain. The copper tang of exposed plumbing ripped from the walls. It creates an olfactory profile that I can only describe as house death.

Then gravity inverts.

My stomach lurches into my throat. I feel myself being pulled up—or what used to be up—toward the collapsing structure. It’s as if I’m standing on the ceiling of existence, watching the floor fall away into the sky.

Above me—or below, the distinction is meaningless now—the sky is visible through the gaps in the maelstrom. A swirling vortex of clouds is being sucked downward, dragging the stars with it. They flicker like dying embers in a storm, winking out one by one as they cross the event horizon.

This is what a god’s tantrum looks like, I think. The metatextual part of my brain is still running its commentary track, frantically trying to categorize the apocalypse into something manageable. The Shard is throwing a fit because someone poked it wrong. Some Ol’ Bulls*** divine petulance.

I watch a gargoyle—one of the estate’s Gothic grotesques—tumble past me. As it crosses the threshold of the singularity, it stretches. Stone softens into putty. The face contorts, its silent snarl warping into something almost expressive—a mask of stone-cold terror—before it vanishes into the white.

The groaning of the building shifts. It deepens, smoothing out into a low, resonant hum. It sounds like a choir hitting a bass note that’s too low for the human ear to resolve, but perfect for the soul to tremble at. As if the singularity is finally singing a song it’s been composing for centuries.

A chandelier, still lit by some absurd miracle of electrical persistence, spirals past me. Crystal pendants catch the white light, scattering rainbows into the void before it hits the core and is gone.

And then, I am alone.

I am the last object with mass in a world of light.

Silence follows. It’s not a peaceful quiet. It’s a complete, absolute, aggressive silence. A weight against my eardrums. The air is gone, stripped away by the vacuum, leaving only the sterile nothingness of raw mana. Even the memory of scent has been deleted.

The singularity knows I have nowhere to go. It’s not rushing anymore. It pulses. Constant. Steady. Patient.

I am the last data point in the file. And the file is being closed.

Scene 5

Subjective time dissolves. There are no seconds, only frames.

My reality becomes a low-bandwidth video feed struggling to keep up with the data rate of a dying universe. Flickering images. Stuttering motion.

My body rotates without my consent. My feet rise above my head as "up" becomes "in." The singularity’s core is no longer a point in the distance; it’s an aperture. A massive, dilated pupil pulsing with a sickly golden light that promises the end of individuality.

I’m drifting toward it like a satellite in a decaying orbit.

A sound fills my skull—a digital rising tone, pure and piercing. It reminds me of the old dial-up modems, the sound of connection being established. The sound of a loading bar inching toward 99%. It vibrates my teeth. It vibrates my thoughts.

Then the pixelation begins.

It starts at my fingertips. I feel a tingling numbness, like circulation being cut off, but sharper. Cleaner. I look at my hand.

My fingers are no longer flesh. They are data.

Rectangles of colored light stutter where my knuckles should be. They flicker, rearranging themselves, converting from biology into a more efficient format.

I am becoming a file attachment, I think. The metaphor is absurd, a desperate joke cracked by a mind trying to armor itself against the terror of its own disappearance. High-bandwidth transfer. Some Ol’ Bulls*** upload.

The pixelation spreads. My hands fade to translucent. I can see my veins—not as vessels carrying blood, but as lines of glowing code beneath thinning skin.

I’m not dying. Dying is messy. Dying is rot and decay. This is formatting. Cold data is replacing warm blood. Information is overwriting the messy redundancies of my biology.

And it hurts. Not like a wound, but like an amputation of the self.

The ring on my finger burns.

It’s a sudden, violent flare of presence against the numbness. My father’s transfigured form, the gemstone I’ve carried through fire and blood, is fighting the dissolution.

It turns hot. Then cold. Then hot again. It pulses against my pixelating finger, an anchor dragging in the mud, refusing to let the ship drift.

Dad’s still in the ring, I realize. I clutch at the phantom sensation with a hand that is increasingly made of light. He’s the only thing keeping me from being completely rewritten. The only thing with enough weight to survive the transition.

My vision blurs. Then it sharpens—but into something other.

I’m seeing the metadata of reality. The underlying code that defines shape and mass and time. The smooth white of the void resolves into streams of logic, equations describing the absence of matter. The sustained tone in my head fractures into harmonics—a chord of frequencies that describes the actual meaning of emptiness.

For a moment, I taste something.

Not blood. Not static.

Burnt sandalwood.

My mother’s favorite incense. Sharp, woody, comforting. It shouldn't be here. There is no air to carry it. But it floods my senses, a memory bleeding through the firewall of my mind.

Then the transition hits its peak.

The world collapses into a tunnel of rushing information. Bodhi Rao—the doctor, the healer, the man who said "F*** Magic"—dissolves.

I am no longer a solid object. I am a stream of light hurtling into the dark.

Upload complete. Welcome to the Manifold.

Scene 6

Grey.

A limitless, suffocating expanse of grey.

Not darkness. Darkness has depth. This is flat. This is static. The visual snow of a detuned television, amplified to cosmic proportions and projected onto the inside of the universe’s skull.

I float in the center of it. Weightless. Formless.

I am acutely aware of the absence of my own biology. I don't breathe, because there is no air. I don't blink, because I have no eyelids. I simply perceive.

The sound is omnidirectional. A hissing roar of white noise that seems to originate from nowhere and everywhere at once. It’s not the aggressive silence of the void. This is the sound of a system waiting for input. The sound of a Loading Screen.

Great, I think, though I’m not sure what I’m thinking with. I’m stuck in the lobby.

I look down—or what my mind insists is "down."

A single point of solid color burns in the grey.

The ring.

It’s still there. Circling a phantom finger I can no longer feel, glowing with a soft, pulsing amber light. The white noise seems to recede around the metal, as if the ring creates a bubble of coherence in an ocean of noise.

Sandalwood.

I can smell it again, stronger now. It’s not just incense. It’s his aftershave. A sharp, clean scent that used to cling to his flannel shirts. It shouldn’t have survived the upload. Impossible.

But the impossible is the only thing that works here. It’s a metadata override. A user-defined variable that refuses to be cleared.

Dad, I think.

And for the first time in what feels like centuries, I feel a tear.

It’s real. I can taste the salt. I can feel the wet track on a cheek that shouldn't exist.

You’re still holding on. You’re still the anchor.

The static begins to thin.

Vague, ominous shapes coalesce in the distance. Monoliths of shadow standing patient and silent in the grey. The white noise resolves into whispers. A thousand voices, too quiet to parse, muttering just at the edge of perception. Each one a thread of biography being pulled from the Manifold’s archives.

A new scent floods the vacuum, overpowering the sandalwood.

Old flowers. Roses and lilies. The cloying, heavy sweetness of a funeral arrangement left too long in a warm room.

Reality is buffering. The next scene is loading.

A pressure builds in the space where my chest used to be. Not physical pressure, but existential. The sense that something vast—something ancient and hungry—is finally noticing me.

The Loading Screen is over. The Main Program is starting.

And I am not ready.

Scene 7

The Main Program doesn't load. It invades.

No signal. Anywhere.

That’s the first thing I know, because "seeing" is gone—not darkness, not black, not even the mercy of a void I can imagine. Just the absence of visual data. It’s like someone yanked the optic cable out of my brain and left the port open to the wind.

The grey static from the Loading Screen clears, and I’m blind.

And then the pressure arrives.

It’s not on my skin. It’s not on my lungs. It’s on me.

A low, throbbing hum replaces the whispers, bass so deep it vibrates phantom bones I’m not sure I still own. It’s the kind of frequency you feel in your teeth at concerts when the speakers are too big for the room.

Except the room is existence. And the speakers are screaming.

The smell of old flowers intensifies—roses, lilies, chrysanthemums. The heavy, cloying sweetness of viewing rooms and open caskets. It suffocates me, a blanket of perfumed death trying to cover up the truth: something rotting is nearby.

Salt stays on my tongue. Grief made tangible.

Weight stacks on top of weight.

At first it’s subtle, like the moment after you step into a crowd and you can tell the air is thick with other people’s moods. Then it multiplies. It crushes from all sides—psychic pressure, emotional gravity, the accumulated grief of a thousand souls pressing against the inside of my skull.

The shard’s battery is aware.

And it’s reaching for me.

My hands—do I have hands?—go cold with instinct. My left heel exists. My right doesn't. My spine is a question mark drawn in trembling chalk.

I’ve been here before.

Not in cosmic static, sure, but in Ward 7-C, when your body is detoxing and your brain is a broken radio picking up stations from hell. The only way you survive the flood is by building a wall between "you" and "everything."

Nurse Padma, I think, grabbing at the memory like a jagged rock. Four count. In-two-three-four. Hold-two-three-four.

Mental Firewall.

Dissociation as a tool, not a curse. A survival separation.

Bodhi Rao.

For one heartbeat, it works.

The hum fractures into individual frequencies. Not words—impressions. Personalities.

Fear that tastes like iron.

Good. Pain is still an address. Pain means I'm home.

Papa.

Within the Shard, the first thing that arrives is noise pretending to be memory—raw packets of terror slamming into my psyche faster than thought can label them.

But one packet is louder than the rest. One memory isn't noise. It's the Missing Frame.

The whiteout un-pauses.

"Protocol mandates immediate processing."

"DISMANTLE."

Flesh -> Math -> Stone.

AND WE
We…
…and…
And ...who...who exactly are we?
we...are...Bodhi? Bodhisunya?

The name feels strange in our mouth. Foreign. A sound someone else makes.

It's a ... strange name...for a child...isn't it?
We think my mama choose it for us...or was it papa...it must have been one of them or the other right?
We believe our papa had strong hands...big strong hands...do you know him?...do we know him...? Our papa...

We remember hands. Rough palms. Smelling of turmeric and surgical soap. Sunday mornings. The steam of coffee shifting the light in the kitchen.

He must have lifted us and carried us in those hands once
We can feel his hands...
Or did we carry him?
He seemed so small the last time we saw him
The last time we held him he was so small

The memory shifts. Darkens. The basement. The ritual. The smell of burning flesh that was also our flesh.

He could fit in our hands
He could fit on our finger

The realization hits us like a hammer to the oscillating bone.

The ring.
The stone.
The weight on our hand.

We think...we believe...we dream...?
He gave us this ring you know?
Our favorite ring.

Silence. A beat of pure, crystalline horror.

We wish they would come and see us sometime.
Our mama. Our papa.
They're both so far away.
Where did our parents go?
Why didn't they take us with them?

The guilt breaks the last firewall.
We turned him into a thing. We trapped him in the metal to save ourselves.
He is watching us dissolve. He is holding our hand while we disappear.

I killed him. The Ritual is a failure. I killed my father. And I turned him into a ring. And now people are going to die. Including me. I failed.

We Want...we want our mama...we want our mama we want our papa...
Please, we just want our mama and papa to come and get us and take us home. Please just let us go home.
We just want to go home. We want our mama.
Please mama.

Images burst across the blindness like lightning behind eyelids.
A child’s birthday party—1942, paper hats, ration-cake, a laugh that ends too abruptly when the door opens and the adults stop smiling.
A surgeon’s hands—1887, blood up to the wrists, knuckles raw from scrubbing, eyes that don’t blink because blinking wastes time.
A soldier’s final sunrise—trenches, cordite and mud, breath steaming, a cigarette held between trembling fingers like it’s a sacrament.

Each flash comes with a full-body memory.
Smoke.
Perfume.
Bread.
Blood.
Cordite.
Cough syrup.
Wet wool.
The sound of a train.
The taste of a final meal.
The taste of a kiss goodbye.
The taste of a tear swallowed in silence.

Everything.

Our "self" starts to fray at the edges. Not peeling, but dissolving. Like sugar in hot tea.
Bodhi becomes porous.
The Shard, without malice, merely tries to fit the deluge of memory in the only vessel available to it.
A container big enough to hold the archive.
A format that won’t reject the upload.

The flood rewrites the narrator.

Our thoughts splinter into broken shards of language—some ours, some borrowed, all sharp. Names repeat until they become a spell. Accusations surface like buoyant corpses. The Cavaretta curse fits our mouth too perfectly.

No sound. Only thought.
Only the pressure.
Only the sense of being eaten from the inside.

Our Mental Firewall tries to rebuild itself automatically, like scar tissue forming over and over in the same place.
It costs.
We feel something tear in the conceptual muscle behind our eyes.
A migraine without a skull.
A nausea without a stomach.
A panic without lungs.

The ring burns with presence, fighting to keep one thread of us intact.
Papa, we think, and it comes out as an anchor line thrown into a storm.
The warmth answers—faint, stubborn.
A grip.
But the flood is bigger than grip.

The firewall splits.
And the grief crashes through like a tidal wave.

We drown.
In lives.
In last breaths.
In the terrible, intimate physics of other people’s endings.

The final, clean boundary inside our mind—Bodhi Rao—warps under the weight.

We feel ourselves becoming plural.
Not metaphorically.
Literally.
We.

We are cold.
We are hurting.
We are missing our hands.
We are screaming for our mamas and Papas and gods that never answered.
We are furious.
We are afraid.

And somewhere deep inside the swarm, a small, bright thought flickers—mine, we think, mine—and it is so petty and human it almost makes us laugh.

*F****.
Magic.

Then the shard leans in.
The weight increases.
More voices queue at the door.

And the last thing we feel…before any semblance of "I" becomes an outdated file format…is the ring pressing hot-cold against a finger that shouldn’t exist.
My father refusing to let the archive take everything without a fight.

The fight doesn’t feel heroic.
It feels like being held under.
And learning, too late, that we can drown in anything.

We reach out.
Not with a hand, but with a need.

"We are coming, mama…papa," we whisper, and the voice is a thousand voices.

The Manifold winks out.

Bodhi Rao is no longer a man.
