...look, maybe I should back up. Definitions matter. The old guard wants you to think Magic is some mystical favor from the Gods. Screw that.
By-and-large, the magical world is wrapped up in the same veneer of Civility as the Mundane. And to the detriment of all involved, is entrenched in the same level of bureaucracy, hypocrisy, and corruption.
But I digress!
...where was I?
Right. "Magic."

1. "Magic Isn’t Scientific"

Wrong. Magic follows laws. Conservation, Entropy, Flux, Displacement. It has principles just like physics. It’s just... messier. Less Newton, more Schrödinger. Magic is a science, like anything else.

Understand this: everything that exists in our reality, exists within the confines of the Rules of Reality. Just because something does not fit into our understanding of the world, does not mean it violates it.

Some time in the 40s, a few magically inclined physicists came to a conclusion that would have turned the World on its head, if the information wasn't surpressed.

At its core, Magic is the fifth fundamental force of the universe. Right up there with gravity and whatever it is that keeps your atoms from vibing out of existence. Except this one has an on-again/off-again relationship with order and loves loopholes.

There’s a whole a** list of magical laws. Thermodynamic knockoffs. Newtonian reboots. Gauss with jazz hands. We call ‘em the 12 Laws of Thaumaturgy. They matter - but only if you plan on living through spellcasting.

Magic displaces reality. Literally. It creates a void where the laws of physics collapse and new rules can take over. Think of it as reality having a panic attack. Physics takes five, and Magic strolls in with a Venti double espresso and a grudge. If reality were a bathtub, Magic is your favorite "'bubber' ducky". It "displaces" the water around it. We call that duck the Thaumaturgic Field. It’s everywhere. It permeates every atom, molecule, and soul. It’s carried by particles we call Thaumions - basically quantum middle fingers to the Standard Model. And trust me, they’re real. We’ve measured them. Every spell ripples through the Thaumaturgic Field like a fart in a wind tunnel - invisible to those without the "gift", but undeniably real. Thank the thaumions for that.

Frosted steel bites. My wrists sing the bruise‑colored hymn they started rehearsing the moment the rune‑cuffs first locked, each note sharper than the last. A crimson‑armored Regulator nudges me forward with the flat of his warded baton. The corridor ahead is iced in thaumic blue, ceiling‑mounted auric strips thrumming like hive wings. The air tastes of antiseptic pine and latent ozone— the Academy’s signature perfume for don’t touch, don’t breathe, comply. I check the strip's pulse rate out of habit; 60Hz. Standard for containment, but the yellow undertone means the backup generators are cycling. Sloppy.

Beneath the hum of the pumps, a speaker system plays a distorted, time-stretched loop of "Toxic" by Britney Spears, retuned to 432Hz for "maximum patient pacification." It’s working; a violent calm settles over me.

I shuffle past a security panel, wincing as the decontamination mist hits the gash on my left eyebrow. Dried blood pulls tight—an ironic tilak, paired with the entropy‑scar constellations glowing a timid violet beneath my skin. Great. I probably look like a discounted saint in the clearance aisle.

"Step," the Regulator drones, voice modulated through a brass half‑mask etched with anti‑contagion sigils.

His partner raises a palm, rune‑plate flaring, and a transparent containment door iris‑spins open. Containment Ward 7‑C scrolls in holographic letters across the threshold. Underneath: BIO‑THAUM RISK: SEVERE.

Intensive care for inconvenient miracles— that’s what 7‑C is. Where the Academy parks anything or anyone too magically radioactive to mingle with the donor‑banquet crowd upstairs. Today I’m the bleeding‑edge exhibit. My eyes half-close. If they want to treat me like a specimen, I might as well act like one.

My boots squeak traitorously on the slippery disinfectant film. Above the ceiling tiles, mana-vacuum pumps whine at a pitch that rattles my teeth—a sub-audible drill into the skull. The air tastes of astringent ethanol layered over burnt rosemary, distinct as a chemical cocktail. Sterility flavored with failed ward incense. Everything is over-lit, over-white; even shadows look laundered here.

A pair of articulated arms descend from the ceiling rig the moment I cross the gasket line. They’re chromed, jointed like praying mantis legs, and end in crystal‑studded rings that dilate to encircle my chest. Needle-thin runes of spell-light probe through skin, bone, soul. Focus. Detox first, existential crisis later.

I keep my sarcasm on an external monologue setting— easier to file complaints with Reality when you narrate.

A phosphorescent diagnostic rune scrolls above me, projecting vitals I don’t need to see: ENTROPY LOAD: 173 μE. PHYS‑ENERGY DEFICIT: −2 800 kcal. SPIRITUAL NOISE: MODERATE. Basically I’m an over‑turned battery leaking acid on the carpet.

Automaton Medic #26 glides forward on rail‑wheels embedded in the floor. Humanoid chassis, alabaster fiber‑resin, face a blank porcelain plate with a single runic lens where a nose would be. An aura‑speaker crackles.

"Subject designator Bodhisunya Rao, unlicensed healer. Processing detox protocol D‑441. Hold still."

Hold still, he says, as if the restraining rings haven’t already turned my ribs into compression art. A spray of cold mist envelops my arms— neutralizer aerosol laced with auric chelators. Feels like being dipped in dry ice and shame.

"Friendly PSA," I grit out through clenched teeth. "The patient preference field on your bedside forms says ‘he.’ I’m a student, not a lab rat."
"Detox commencement: 15… 14…13…"

A violet surge ripples across my forearms, entropy wards flaring defensively. The automaton’s sensor registers the spike; mechanical arms ratchet tighter. Tendons creak. Pain booms behind my eyes.

The world tilts. For a split second, the pain tastes like mint and the sound of the machine's gears looks like jagged yellow lightning. Synesthesia override. The limiter's kicking in.

The protocol runs in three vicious acts:

  1. Thaum‑Ion Extraction: Needle‑thin conduits breach skin, siphoning rogue particles. Each extraction feels like a tiny sun being yanked through capillaries.
  2. Entropy Scrub: Nanoscopic glyphs crawl over my nerves, shaving runaway probabilities back into compliance; every shave is a razor dipped in nostalgia— I relive the alley clinic in flashes: cracked linoleum, girl’s ribs fluttering, sirens screeching.
  3. Limiter Sync: My student ID cuff— the same one that throttled me mid‑spell— is attuned to the mainframe here. It re‑imposes safe‑casting thresholds by burning a brand into the metaphysical equivalent of marrow. The process tastes like burnt cinnamon and regret.

By the time the countdown reaches zero I’m panting, shirt plastered to spine, eyes watering from ozone stench. The automaton retracts its claws.

"Detox complete. Probability deviation reduced to Academy standard. Subject remains bio‑thaum risk due to unstable entropy signatures."
"File it under feature, not a bug," I rasp. My voice rides a raspy, half‑drunk chuckle— more defiance than humor.

The rings detach and retreat into the ceiling. Gravity is obscene without their support; I nearly collapse before my knees catch. Two Regulators step inside, wands leveled—you could slice bread on their righteousness.

"Hands behind—" one starts.

I comply, palms outward, entropy scars still glowing faintly.

"Dude, relax. I’m environmentally friendly now— zero‑emission cleric. Just don’t shake me, you’ll void the warranty."

No laugh. Philistines. They usher me to a magnesium‑polished examination plinth. Every step squeaks. My soles leave watery boot‑prints that evaporate in seconds, devoured by ambient ward‑heat.

The medic returns with a tray of opalescent phials. Each is labeled THAUM‑CREDIT ASSESSMENT SAMPLE. A little meter hovers overhead, ticking like a parking clock. Even here— especially here—the ledger rules. The Academy can’t bill me for unauthorized miracle‑working until they quantify exactly how much miracle I leaked onto the alley floor.

The automaton unsheathes a humming lancet rune.

"Blood and aether sample required for tuition damage appraisal," it explains in a voice blended from two hundred bored bureaucrats. "Failure to comply will result in asset seizure."
"Take a number," I mutter, presenting my forearm. "I’m worth at least four kidneys on the thaum‑organ market. There’s a discount for bulk."

The lancet kisses skin— bright sting, then numb. The phial fills with shimmering, ink‑dark fluid threaded by violet sparks. My blood looks like a night sky in a test tube. Poetic or biohazard? Why not both.

The meter chirps and calculates. Projected fine: 89,000 lumens. Lumens— the Federation’s idea of magical fiat currency, pegged to the average mana cost of a Class II shield rune. Eighty‑nine grand for saving a kid. That's three years of Dad's ventilator bills. Or a decade of indentured servitude in the mana mines if I default. My stomach hollows.

"Itemized receipt?" I ask.
Sarcasm is my respirator; inhale at your own risk.

The automaton prints a vellum slip. Services Rendered: Emergency Null‑Field, Unlicensed Regeneration, Municipal Ward Damage (minor), Regulator Deployment (level B mobilization). Below the total: PAYMENT PLAN AVAILABLE— INTEREST ACCRUES DAILY.

I laugh— a cracking glass noise. "Any chance I can pay in existential dread? I’ve got surplus."

"Humor detected. Humor flagged as potential defiance response. Recommending disciplinary oversight."

"Put it on the stupid tax."

Adjacent countertops are littered with half‑dissected manikins, rune‑etched organ jars, and crimson binders titled Miracle Efficiency Index—Q4. They logged my transgression as a data set.

Here’s the Academy’s math: unauthorized healing equals uncontrolled variables. That terrifies grant committees. Better to let a girl bleed out than risk statistical anomalies in miracle yield.

The detox lamps are jealous little suns. They hang from the ceiling like metallic fruit, each one pulsing a sequenced ultraviolet strobe meant to burn residual magic out of flesh. Every third pulse slams into my retinas and ricochets into the back of my skull, leaving after‑images of jagged runes that refuse to blink away. I try to look anywhere else, but the room offers only polished steel, reinforced glass, and the reflection of a handcuffed idiot who tried to save a stranger.

The idiot looks tired. His cuffs— thaumically graded adamantite, because of course we can’t skimp on theatrics—are secured to a standing restraint column shaped like a six‑armed tuning fork. The bolt‑ring at my spine buzzes with low‑grade suppression wards, just strong enough to make every lymph node feel like it’s full of crushed ice. My tattoos—entropy wards carved into skin last semester during an ill‑advised practical on harmonic scarring—still glow under the ultraviolet, but the light robs them of color, bleaching violet into something sickly and wrong.

A wave of nausea climbs my spine— part detox, part fury. My fingers flex involuntarily, entropy crackling. I force a deep breath— count the sensory anchors:

  • Cooling coolant smell in distant vents.
  • Sub‑bass throb of mana turbines.
  • Sharp tang of copper on tongue.
  • Clinical white glare prickling eyes.
  • Heartbeat— heavy, syncopated.

I swallow anger. Not here, not yet.

A maintenance automaton trundles by outside the glass partition, spray‑nozzles hissing. It pauses, identifies me as "Biohazard Candidate," and mists the corridor with a lemon‑pine antiseptic that coats the back of my throat in sweet chemical dread. The smell mixes poorly with the copper ghost of my own burned mana still lodged behind my sinuses.

I keep checking the chrono‑glyph projected on the far wall. 22:15. 22:16. Each minute drags nails across the chalkboard of my patience. Where is he? The entire Academy knows I melted half a regulator grid re‑knitting a girl’s lungs. Public‑relations crisis, ethics violation, textbook recklessness—pick your scandal flavor. That alone should have Hartnell sprinting down the halls like his starched lab coat’s on fire.

Unless, of course, he’s rehearsing the narrative. Drafting the press release where he’s the diligent surgeon blindsided by a rogue student. Polishing the language so "Bodhi Rao" rhymes with "irresponsible martyr."

"Subject displays elevated cortisol, 38 percent above baseline. Recommending sedative rune application."
I raise a brow. "Bill me extra for the lullaby?"

22:19. Footsteps at last. Not the clipped, baton‑steady march of Regulators; these heels are softer, arrogant. The door rune‑scans an approaching badge, decides the newcomer outranks everyone within fifty meters, and swishes open with a hissed salute.

The machine lifts an injector sigil; I flinch, readying a counter‑glyph. Before escalation can bloom, another voice ripples down the corridor—smooth, cultured, heavier than the gravity in the room.

"Stand down, Medic #26. The council wants him alive and conscious for questioning."

Footsteps approach— leather soles, unhurried, expensive. I know that cadence: Dr. Lucien Hartnell, Staff Medical Surgeon, absentee guardian of the alley’s dying girl. Scene 4’s upcoming antagonist.

The moment crystallizes; I can practically feel the narrative baton passing, but Scene 3 isn’t done milking me yet.

Regulators snap to stiff attention. The automaton retracts the injector, placing it delicately back on the tray.

A red rune flashes on the wall monitor: CODE BLUE: WARD 4B.

Someone screams down the hall. A junior medic freezes, panic in his eyes.

Dr. Lucien Hartnell glides into view. He doesn't break stride. He doesn't even look at the monitor. He simply raises his left hand, snaps his fingers, and flicks a stabilization glyph—perfectly calculated to the patient’s entropy signature—through the wall. The alarm dies instantly. The screaming stops.

He saved a life like he was swatting a fly.

Then he turns that gaze on me. Immaculate white coat, high collar starched into an exclamation point, hair silvered at the temples. His surgical mask hangs loose around his neck. Diamond‑point cufflinks wink thaumic gold sigils: Primum non nocere—First, do no harm. The irony prickles.

Behind him float two assistant scribes—etheric drones shaped like elongated teardrops of blue light. One of them pulses red as I shift my weight, logging a potential aggression vector. They don't just record; they curate. Every twitch, every elevated heartbeat, every sarcastic aside is being hashed, indexed, and fed into a liability algorithm before I even speak.

Hartnell’s eyes rake the room, cataloguing equipment like a head chef spotting fingerprints on silverware. Then he fixes me with a look polished to a mirror sheen. I know that look. Professional disappointment, version 3.6. The one reserved for interns who drop priceless vials and for departmental budgets that arrive one digit short.

He pauses, eyes skimming readouts on my file projected in mid‑air. His jaw tightens. I see the moment he clocks the 89 000‑lumen liability tied to my name— a liability linked to his department’s budget because he was off playing god‑knows‑what while a kid flat‑lined.

For a second, the projection flickers, overlaid by a memory I can't blink away: a girl's face, grey as ash, blood blooming on pavement like a dark flower. I taste copper. The sarcasm catches in my throat. I didn't save her because of the budget. I saved her because she was scared and I was the only goddamned thing standing between her and the Void.

The door’s runic lock disengages with a polite chime.

I crack a grin, blood from the split lip finally thawing enough to trickle.

"Doctor Hartnell," I croon, voice low, a blade ribboned in velvet. "Glad you could join the f***‑up you left on hold."

Shock rolls through the ward like silent thunder— the Regulators stiffen, the automaton recalculates threat matrices, the containment lamps flare a defensive yellow. Hartnell’s expression slides from controlled annoyance to ice‑sheen rage.

Good. Rage means leverage.

But that, as bards say, is another scene’s problem.

The containment lamps dim back to sterile white. The automaton withdraws, wheeling its tray of my priced‑out miracle to some accounts‑receivable abyss. The Regulators stand sentinel, wands crackling with non‑lethal voltage.

I flex my entropy‑scarred palms behind me, feeling the singe of detox burns and the new‑formed callus of resolve. The system just billed me for compassion. I’m about to invoice it for arrogance.

Hartnell steps fully inside, lips parting to speak—

2. "Magic is Genetic"

Also wrong. Magic isn’t ancestral. Or at least, not entirely. Anyone can learn it, if they survive the process. Yes, some people are born with an edge. Sorcerers with magical, sometimes divine ancestors. Warlocks with spooky contracts. But the rest of us? We’re Wizards. We study. We train. We bleed.

Point being, you don't have to have wizard parents, sell your soul to some Otherworldy Patron, or sleep with a Satyr on a Full-Moon night to cast fancy spells. It might help, sure, but it isn't a prerequisite.

Most folks slot Magi into two neat piles: the "born with it", and "broke themselves for it". Sorcerer's and Wizards. Those born with innate magic and those who learned magic. Depending on how they use their magic, a mage might be a Warlock, Druid, Cleric - or some DIY hybrid nightmare. Labels are blurry. People aren’t spreadsheets.

*Say it with me now: "All Sorcerers, Warlocks, Druids, and Clerics are Wizards. Not all Wizards are Sorcerers, Warlocks Druids, or Clerics. More on this later.*

3. Magic is Easy.

Magic is a skill. It’s learned, refined, and brutally punishing. Most people burn out before they master their first real spell. And the ones who don’t? They still screw up. One missed calculation and your fireball becomes a localized sun. Good luck explaining that to your neighbors.

It can take years of practice, study, and application to be capable of Magic, decades for it to be useful. You ever run into a street magician? I'll bet you a haypenny you've encountered a Mage. That's right, maybe one in ten times, that guy actually pulled that card out of your ear. The sad part is that's probably as much magic as they are capable of. Poor booboo probably exhausted themself for the rest of the day to make you giggle. Magical processes require a lot of concentration. Think the same Order of Magnitude as trying to run PERL code in your head. Physically, you're literally burning calories to give physics that sweet chin music. Combine with the mental toll, and you're looking at a difficulty curve that intersects with NASA Scientists and Olympic Athletes.

4. Magic is Free

Absolutely not. The universe is a stingy bastard. Magic increases entropy - chaos. Every spell you cast pushes the universe a little closer to heat death. You can maybe reverse that with some extremely complex negentropic spells, but most mages just blow stuff up and call it a day.

In our world, this concept is known as "Equivalent Exchange." You burn what you’ve got to break the rules - heat, stamina, heartbreak, whatever’s handy. Magic doesn’t run on goodwill. If you are prepared, you can use an external source of Magic, and If you get creative enough, that cost can be reduced to next to nothing. But never "something" for "nothing".

There’s no "spell slots" or wands that do all the heavy lifting. Every magical action you take costs energy - from your body (Physical Energy), your mind (Spiritual Energy), but usually some mixture of the two. The more intricate or powerful the spell, the more you burn through your reserves.

Imagine running a marathon while solving differential equations in your head. That’s what casting a mid-level spell feels like. The higher-tier stuff? That’s Olympic-level burnout paired with a migraine and an existential crisis.

Most mages, even those of the highest order, will still find themselves catatonic trying to conjure Silver. Gold? Uranium? Platinum? Don’t even try. Nature charges interest for those. You’d need the First or Fourth Divinity, and those haven’t been seen since gods walked the earth - or pretended to.

Because no matter what you do, using magic incurs a cost. And the bill always comes due.
Wanna make a fireball? Best to hang onto that Zippo for now.

Somewhere distant, a chime announces tribunal preparations. I flex my hands, even though the cuffs bite. The hunger to fix things gnaws louder than the pain.

5. "Magic is Uniform"

Feckin' laughable. Capital L.

Magic isn’t uniform. It’s messy. It's inconsistent. It’s personal. But try telling that to an academy-trained bureaucrat with a wand up their a** and a PhD in Thaumaturgy. The magical world loves its categories - Magecraft vs. Occult Practice, "High Magic" vs. "Wild Magic," formal vs. tribal. As if a hex from a back-alley Bokor is somehow less legitimate than a spell written in eleven-point Times New Roman with footnotes. They divvied up magic into "Schools" like a Hogwarts-themed TED Talk: Alchemy? Chemistry with a god complex. Potions? Middle-school chemistry, but deadly. Runic Magic? Writing that bites. Ritual Magic? Weird hats, Latin chanting, and definitely candles. Divination? Magical gambling with worse odds than Vegas. And Healing? Oh, that’s where they put us - the glorified magical nurses. You know, the ones who hold you together while everyone else is too busy blowing things up for prestige.

If you really want to get spicy, ask ten different nations what "real" magic looks like. In Vaticanus, it’s prayer. In NAMF, it’s code. In Moscow-Kiev, it’s socialist ritualism. Then you’ve got the stuff the Western world either exoticizes or ignores - Djinn-binding in Cairo, shadowcasting in Lagos, Kabbalistic syntax in Jerusalem, Tantric recursion in the Punjab Republic. These aren’t just styles. They’re entire magical ideologies, shaped by belief, geography, and power. Magic isn’t neutral - it reflects who gets to define "order" and whose gods are allowed to speak. Westerners love acting like they discovered structure, but go tell a Haitian Bokor or a Himalayan monk that their magic’s outdated. If they’re feeling generous, you’ll walk away with a minor hex. If not? Well… hope your insurance covers metaphysical trauma.
