Chapter 5: The Transfer Notice

Beat 1: The Transfer Notice

The New Canaan Hospice waiting room was a masterpiece of beige bureaucracy. Designed to induce immediate submission. Overhead, fluorescent tubes hummed at a "headache-yellow" frequency, vibrating against my optic nerves, while the air scrubbers pumped out a scent that was equal parts stale coffee and rain on hot asphalt—the perfume of dying in a room that charges by the hour. A cooling unit near the reception desk clicked with a violent, irregular thrum-click that mimicked a failing heart. It punctuated the silence of my own poverty.

I flinched at the sound, my body betraying the exhaustion I'd been trying to plaster over with a mask of stoicism. My stomach clenched. It was a knot of acid and bile that hadn't seen real food in twenty hours. I tasted the sharp, metallic tang of blood where I'd bitten my tongue during the intake interview. My fingers crumpled the edge of the paper in my hand, the thermal texture slick and cheap against my sweating palm.

Stop it. I forced my hand to smooth the notice back out. You can't fight the enemy if you can't read the fine print. I raised the notice to eye level, dissecting the document like it was a cadaver in anatomy lab. I traced the header—Account Arrears Summary—and the bolded deadline, calculating the minutes until my father became a municipal statistic. My eyes tore through the fine print, hunting—for a loophole, a typo, a statute citation I could weaponize.

The font was serif—Cambria or Times New Roman. Formal. Polite. Lethal. It's always a serif when they're killing you, I thought. It insulated the administrators who signed these warrants. "Municipal Storage" was the polite fiction they used for the mass graves in the Exclusion Zone. A trick that turned a corpse into "inventory." This wasn't medical care; it was a supply chain issue, and my father had been flagged as expired stock.

"Excuse me,"

I said, my voice a scrap of broken glass.

"There's a procedural desync in the discharge billing. Chronal markers aren't valid until countersigned."

A lie. A desperate technical fabrication. I delivered it anyway, wearing the ghost of a white coat I no longer owned.

The Administrator finally looked at me. Her eyes slid over my ruined suit and unkempt hair with the practiced dismissal of someone cataloguing garbage. She tapped a single key—Enter—and the printer behind her spit out a confirmation slip.

"The queue automated physician sign-off at 0800 hours, Mr. Rao," she said, not looking up. "The transfer is immutable. System-level protocol. There is no desync."

She placed the Transfer Confirmation on the counter. The sheet landed with a soft, final slap. A gavel made of paper.

I stared at the white paper glaring under the harsh lights. No human being left to negotiate with. Only algorithms executing orders. I couldn't pay the arrears (Insolvency). I couldn't hack the municipal grid without my academy credentials (Access Denied). I couldn't punch a server rack (Futility—also, assault charges).

A perfect, silent punchline.

I backed away from the counter, eyes darting around the lobby for any other option. They landed on the Holographic Loan Kiosk in the corner. A predator trap for the desperate. A digital shark smiling with 400% APR teeth. The only door left open.

Beat 2: The Automated Threat

The Loan Kiosk squatted in the corner of the lobby, a slab of off-white plastic that had been bleached by too many rounds of hospital-grade disinfectant. It hummed with the high-pitched, tooth-aching frequency of a device that never slept. Stained with the ghost of a thousand desperate palm-prints, it was a shrine to the commodified soul.

The screen chirped a cheerful, synthetic major-key melody. A simplified, wide-eyed cartoon fox wearing a stethoscope—Dr. Penny-Wise—flickered to life. Its pixelated tail wagged in a rhythmic, hypnotic loop.

"Good morning, Neighbor!" the kiosk chirped, the fox's voice a syrup of artificial empathy. "I see a lot of stress in your bio-markers! Would you like to access some 'Neighbor-Help' Liquid Assets? We're dedicated to bridging the gap between your health and your bill!"

I stepped into the proximity field. The machine’s camera lens, a tiny black eye nestled in the plastic, whirred as it tracked my movement. The air smelled of burnt dust and warm, scorched plastic—the scent of a processor working overtime to strip-mine the poor.

"Account 88-Delta," I said, my voice barely a whisper against the hum. "Entropic stabilization for C. Rao. Seventy-two hour bridge."

The fox’s ears perked up.

"Processing... Oh dear! It looks like your traditional fiat-credit has suffered a total-system collapse. But don't worry! At Neighbor-Help, we know that you are your greatest asset. Would you like to review our Vitality-Lien options?"

Biological collateral. The polite term for carving out pieces of your future to pay for the present.

"Give me the numbers," I rasped.

"For a seventy-two hour stabilization, we're looking at a standard Causal Excise. Five percent of your peak-metabolic window, to be harvested during your chronological fiftieth year. Plus a minor neurological 'sampling' of your long-term creative memories for our demographic-enrichment training sets. Shall we begin the harvest?"

Five percent of fifty. The years when I’d finally be able to breathe. And the memories—they didn't just want my labor; they wanted my childhood as training data for an algorithm designed to replace me.

I laid my hand on the scanner bed. It wasn't glass. It was a mesh of micro-needles and thermal sensors.

It latched.
A sudden, jarring suction that felt like it was trying to pull the marrow right through my pores. My vision swam with static. A fleeting image of my seventh birthday flashed: the smell of the ocean, the wooden boat's weight—then grayed out, like a file being deleted in real-time.

A sharp, electric sting. The machine shuddered, a satisfying ka-chunk of gears deep inside its guts.

"Alert: Biological Yield Insufficient," the fox said, its voice losing its warmth. The tail stopped wagging. "Causal data-density too low. Applicant age-to-yield ratio is 'Sub-Optimal.' Transaction Denied. Action: Refund of harvested data is unavailable due to file corruption. Have a nice day!"

It didn't just reject me. It stole from me and then called me garbage. Typical. I should have known—the last time I signed a contract that promised 'futures' for 'service', it had landed me in the mud for four years. The Tuition Assistance was supposed to be the discharge, not a prelude to another 'enlistment'.

I laughed—wet, broken, scraping my throat raw. The machine was polite because it didn't need to be violent. The math did the violence for it. I turned my back on the pixelated fox. I had to escape this administrative violence before I screamed or broke something.

Beat 3: Neon Bruise-Light

I retreated into the alcove of a row of vending machines, a narrow Purgatory of buzzing coils and stale air. Above me, a fluorescent tube was dying. It flickered in a rhythmic, violent pulse of "Bruise-Purple"—a color that felt like a physical throb against my eyeballs. Strobe... dark... strobe... dark...

The air smelled of warm, scorched plastic and the cloying, fake-cherry scent of generic soda syrup. It was thick. Too thick to actually inhale.

I fumbled for the receipts in my pocket. My hands weren't just shaking; they felt like they were vibrating at a different frequency than the rest of my body. These weren't just paper; they were indices of failure, thermal-printed anchors dragging me into the gray slush.

Hospice Entry: $1,200 (Non-Refundable). Causal Audit: $450. Parking (Zone 4): $80.

Eighty dollars for the privilege of waiting for a man to die. The irony was a physical weight on my solar plexus, a crushing gravity that made the floor feel like it was tilting at a forty-five-degree angle.

My heart was galloping, a frantic, trapped animal in a ribcage that was suddenly three sizes too small. I tried to swallow, but my throat had turned to sandpaper. The sound of the fluorescent light—a high-pitched skreeeee—grew until it was the only thing I could hear.

I'm dying, I thought. The medical student in me tried to run the diagnostic—tachycardia, hyperventilation, acute adrenergic surge—but the student was drowning in the noise.

A Billing Administrator walked past the alcove. She was a blur of crisp white poly-blend and the scent of expensive toner. I reached out, my fingers brushing the air where her sleeve had been.

"Wait," I tried to say.

It came out as a wet, pathetic wheeze.

She didn't even turn her head. Her heels clicked away—clack, clack, clack—the sound of a system that functioned perfectly as long as you weren't an error code. I wasn't a man to her. I wasn't even a patient. I was a negative balance. To the system, I was insolvent; therefore, to its agents, I was invisible.

The purple light flared once, blindingly bright, and then died with a final, pathetic pop.

I was left in the semi-darkness. The silence was worse. It was the sound of my father's lungs failing in a room three floors up, and there was nothing—not a single credit, not a single drop of childhood memory—that I could do to stop it.

I slumped against the side of a 'Protein-Plus' dispenser. The cold metal was the only thing keeping me from dissolving into the floor wax. Panic rose in my throat, hot and acrid. I needed air. I needed to not be here.

Beat 4: The Alley Exit

The alley behind the hospice was a monochrome canyon of wet concrete and overflowing dumpsters. It was snowing—heavy, wet flakes that tumbled from the gray sky like torn clumps of cotton. They sizzled faintly as they hit the trash-smeared pavement, dissolving instantly into the toxic slush that coated New Canaan like a second skin.

The air here bit. It tasted of wet ash and diesel, rotting vegetables, and the metallic tang of unscrubbed atmospheric soot—the flavor of the city when it thinks no one is tasting it.

I pushed away from the heavy steel door, letting it click shut behind me. I needed distance. I needed the cold to numb the panic radiating from my chest. I fumbled for my pack of cigarettes—cheap "Synthi-Slims" that burned too fast and tasted like tire rubber—and lit one with shaking hands. The flare of the lighter momentarily illuminated the grime on the dumpsters: layers of graffiti and grime that looked like geological strata of urban decay.

I dragged deep, the harsh smoke scouring my lungs, grounding me in the present moment. I tilted my head back, letting the snow melt on my face.

Low-res precipitation, I thought, the analytical loops of my training failing to disengage. The particle physics are lazy. Uniform trajectory. No drift.

I deconstructed reality to keep it at arm's length. I wondered if the snow was even real, or just another layer of the municipal illusion designed to hide the dirt. If I looked close enough, would I see the pixels?

Crunch.

The sound cut through the ambient hum of the city like a gunshot. A heavy, deliberate step on gravel.

My brain categorized it: Expensive sound. Heavy leather sole. High density rubber heel. Not the cheap, slapping tread of synthetic sneakers.

I froze, the cigarette halfway to my mouth. My gut went still—the kind of stillness that comes before an ambush, a half-remembered instinct from briefings I wasn't supposed to remember. The silence that followed was too heavy. Suffocating.

The alley wasn’t empty. Not a sanctuary. A kill box.

I turned, calculating the vectors of escape. A lock secured the back door from the outside—standard security protocol. The alley mouth was fifty yards of slippery, uneven terrain. I stood cornered in a dead-end of my own making.

I didn't run. Running implies guilt. Worse, it triggers a predator's prey drive.

I dropped the cigarette into the slush, the embers hissing as they died. I turned to face the darkness at the end of the alley, forcing my posture to straighten. I swapped my "Exhausted Student" persona for something harder, sharper. Armor I hadn't worn in years.

Beat 5: The Soldati Prompt

Three shadows detached themselves from the darkness at the mouth of the alley. Shadows I recognized.

The Jims.

That’s what I called them. I wasn’t interested in giving Marco’s muscle generic mobster monikers like ‘Tony’ or ‘Fredo.’

If I'm being frank (not 'Jim', HA!), I was never entirely sure I wasn’t meeting the same three men every time. They seemed grown from identical vats of high-grade protein and low-grade empathy, processed into matching, wool-coated walls of professional violence. Marco liked his assets modular; if a Jim got his head taken off by a backfiring ward, another one with the same haircut and blank stare would be out for coffee ten minutes later.

Soldati of the Cavaretta Famiglia. Combat Mages. Professional Hitmen.

Oh, piss my a** in half. Was there a text thread to coordinate collecting on my debts today?

The first thing I noticed wasn't their size. It was the taste of the air around them. A sharp, singing scent like violin rosin—the Trioxygenic-residue of active, primed spell-work. My entropy wards prickled in warning. These weren't street-thugs; they were processors.

They moved in a staggered triangle formation, each step synchronized with military precision. The Jim on point—Kinetic Knuckles—had glyphs crawling across his knuckles that flared a dull, predatory red. The second Jim hung back, fingers tracing lazy patterns in the air—Vector Convergence. The third, the Suppression Jim, was the scariest: he wasn't doing anything visible, but I could smell the chalk-and-sulfur stench of a prepared alchemical dampening array.

S***.

I ran the probability calculus in my head, the numbers tumbling like dice.

One-on-one against a Jim? 50/50, maybe. I'd clocked faster problems in deeper mud. Against two of them? 15-20% odds—still a gamble I might take if I were desperate. But three? Three trained mage-enforcers working in coordinated formation?

Less than 3%.

The lead Jim lifted his left wrist. A high-fidelity holo-emitter sparked to life, cutting through the falling snow like a welding-arc.

A small, shivering hologram of Marco Cavaretta materialized. He was holding a flute of champagne, the background noise of a gala—expensive laughter and chamber music—buzzing through the transmission. Marco looked great. He always looked great. The sleek, tailored cut of his suit and the practiced shine of his hair screamed "Old Money," even if the blood under his fingernails was still fresh.

"Bodhi," Marco's voice said, crisp and terrifyingly casual. "You're late. Again."

"Marco," I said, squinting at the blue ghost. "You've got a voice that goes up one side of me and down the f***ing other. The hair plugs are keeping, I take it?"

The hologram flickered as Marco laughed. It was a digital, hollow sound.

"Humor is a poor hedge against insolvency, Bodhi," he said, taking a sip of his virtual champagne. "Your father’s hospice bill hit the arrears limit at 0900. The hospice admin sent the transfer notice, didn't they? Municipal storage for old Dinesh. A cold end for a man who built the very Ley Lines this city uses to bleed its citizens dry."

I felt the anger flare—hot and acrid, burning through the exhaustion.

"Don't talk about my father," I spat.

"I’m talking about assets, Bodhi. Dinesh Rao was a brilliant engineer. He built the veins of New Canaan. It’s only poetic that the family he served should eventually... own the blood in his. But my father—Don Vincenzo—he’s a traditionalist. He has a certain sentimentality for your mother’s research into Entropic Manipulation."

Marco leaned in, his holographic face enlarging. Between his fingers, he held a tiny, glowing object. A sliver of something that looked like calcified violet light.

"The Shard," he whispered. "Vatican-grade mana. I’m offering a trade. Perform a Class V Sub-Resurrection on my father using this sliver as your anchor. A Causal Inversion of his failing vitals. Do that, and your father’s debt is cleared. Permanently. He stays in comfort. You get your license back."

I stared at the glowing sliver in the hologram. My stomach did a slow, sickening roll. A Class V? That wasn't just healing; that was violating the conservation of soul-data. It was a technical impossibility without a massive sacrifice.

"Men in positions of bargaining aren't usually in the habit of sending Three-Man Candygrams to communicate terms, Marco." I bit out before I could stop myself. Ohhhh, bad. Very bad. Bite your tongue, dude. BITE IT!.

"You didn't think I'd send mundanes after a cleric, did you?" Marco said, with a thin, pitying smile. "The deal is non-negotiable. I'm authorizing the Jims to provide... a gentle reminder of the urgency."

I stepped back, my heel hitting the rusted flank of a dumpster.

My tenure under Marco painted a vivid image of where this was going.

Oh well. Might as well set the tone.

"You've been a real twat since you got good hair, Marco..."

The hologram snapped off. The silence returned, filled with the static pressure of violence and the acrid bite of primed spell-work.

I raised my hands. Not in surrender. In the shaky guard of a man who knew he was about to be processed.

My brain, starved of options, did the unthinkable: it started playing music. Not real music—a hallucination of adrenaline and sleep deprivation. A stadium-announcer voice echoed in the hollow of my skull, absurdly sincere.

Who is freezing cold, with the strength of a hati? (BODHI!)
Who uses the microwave to warm up his mom's chapathi? (BODHI!)
Who is sick of eating pizza every night? (BODHI!)
Who is going to lose a fight to prove that Amma was right? (BODHI!)

I barked a laugh that died before it formed. The absurdity of it was a pressure valve, a psychological circuit-breaker before the fuse blew.

"Fine then,"

I ground out, the words escaping before the fear could choke them.

"Three-on-one? Somebody play my theme music, it's time to throw our lives away, boys! I hate you, bhenchod, let's die!"

Beat 6: Administrative Violence

The movement was so efficient it registered as violence. Kinetic Knuckles stepped in—no telegram, just a sudden encroachment of my personal space—and I felt the air compress around his fist. The kinetic amplification runes across his knuckles flared cherry-red.

I threw up a ward. Instinct. My left hand snapped up, fingers rigid, thumb bent inward. Pataka. The Flag. A banner of kinetic resistance unfolding in the space between us. The glyph blazed to life in the air—a hasty Kinetic shield that should have eaten the momentum of his punch and scattered it into thermal noise.

It didn't.

His fist punched through my ward like it was tissue paper. The glyph shattered into a shower of violet sparks, and the impact kissed my ribs with the force of a wrecking ball. I staggered, the breath exploding from my lungs.

Bad math. Very bad math.

I sidestepped the follow-up strike, the motion more instinct than training. The blow whistled past my ear, and I felt the displaced air tug at my hair like a jealous lover.

"Tough day at the barnyard then, eh Jim?" I spat, my heart hammering against my ribs. "Which one of you is the animal wrangler? Can we put that thing back in it's pen before it poops on something?"

They didn't laugh.

I didn't wait for the second move. I lunged. Not at Kinetic Knuckles—at the Vector mage. Disrupt the formation. Break the geometry.
I chambered my right hand, curling the fingers tight, thumb locking the index. Mūṣṭi. The Vajra Fist. I throw a punch and cast at once. My knuckles connected with his jaw, and the mudra multiplied the vector. The air distorted—a spatial lag, a double-shock of force that cracked bone like porcelain.

For a microsecond, the fear vanished. Something older took its place—not dark, not ancient, just sharp. The world narrowed to angles and openings. The crazed smile found my lips before I could stop it. I knew that smile. I hated that smile.

Vector Jim staggered back, his jaw already swelling. Then he spat blood and smiled back.

His fingers twitched, and the world went sideways. Not metaphorically—literally. The Vector Convergence field activated, and suddenly my forward momentum was redirected ninety degrees to the left. I stumbled, the geometry of my own movement betraying me.

He wasn't taking the hit—he let me commit to the motion so he could bend it.

Before I could recover, Kinetic Knuckles was on me. A jackhammer blow caught my side—amplified, compounded—and I felt a rib crack with a sound like a green branch snapping. The pain was a white flower blooming behind my eyes.

I snarled, curling my index finger deep, thumb triggering the release. Arala. The Bent Finger. Not a sloppy pulse, but a focused Kinetic Gust. A sudden, violent vacuum shrieked from my palm. The air screamed as I displaced it.

The pulse hit the Vector mage square in the chest. He flew backward, slamming into a dumpster with a crash that echoed off the alley walls. My palms scorched, the recoil a hot wire snapping through my wrists. For one second—one gloriously stupid second—I felt like a protagonist again.

Then the Suppression specialist moved.

He'd been circling. Patient. Professional. His fingers had been busy the whole time, and now I saw why: a pale chalk circle glowed on the pavement beneath my feet, hidden under a thin layer of slush. Alchemical symbols pulsed along its perimeter—binding glyphs, dampening sigils, a f***ing suppression array.

The power hemorrhaged out of me.

Not fade—die. Like someone had ripped the plug from a socket. The runic wards I had tattooed on my forearms went dark. The ambient thaumic field that had been buzzing at the edge of my senses went silent. My connection to the Old Speech, to the raw causal weight of existence itself, was severed.

"No—" I started.

The suppression field clamped down like a vice. My muscles locked. My lungs stuttered. Every metaphysical nerve in my body screamed as the alchemy scoured my mana channels raw.

No. NO.

I pushed back.

I don't know where the strength came from—rage, maybe, or the memory of my mother's hands teaching me to reach past limitation. The suppression field flickered. The alchemical circle beneath me cracked along one edge, chalk flaking away.

For one heartbeat, my magic roared.

The wards on my arms blazed back to life—not violet this time, but a molten, furious magenta glaring through phantom cracks in my skin. The air around me superheated. The slush at my feet flash-boiled into steam. Suppression Jim's eyes went wide.

"Triśūla—" I started to grit out, my fingers straining to form the Trident, the syllable tearing at my throat.

Then the Suppression Specialist slammed a dampening glyph into my spine.

The world went white.

The suppression field doubled in intensity, tripled, until I couldn't feel where my body ended and the binding glyphs began. My knees buckled. My face slammed into the icy concrete, the impact sending a shockwave through my jaw. I bit my tongue for the third time today. My mouth filled with fresh, warm copper.

"HOW F***ING ELABORATE DO WE HAVE TO MAKE THIS FIGHT?!"

I screamed into the slush, the words bubbling through blood and spit.

It was absurd. All of it. The choreography, the precision, the teamwork. They'd done this before. Hunted mages. Broken sorcerers. I was just another line item.

I looked up at them from the slush. Three faces, three expressions of professional boredom. They weren't fighting me anymore. They were processing me. Administrative Violence—impersonal and absolute.

The pain bloomed late, a sudden, hot fire in my chest and leg that washed over me, drowning out the cold. I gasped, finally sucking in a ragged breath that tasted of stricken matches.

The shock went deeper than physical. In the span of ten seconds, I had gone from sorcerer to meat.

Stop struggling, my brain commanded. Your magic is offline. You can't win a physical contest against three trained enforcers. This isn't the field; the old unit isn't coming for you.

I needed to survive. I shut down my emotional centers, visualizing a heavy steel shutter closing over my fear—and that new, hungry heat.

Switch to Analytical Mode, I whispered to the pavement. Observe. Record. Survive. And don't s*** yourself.

Beat 7: Fiscal Metadata

Context: Bodhi analyzes his injuries through the lens of medical billing codes.

The world reoriented itself ninety degrees. I was suddenly intimate with the slush, staring at it from a distance of two inches. The cold seeped instantly through my clothes, a shock of ice water against burning skin.

I tried to inhale, but my chest wall refused to expand. There was a deep, grinding sensation in my left side, like two tectonic plates rubbing together. It isn’t pain yet; it was just a wrongness. A mechanical failure of the chassis.

My mind stuttered, trying to process the data input. Impact detected. Structural integrity compromised.

I coughed, and the motion sent a jolt of white-hot agony through my ribcage that made my vision black out for a microsecond. The pain was absolute, a singular point of focus that obliterated the cold, the snow, and the Soldati standing over me.

I didn't scream. Screaming requires breath I didn’t have. Instead, I forced my eyes to focus on the mud in front of my face.

I saw a cigarette butt (Synthi-Slim, menthol). A shard of green glass (Heineken bottle, pre-war). A frozen rat turd (Rattus norvegicus).

I named them. Nicotine delivery system. Silicon waste. Bio-waste. I forced my brain to categorize the environment to keep from drowning in the sensation of my own broken bones.

The pain tried to pull me under, but my training kicked in—not the healing arts, but the billing codes. My mind involuntarily overlaid "Fiscal Metadata" onto the injury, projecting a glowing blue HUD onto the dirty snow.

I visualized the fracture line on my fourth rib. ICD-10 Code S22.40xB: Multiple fractures of ribs, initial encounter.

I ran the numbers.

Cost of a Level 4 bone knit: 500 credits.
Anesthesia (local block): 200 credits.
Post-op suppressants (Percocet-IV): 50 credits per dose.

But that was just the physical damage. My mind—treacherously thorough—kept scrolling.

TMI-7.2: Thaumic Nerve Burnout, secondary to forced suppression. Cost: 2,200 credits.
TMI-3.1: Mana Channel Scarring, alchemical origin. Cost: 1,800 credits, non-reversible.

The suppression hex they'd hit me with? That was going to leave entropy scars that would ache every time I tried to cast for the next six months. Maybe longer. The kind of magical nerve damage that insurance companies classified as "pre-existing condition" the moment you filed a claim.

The realization hit harder than the boot: I was broken—physically and metaphysically—and I couldn't afford to be fixed. The Soldati hadn't just beaten me; they had levied a fine measured in years of my life.

Every breath that grated bone-on-bone was a reminder of my negative net worth. Every time my mana channels twitched in phantom protest, I was hemorrhaging credit I didn't have.

Stop it, I told the HUD. Data doesn't hurt. Data requires processing.

I chose not to be a victim; I chose to be an auditor. I closed my eyes and visualized my ribcage as a 3D schematic, highlighting the fracture in angry red. I "equipped" the pain, wearing it like a heavy coat rather than letting it crush me.

They could break the hardware. They couldn't touch the source code.

Beat 8: The Beatdown

The beating found a rhythm. A metronome of violence played out on the snare drum of my torso. It was a calibration I recognized from the brass's classified briefings: how to tenderize a high-value asset without breaking the hardware.

Thud. Pause. Thud. Pause.

They'd settled into a formation—the Suppression specialist maintained the dampening field, keeping my magic pinned like a butterfly on corkboard. Kinetic Knuckles delivered the blows, each one landing with that sick, amplified crack that turned bruises into fractures. The Vector mage circled, favoring his left leg, watching, adjusting angles, ready to redirect any desperate movement I might attempt.

Military. Clockwork. They'd done this to mages before. I was just another entry in their logbook.

I curled tighter into the fetal position, knees drawn up to my chin.

"Three against one," I wheezed through a mouthful of copper-tasting slush. "Real heroic, boys. Your mothers must be so proud."

A kinetically-amplified boot slammed into my thigh, silencing the commentary. The impact condensed into a single point of white-hot agony.

These weren't the chaotic, frenzy-kicks of street thugs high on stims. These were professionals with spells. They were placing their shots with the precision of butchers separating cuts of meat. The sound was wet and dense—leather on wet wool on bruising flesh—punctuated by the sharp, electric crack of magically-enhanced impacts near my kidneys.

I curled tighter, head tucked down. But my primary focus—my obsession—was my hands.

I wove my fingers together and jammed them deep into the space between my chest and my thighs, creating a fortress of bone and muscle to shield them.

Not the hands, I chanted internally, the mantra looping like a corrupted audio file. Anything but the hands.

They were my interface. My livelihood. The only tools I had left to interact with the world's code. If they broke, I was offline permanently. A wizard without gestures is just a lunatic muttering at the sky.

The Soldati seemed to sense my strategy. They adjusted their vector, aiming lower, targeting my thighs and the exposed curve of my spine. The pain was blinding, a white noise that threatened to drown out my "Surgical HUD," but through the haze, I noticed something odd about the timing.

They were stopping every few seconds. They were letting me gasp for air, letting my heart rate stabilize just enough to keep me from passing out, before delivering the next blow.

They are managing my vitals, I realized with a jolt of horror that cut through the agony.

Not an execution. A calibration. They needed me functional.

Dead debtors don't pay interest.
Unconscious debtors can't accept terms.

They walked a razor's edge. They inflicted suffering without causing permanent damage that would lower my resale value. I was being "tenderized."

One of the Jims delivered a sharp, testing kick to my exposed elbow, trying to pry my arms away from my chest. It sent a shockwave up my ulnar nerve that felt like liquid lightning. The instinct to uncurl, to fling my arms out to protect my body, was overwhelming. My lizard brain screamed at me to surrender, to open up and beg for it to stop.

I clamped my jaw shut so hard I felt a molar crack. I overrode the lizard brain.

"It's a good thing you're funny, Jimbo," I wheezed through a mouthful of copper-tasting slush, "since you're the uglier triplet."

He didn't even flinch. He just kicked me again.

I mean, I thought it was funny.

I deliberately relaxed my ribs, accepting that they were ablative armor. I kept my hands locked tight, burying them deeper into my gut.

Take the chassis, I thought, tears of pain leaking from my squeezed-shut eyes. Break the housing. Wreck the frame. But you don't get the goddamn tools.

I stayed curled in the slush, trading broken ribs for intact fingers.

Beat 9: The Ultimatum

The violence stopped as it began. There was no wind-down, no panting exhaustion—a sudden cessation of impact.

A gloved hand tangled into my hair, yanking my head back with enough force to strain the vertebrae in my neck. The world spun from gray slush to the gray face of the lead Soldato, hovering inches from my own.

He smelled of stale coffee and recently applied cologne. It was a mundane, office-worker scent that clashed with the brutality of the moment.

"The Director expects your presence at the Estate tomorrow, dusk," the Soldato said, his voice dropping to a conversational register. "Clear your schedule; it's a mandatory consult. Don't be late again."

The words—clipped, precise.

He spoke the words like he was reciting a grocery list. His eyes were dead. No enjoyment. No hatred. A courier delivering a package that happened to be a threat.

I stared into his eyes, scanning for something—sadism, reluctance, any data point to file. I found nothing. Just the flat operational gaze of a man clocking overtime.

The humiliation washed over me, hot and shameful. A rival had not beaten me; a tool beat me. I was trembling from the sheer indignity of being handled like livestock.

Subject lacks metadata, I thought, my mind retreating to the safety of critique. No distinct features. No narrative weight. A Non-Player Character.

This man didn't matter. He was just a pixel in Marco's rendering. The debt had shifted. It was no longer about the 500 potential credits for the rib; it was about "Service." Marco didn't want money anymore; he wanted ownership. The beating was the down payment.

The Soldato released my hair with a shove, letting my head snap back down toward the concrete. I caught myself with one hand—the hand is safe, thank god—but my cheek smacked into the slush. The wet cold was shocking against my burning skin.

I heard them turn and walk away, their boots crunching on the gravel, already moving on to the next item on their agenda. Ideally, they would "double-tap" me, but I was an Asset now. Assets are preserved.

I lay there for a moment, listening to the fading footsteps. I tasted copper and grit.

The "Consult" was a trap, a servitude contract wrapped in a job offer. I had to take it. I had no choice. But I could still refuse the narrative of submission.

I gathered the blood and saliva pooling in my mouth and spat it onto the pristine white snow next to my face.

"Tomorrow,"

I whispered into the slush.

Beat 10: Aftermath

The alley fell silent again, but it was a heavier silence now, weighed down by the accumulated violence.

The snow continued to fall, indifferent to the blood staining the concrete. It covered the red slush with a fresh layer of pristine white. The beating had kept me warm. Now the cold sank its teeth in. Every exposed nerve screamed. The cuts on my face crystallized, flesh freezing to the air itself.

The adrenaline evaporated. The pain crashed back in.

No longer a sharp signal—a throbbing, systemic ache that pulsed in perfect sync with my heartbeat. Chilled barbed wire wrapped my ribs. My jaw ached with a dull, hydraulic pressure that radiated up into my sinuses.

I groaned, a wet, ragged sound that puffed out in a white cloud of vapor.

Diagnostic check.

I forced my eyes open. I ignored the ribs, the jaw, the throbbing warmth in my legs. I lifted my hands.

They hovered in the gray air, trembling. There was swelling across the knuckles—edema from the impact—and the skin was raw from the cold, but the structure...

I flexed the left hand. Then the right. Full extension. Range of motion unimpeded. The metacarpals were intact.

A laugh bubbled up in my chest, turned into a painful wheeze.

The tools are safe.

The laugh dislodged something in my mouth. I felt a hard script of enamel loose against my tongue. I spat it out.

My tongue traced the gap in my gumline. It was a permanent subtraction. I would never get that tooth back. The transaction with the Soldati had left a permanent receipt.

I stared at the tooth, my mind framing the shot. *White tooth. Red blood. Gray snow.**

"It’s a bit on the nose," I murmured to the empty alley, my voice thick with blood. "Color grading is too high-contrast. Cliché symbolism."

I slammed my palm against the rusted flank of the dumpster, using the leverage to force my body upright. Every bone in my legs threatened to buckle. My ribs screamed. My vision swam with gray spots, but I pushed through, refusing to stay on the ground. The ground is for corpses and trash.

I dragged myself to my feet, swaying like a drunk, listing to the left to favor my broken rib. Each breath was a negotiation with pain.

I was standing. In New Canaan, that was the only victory available.

Beat 11: The Deal Accepted

Leaning against the rusted flank of the dumpster, I looked up.

Above the canyon of the alley, the New Canaan Hospital tower rose like a monolith of glass and steel. The upper floors—the VIP wards, the gene-therapy suites, the executive offices—were glowing with a soft, periwinkle light.

The glow was the color of health and money—the hue of a world I used to live in.

A pang of loss hit me, sharper than the broken rib. Deeper than nostalgia. A door had closed. Yesterday, I walked those halls as a resident, a healer, a student of the Asclepius Guild. Today, I was standing in the trash looking up at heaven.

Class measured the distance between the alley and those windows.

The genre had shifted. The network cancelled the Medical Drama.

I am the f***ing servant again.

Cue the title card, I thought. Looney Tunes font. Sad jazz.

I wiped the blood from my chin with the back of my hand. I pushed myself off the dumpster, testing my balance. It held. Damage existed, but I was functional.

The "Quest"—work for Marco, pay the debt, keep my father out of the mass grave—was locked in. There were no side quests. There was no fast travel.

"Dusk,"

I said aloud, the word forming a puff of steam in the freeze-frame air.

An appointment and a sentence.

I turned my back on the Optimistic Blue light of the hospital and looked down the dark throat of the alley.

I started walking. One step. Then another. The pain was there, constant and loud.

I tapped the rhythm of it against the pavement as I walked into the next scene.
