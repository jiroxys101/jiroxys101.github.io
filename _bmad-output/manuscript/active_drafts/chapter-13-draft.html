Chapter 13

Scene 1

The world did not end with a bang, but with a hollow sound. A sound like a god inhaling the sky, followed by the wet, percussive slap of reality trying to fill the vacuum.

Bianco Cavaretta lay face down in the slurry of what used to be the family estate's rose garden. It was a place he knew better than his own reflection. He knew the specific ph of the soil here (acidic, tweaked for the hybrid tea roses). He knew the layout of the trellis work (imported ironwood from the Northern Zone). He knew that if he turned his head three degrees to the left, he would see the marble fountain his grandfather had commissioned to celebrate the family’s ascent to the High Council.

Now, there was only ash.

The mud was boiling. It pushed against his cheek, hot and viscous, smelling of ozone, burnt sugar, and the coppery, unmistakable tang of atomized blood. It wasn't just mud; it was a soup of destruction, a suspension of everything Bianco had ever called home, ground down to its molecular substrate.

He tried to push himself up. His brain sent the command—elevation, urgency, flee—but the signal died somewhere in his spinal cord. His arms were shaking so violently they wouldn't lock. He managed to roll onto his side, the movement agonizing, like grinding broken glass into his hip. He coughed, a wet, rattling sound, and spat out a mouthful of black phlegm that tasted of propellant and death.

His bespoke silk suit—a gift for his twenty-first birthday, tailored by the finest weaver in Rome, a garment that cost more than a citizen earned in a decade—was fused to his skin in places. He could feel the ruined fabric pulling at his burns, a constant, stinging reminder of his own fragility.

Nonno.

The memory hit him harder than the blast wave. It wasn't a vague sense of loss; it was a high-definition assault. He remembered the smell of his father's cologne—sandalwood and expensive tobacco—that had lingered in the library just this morning. He remembered the weight of his father’s hand on his shoulder during the Solstice Gala, the pride in the old man's voice.

“Resistance is legacy, Bianco. We do not break. We endure.”

And then, the light. The blinding, white-hot silhouette of his uncle standing against the sun, a figure of pure radiant energy. And Papa standing there, defiant, unbreaking.

And then... simply ceasing to be.

Not dying. Dying implied a process. Dying implied a body, a funeral, a transition. This was deletion. One moment, the patriarch of House Cavaretta was a pillar of the world; the next, he was physics. He was heat. He was a shadow burned into the retina of the universe.

Bianco wept. It was a small, pathetic sound in the silence of the crater, a whimper that had no place in the aftermath of such divine violence. He wiped his eyes with a trembling hand, smearing soot and blood across his face, effectively blinding himself with the grime of his own survival.

He blinked, trying to clear the grit. He looked toward the epicenter.

He expected to see a corpse. He needed to see a corpse. He needed to see the charred, twisted remains of the cousin he had tried to save, the cousin whose instability had birthed this nightmare. He needed Bodhi to be dead. If Bodhi was dead, then this was a tragedy. A terrible, incomprehensible accident.

But if Bodhi was alive... then this was a war crime. This was a murder.

The smoke cleared, stripped away by the heavy, radioactive rain that had begun to fall.

And Bianco saw the Monster.

It stood in the exact center of the glassed earth, at the focal point of the deletion event. It was naked, its skin pale and luminous against the charcoal ground, like a shard of moonstone dropped into a coal bin. It wasn't burned. It wasn't bleeding. It was pristine.

But it wasn't standing still.

It was... vibrating.

Bianco squeezed his eyes shut and opened them again, certain he was suffering from a concussion. But the image didn't resolve. The figure—Bodhi—seemed to be skipping frames in reality's render cycle. One second he was facing north, looking at the ruin of the manor house; the next, he was facing south, staring into the dark tree line. There was no transition. No blur of motion. Just Variable A becoming Variable B without passing through the space in between.

His limbs moved with a fluid, aquatic grace that defied the physics of gravity. He didn't shift his weight; he simply altered his position relative to the ground.

And the noise.

It wasn't a hum. It wasn't the wind. It was the sound of a hard drive scratching, amplified to a deafening screech that bypassed the ears and vibrated directly in the jawbone. It was the sound of logic failing. It was the high-pitched whine of reality complaining that something existed within it that was violating its Terms of Service.

The rain fell harder, a torrential downpour of cold, acidic water.

But it didn't touch him.

Bianco watched in horror as the raindrops curved around Bodhi’s form. They didn't bounce off a shield; they simply refused to occupy the same coordinates. The space around Bodhi was a dry zone, a bubble of rejected causality. The water feared him. The air seemed to recoil from his skin, creating a shimmering heat haze that distorted the background.

"Bodhi?" Bianco whispered. The name felt like a curse on his tongue. It felt like summoning a demon.

The figure stopped vibrating. The transition was instant. From 100 mph blurring to absolute statuesque stillness in zero seconds. Inertia was ignored.

The head snapped toward him—too fast. Necks shouldn't move that fast. Vertebrae should snap. Muscles should tear.

But the Monster just smiled.

The eyes were gone. There were no whites, no pupils, no irises. There were just twin pools of spinning gold fractals, infinite geometric loops eating the light around them. They were windows into a furnace, into a place where math burned hot enough to melt souls.

Bianco stopped weeping. He forgot how to breathe. He forgot his father. He forgot the estate.

In the face of this anomaly, grief was a luxury he could no longer afford. There was only the primal, lizard-brain terror of a prey animal realizing it has locked eyes with the apex predator of a new ecosystem.

The Monster took a step. The mud didn't squelch. It didn't yield. The ground seemed to harden to meet his foot, eager to support him.

Bianco tried to crawl backward, digging his heels into the sludge, but his body was failing him. The terror was a paralyzing agent.

Run, his mind screamed. Run before it deletes you too.

But he couldn't move. He could only watch as the thing that wore his cousin's face began to drift toward him, carried by a wind that didn't exist.

Scene 2

The Monster smiled.

It wasn't a human smile. It was too wide, showing too many teeth. It was a rictus of pure, chemical mania. It looked like the expression of a man who had stared into the heart of a nuclear explosion and found it funny.

"Little Cavaretta," the Thing said.

The voice did not come from its mouth. It came from the air, from the mud, from the buzzing inside Bianco's molars. It sounded like a choir of thousands, all screaming in digital harmony.

"Little white stallion," the voice distorted, pitching down into a bass that rattled Bianco's ribs. "Did you lose your saddle?"

Bianco scrambled backward, his heels digging into the sludge. "Stay back," he whimpered. "Stay back, you... you thing."

Bodhi didn't walk. He just... occurred closer.

A flicker of black static, and suddenly he was standing five feet away. Another flicker. Two feet.

Bianco could see the details now, and he wished he couldn't. Bodhi's skin was translucent, revealing veins that pulsed with black light instead of blood. There were symbols—glowing, golden glyphs—crawling under his epidermis like worms.

Scene 2

The Monster smiled.

It wasn't a human smile. It was a geometry problem. It was too wide, stretching the skin past the point of elasticity, revealing too many teeth that looked too white, too sharp, too... new. It was a rictus of pure, chemical mania, the expression of a man who had stared into the heart of a nuclear explosion and found the math hilarious.

"Little Cavaretta," the Thing said.

"Little white stallion," the voice distorted, pitching down into a sub-frequency that rattled Bianco's ribs like a cage. "Did you lose your saddle?"

Bianco scrambled backward, his heels digging furrows into the radioactive sludge. "Stay back," he whimpered, his voice cracking like dry wood. "Stay back, you... you thing."

Bodhi didn't walk.

Walking involved locomotion. Walking involved leverage, friction, the transfer of kinetic energy. Bodhi simply... occurred closer.

A flicker of black static—like a corrupted video file—and suddenly he was standing twenty feet away. Another flicker. Ten feet. There was no movement, only a re-writing of coordinates. The air displaced by his sudden arrival cracked like a whip.

Cousin Bodhi. The failure. The Null. The boy who couldn't light a candle with magic if his life depended on it. Bianco remembered him tripping over his own robes at the academy. He remembered him stuttering through basic theory exams. Make-fun-of-him Bodhi. Pity-him Bodhi.

That boy was gone. Deleted.

Bianco could see the details now, and he wished he couldn't. Bodhi's skin was translucent, glowing with an inner, sickly light. Beneath the epidermis, veins pulsed not with red blood, but with black, viscosity-free light. And deeper still, etched into the very muscle fibers, were symbols—glowing, golden glyphs that crawled and shifted like living worms.

"Monster," Bianco breathed. It was the only categorization his brain could offer.

Bodhi tilted his head. The gold fractals in his eyes spun faster, a kaleidoscope of impossible geometry.

"Monster?" the voice echoed, layering over itself—soprano, alto, bass, static. "That IS a categorization error, cousin. Check the scoreboard. We are the only reason you aren't currently participating in the water cycle as a fine red mist."

Use of the plural. We.

Bianco felt his bladder loosen. The horror was total. "You killed him. You killed my father. You murdered him."

"We deleted an obstacle," Bodhi corrected, his tone bored, clinical. "He was holding onto legacy code. Deprecated software. He tried to upload a firewall when the system was already formatting the drive."

He reached out. His hand was wreathed in black sparks that hissed as they touched the oxygen, burning holes in the very air.

"And you know what happens to legacy code, Bianco?"

Bianco shut his eyes, waiting for the end. Waiting to be deleted. He tried to think of a prayer, but the Gods felt very far away, and the Monster was very, very close.

Scene 3

A finger touched his nose. Boop.

It didn't feel like flesh. It felt like touching a live wire, a localized polarity reversal that sent a jolt of static electricity snapping against Bianco's skin.

He yelped, his eyes flying open.

Bodhi was leaning in, mere inches away, that terrible, shark-like grin stretching his face to the breaking point. The gold eyes were spinning so fast they were a solid disk of light.

"Sike," Bodhi whispered.

The word hung in the air, heavy and wrong.

Then, the world snapped back. The static vanished. The choir of voices cut out, leaving only one: Bodhi’s familiar, mocking tenor.

Bodhi pulled back, executing a pirouette that looked like a video game character glitched into a loop, spinning three times before landing in a perfect crouch.

"I'm just f***in' with ya, B!" Bodhi crowed, pointing a black-clawed finger at Bianco. "Look at your face! You look like a constipated gargoyle who just realized he’s made of stone!"

The laugh that followed was a wet, barking sound. It wasn't the laugh of a sane man. It was the laugh of someone whose sanity had snapped so cleanly it had left no jagged edges to snag on reality.

Bianco stared at him, his mind unable to bridge the gap. The cognitive dissonance was physically painful. One moment, he was facing an eldritch horror, a god of entropy. The next, his cousin was making a 'your mom' joke in the middle of a blast crater. The whiplash made him want to vomit.

"What... what are you?" Bianco whispered, his voice trembling.

Bodhi stopped laughing. He stood up, throwing his arms wide. Black lightning arced from his fingertips, scorching the mud, writing equations in the ash that faded before Bianco could read them.

"Survival!" Bodhi shouted to the storm. "We are the Update! We are the Patch! The regulators are coming, cousin! The devs are trying to roll back the server!"

Bianco heard it then. Beneath the rain, beneath the ringing in his ears.

The low, rhythmic thrumming in the distance. Engines. Heavy, military-grade turbines displacing air.

"NAMF," Bianco realized, the name tasting like cold iron. "The drones."

"The Fun Police!" Bodhi corrected, grabbing Bianco's arm. His grip was like iron pliers, unyielding and hot. "Time to go! Chop chop! I want to see if I can outrun a missile! I bet I can. I bet I can outrun the concept of 'impact'!"

He hauled Bianco to his feet as if he weighed nothing. Bianco stumbled, his legs jelly, his equilibrium shattered.

"Bodhi, we can't," Bianco sobbed, the hopelessness crashing down on him. "There's nowhere to go. They'll kill us. Look at this... look at what you did. There’s nothing left."

Bodhi turned. The gold eyes locked onto him, and for a second, the mania vanished. The laughter died. In its place was a cold, terrifying clarity. The clarity of a machine that had calculated the odds, run the simulation a billion times, and decided that the outcome was irrelevant because the game itself was rigged.

"They can try," Bodhi said softly. "But I think we're going to find that their permissions... have been revoked."

He turned and dragged Bianco toward the smoke of the shipping yards. Toward the killing fields.

And Bianco followed, because the monster in front of him was the only thing scarier than the monsters behind.

I look around. The yard is full of shipping containers. Iron. Steel. Carbon. Mountains of mass.

Matter.

My physics textbook from the Academy flashes in my mind. Chapter 4: Equivalent Exchange. Professor Aris explaining the relationship between matter and energy.

E = mc².

Energy equals Mass times the Speed of Light squared.

It’s the most famous equation in history, but nobody really gets it. They think it's about bombs. It’s not about bombs. It’s about Identity.

It means that Mass is just Energy that has slowed down. It means that Energy is just Mass that has decided to go for a run.

Which means... they are the same thing.

If I can just... flip the bit.

I stare at the nearest container. It’s a standard twenty-foot TEU, painted a flaking russet red. Iron oxide. Fe2O3.

I focus my Eye. I don't look at the box. I look into it.

Zoom: x1000. I see the crystal lattice of the steel.
Zoom: x1,000,000. I see the molecular bonds, the electromagnetic forces holding the atoms together.
Zoom: x1,000,000,000.

I see the atoms.

They look like solar systems. A dense nucleus of protons and neutrons, orbited by a cloud of probability where the electrons live.

I find a single atom of Iron-56. Fate. Randomness. It doesn't matter. It’s the one.

Isolate.

The universe falls away. The rain stops. The drones stop. The only thing that exists is me and this atom.

I focus everything I have left—every scrap of mana, every shredded neuron, every drop of willpower—on the sub-atomic particles inside that nucleus.

I don't want to destroy it. I want to invert it.

I see the Protons (Positive). I see the Electrons (Negative).

I assert Admin Privilege.

Command: Invert Polarity.
Target: 1 milligram of Iron.
Action: Spin Reversal.

My brain screams. It feels like I’m trying to lift a mountain with my eyelids. The laws of physics are heavy. They don't want to bend. They have been written this way for fourteen billion years.

But I am not a physicist. I am a Cleric. I am a Heretic. I am the Glitch.

I force the spin to flip.

Property: Matter --> Antimatter.

For a nanosecond, I hold a tiny speck of anti-iron in the middle of a universe of matter.

"This is how the World begins anew, B," I whisper. "Not with a whimper. But with a bang."

I snap my fingers.

"Divinity of Entropy: Song of the Tyrant Sun"

For a fraction of a second, the universe flinches. The light is sucked into the reaction point. Sound is deleted. The rain hovering in the air evaporates into elementary particles.

Then, the annihilation event occurs.

The world doesn't turn white.

It turns Null.

Matter meets Antimatter. They cancel each other out. And in their death, they release 100% of their mass energy.

E = mc².

It’s not an explosion. It’s a correction.

The shockwave moves faster than sound. It moves faster than thought.

I see the shipping container—thirty tons of steel—simply cease to be solid. It unspools. The atoms lose their grip on each other. The binding energy that held them together is released in a catastrophic, joyful shout.

Then the heat hits.

It’s not a wave. It’s a wall. A solid, physical wall of pure thermal expansion.

I have 0.0001 seconds to react.

Execute: Cleric_Shield_v9.0
Error: Insufficient Mana.

Execute: Recusive Regen Loop: Divert_BioMass_to_Epidermis.
Warning: This will result in 90% dermal liquefaction and massive internal trauma.

I grin as my face begins to melt.

Do it.

The blast wave slams into us. I tackle Bianco, driving him into the mud behind the engine block of a rusted forklift. It’s tiny cover, pathetic cover, but it’s mass.

I wrap my body around his, creating a cocoon of flesh and bone. I become the shield.

I feel my back vaporize.

It’s not pain. It’s too fast for pain. It’s just... loss. I feel my skin strip away in layers. The epidermis goes first, turning to carbon dust. Then the dermis. Then the muscle.

I feel my ribs turn to charcoal. I feel the water in my blood flash-boil into steam, inflating my veins until they burst.

The shockwave hits my spine and I feel the vertebrae crack, grinding together like mortar and pestle.

And the noise.
It wasn't a boom. It wasn't a roar.

It was the sound of a god inhaling. It was the sound of reality screaming as a hole was punched through its chest. A high, thin, impossible shriek that shattered glass ten miles away.

Scene 7

Bianco scrambles into the pilot’s seat. His hands are shaking so bad he can barely find the ignition sequence. He keeps missing the biometric reader, his thumb sliding off the glass panel in a smear of oil and sweat.

"Start it up, B!" I yell from the co-pilot’s chair, spinning around in it like a child in a candy store. "Spin the wheel! Pull the lever! Do the thing! The popsicle stand is blow, the candle is lit! LET'SA F***ING GOOOOOOOOOOOOO-"

"I’m TRYING, jackass!" he screams, jamming his thumb onto the reader with enough force to crack the screen. "It's cold! The reactor is cold!"

The engine coughs. It wheezes. It sounds like a smoker with bronchitis trying to run a marathon. The dampeners whine, protesting the sudden demand for power. But then—catch. A roar of combustion. Black smoke billows out the back, instantly swallowed by the storm.

"Yes!" I cheer, punching the dashboard. "She lives! She breathes! She pollutes!"

We lurch into the air just as the yard’s main alarm goes off. A siren wails—high-pitched, frantic, a mechanical scream that cuts through the silence I just created. Spotlights sweep the fog, hunting for the source of the devastation.

"We have company!" Bianco yells, wrestling the yoke as the skimmer fights the crosswinds. "Sensors are lighting up!"

I look at the rear monitor. The visuals are grainy, distorted by the radiation interference, but unnecessary. I can feel them.

Three NAMF interceptors are rising from the garrison loop. Fast. Sleek. They look like sharks made of matte-black hate. Their engines burn with pure blue mana-fire.

"They're locking on!" Bianco cries, his voice jumping an octave. "I can see the radar paint! They have tone!"

I lean back, putting my feet up on the dashboard, crossing my ankles. "Relax. I got this."

"You got this? You're half-dead! You don't have enough mana to light a cigarette!"

"True," I admit. "But I don't need mana. I just need... access."

I close my eyes.

I don't visualize the sky. I don't visualize the interceptors. I visualize the network.

Everything is connected. The radar waves bouncing off our hull are just data packets. The targeting computers in the interceptors are just logic gates processing variables.

Variable: Target_Acquired = True.
Variable: Target_Location = [Latitude, Longitude, Altitude].

I reach out. My mind feels slippery, frictionless. I slide past the encryption keys. I ignore the firewalls. They are looking for magic intrusion; they aren't looking for a syntax error.

I find the data stream connecting the radar dish to the cockpit display.

Edit Property: Opacity.
Edit Property: Radar_Cross_Section.

Set Value: NULL.

I push the command out. It drains me—a sudden, sharp headache that feels like an icepick to the frontal lobe—but the result is instantaneous.

On the monitor, the interceptors veer off. They bank hard left, breaking formation. They’re confused. Their sensors are telling them that the target they were just locking onto has ceased to exist. To them, we are no longer a physical object. We are a ghost. A glitch in the skybox.

"Where did they go?" Bianco asks, banking the skimmer hard to the right, following the coastline. "Why did they turn?"

"They didn't go anywhere," I grin, opening one eye. The gold iris spins lazily, tracking the interceptors as they vanish into the clouds. "We just... adjusted our render distance. To them, we're just a skipped frame."

I laugh, a high, thin sound that barely covers the ringing in my ears. I’m pushing the hardware too hard. I know it. My brain feels like it’s vibrating in a bath of acid. But God, the speed. The absolute control.

For the first time in my life, the world doesn't feel like a cage. It feels like a terminal. And I have the keyboard.

Scene 8

We’re over the Atlantic now. The ocean is a black void beneath us, endless and indifferent. The storm has broken, leaving behind a sky full of cold, distant stars that look down on us with zero empathy.

Bianco has leveled us out at thirty thousand feet, but he’s white-knuckling the yoke like he’s trying to strangle it. The skimmer groans in the jet stream, trembling like a frightened animal.

"This is crazy," he mutters, his eyes fixed on the horizon. "This is absolutely crazy. We just... we just erased a shipping yard. We erased a Sentry Squad."

"It’s aerodynamic!" I correct him. "Usually."

I’m sitting on the floor of the cargo hold now, cross-legged. I’m vibrating. My leg is bouncing up and down at a frequency that is probably annoying, but I can't stop. The energy is trapped under my skin. The Ouroboros on my shoulder is humming, a quiet background process keeping my arm attached to my torso.

"Bodhi," Bianco says, his voice tight. He doesn't look back. "Are you... okay?"

"Okay?" I look at him. "Define okay. Am I functioning? Yes. Am I currently metabolizing adrenaline at a rate that would kill a horse? Also yes. Is my soul technically a piece of malware? Debateable."

"You... you're smiling," he says. The accusation hangs in the air. "We just lost everything. Your father is... gone. The estate is gone. Everything we knew is ash. And you're smiling."

I stop bouncing. The silence stretches, thin and taut, filled only by the drone of the engines.

"I'm not smiling because I'm happy, B," I say, my voice dropping that octave again. The mania bleeds away, leaving something colder, harder. "I'm smiling because if I stop, I have to acknowledge the fact that I'm currently running on a system that has had its emotional core excised with a rusty spoon."

I tap my chest, right over the spot where my heart is beating a frantic, arrhythmic tattoo against my healed ribs.

"I deleted the panic, Bianco. I looked at the fear—the paralyzing, screaming, animal terror of what we just did—and I dragged it to the Recycle Bin. Because if I feel it? If I actually let myself feel the statistical probability of our survival? If I let myself feel the weight of Papa's death?"

I lean forward, the grin returning, sharp and brittle as broken glass.

"I’d crash this ship into the water just to make the noise stop."

Bianco stares at me in the reflection of the cockpit glass. He looks horrified. Good. Horror is a valid output. It means he’s processing the data correctly. He understands the stakes.

"So," I clap my hands, the sound echoing in the empty hold. "Let’s focus on the mission! Rome! Pizza! Sanctuary! I am the CPU, you are the Driver. Keep eyes on the road, and I’ll keep reality from collapsing around us. Deal?"

Bianco swallows. He nods slowly. "Deal."

"Excellent!" I flop back onto the deck plating, staring up at the rivets in the ceiling. "Now, wake me up when we hit the Eurozone. I need to defrag my hard drive. I think I have a memory leak in my amygdala."

Scene 9

I don't sleep. Sleep is for people who need to knit their subconscious back together. Sleep is for people who have dreams. I don't have dreams anymore; I have simulations.

I just stare at the ceiling rivets. I count them. 1,402 rivets. Rivets 45 through 92 show signs of oxidative stress.

I look at the ring on my finger.

Papa’s ring. Obsidian. Cold. It absorbs the ambient light of the cockpit, a tiny black hole on my hand.

I spin it with my thumb. Over and over. A fidget loop.

I think about the Source Code.

The souls—the 1000 nodes I touched in the Shard—they weren't just data. They were files. Complex, encrypted, beautiful files. And if a file can be deleted... it can be restored.

I don't need a resurrection spell. Resurrection is messy. It’s necromancy. It’s guessing. It's trying to paint a portrait from memory.

I need a System Restore.

I need to find the backup.

I hold the ring up to the faint light of the dashboard. The gold in my eyes reflects off the black stone, creating a tiny, inverted universe in the gem's surface.

"I’m going to find the server," I whisper to the ring. "I’m going to find the admin console for this whole stupid, broken reality. And when I do..."

I clench my fist, feeling the metal bite into my skin.

"...I’m going to fix the bug."

It’s not a prayer. Prayers are requests. This is a notification. A patch note.

I close my eyes and let the hum of the engine vibrate through my bones. I am a weapon now. I am a tool. And tools have a purpose.

Object: World.
Status: Broken.
Action: Repair.

Scene 10

Dawn breaks over the Mediterranean. The light is sickly, filtered through the smog of the Industrial Zones, turning the clouds a bruised purple.

But then—

Rome.

It hangs in the sky like a chandelier made of gothic stone and neon.

The Holy Roman Empire. The Eternal City. Floating a kilometer above the earth, tethered by crackling ley-lines that hum with raw mana. It is a jewel of impossible architecture, spires of white marble piercing the smog layer, bathed in perpetual, artificial sunlight.

I stand up and press my face to the cockpit glass, leaving a greasy smear.

"Whoa," Bianco breathes. "The Golden Cage."

I look at it. My Ajna flares, overlaying the view with data.

I don't see the beauty. I see the cost.

I see the flow of power—thick, pulsing arteries of mana being sucked up from the earth below. I see the structural stress points on the suspension cables. I see the defensive wards shimmering like heat haze, a complex lattice of 'Keep Out' signals.

And I see the shadow it casts. The miles of slums underneath, living in the permanent eclipse of the city above.

It’s massive. It’s arrogant. It’s a city built on the backs of the 25 million souls they harvested to fuel this "Paradise." It is a monument to efficient predation.

But I don't feel awe. I feel... hunger.

"It’s not a cage, B," I say, and my voice is steady. The mania has cooled into a cold, hard focus. "It’s a target."

I trace the outline of the Vatican on the glass. The Great Dome. The seat of the High Council.

"That’s where the server is," I say. "The Vault. The Philosopher's Stone. The Root Directory."

I look at Bianco. The fear is still there in his eyes, but there’s something else now. Hope? Or just the desperate realization that he’s strapped into a rollercoaster that has no brakes.

When I take over the world, this will be the first place I come back to.

"Take us in," I command.

Scene 11

We descend toward the Ludus Magnus boundary. The traffic is thick—luxury skimmers, cargo haulers, military patrols. We slip into the stream, just another piece of debris in the river of commerce.

I watch the advertisements flashing on the holographic billboards that line the approach vector.

MANA-MAX SUPPLEMENTS! Guaranteed awakening or your credits back!
INDULGENCE CREDITS: Buy Now, Sin Later! Pre-approved forgiveness for Tier 1 Citizens!
Sentry Drones: Keeping You Safe. Keeping You Pure.

It’s a system. A marketplace. A corrupt, bloated operating system that thinks it’s invincible because no one has ever tried to access the kernel.

I flex my fingers. Black sparks dance between my knuckles. The Ouroboros tattoo on my shoulder burns with a cold, comforting heat.

"They think they’re safe," I murmur. "They think they wrote the code."

I turn to Bianco. The gold in my eyes is pulsing a steady, rhythmic beat. A countdown.

"When we land, we don't just survive," I say. "We log in. We learn their syntax. We find their exploits."

I grin. It’s not the manic, terrifying grin of the crater. It’s the sharp, predatory smile of a hacker who just found the password taped to the monitor.

"And then... we crash the server."
